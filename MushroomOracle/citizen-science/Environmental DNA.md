# Environmental DNA and Metabarcoding

Environmental DNA (eDNA) metabarcoding extracts DNA directly from environmental samples — soil, water, wood, or air — rather than requiring visible fruiting bodies. This approach has revealed that traditional fruiting body surveys dramatically underestimate true fungal diversity, typically detecting only a fraction of the species present in a given habitat.

---

## How Fungal eDNA Surveys Work

1. **Standardized field sampling** of substrate (soil cores, water filters, wood samples, air filters)
2. **DNA extraction** from the environmental sample
3. **PCR amplification** of the ITS region (or ITS2 sub-region) using universal fungal primers (see [[DNA Barcoding]])
4. **High-throughput sequencing** — Illumina MiSeq is the current standard platform
5. **Bioinformatic processing** — sequences clustered into Operational Taxonomic Units (OTUs) or Amplicon Sequence Variants (ASVs), then compared against curated reference databases like UNITE (see [[Fungal Databases]])

---

## eDNA vs. Traditional Surveys: Key Studies

### "Man Against Machine" (Krah et al., 2019, *Biological Conservation*)

Conducted in the Bavarian Forest (Germany) across 144 sample plots on an altitudinal gradient:
- Metabarcoding detected **significantly more species** than traditional fruit body inventory
- Fruit body inventory was dominated by Basidiomycota; metabarcoding showed approximately **balanced Basidiomycota/Ascomycota** distribution
- Species forming inconspicuous or **hypogeous (underground) fruit bodies** were detected **only by metabarcoding**

### Deadwood Fungi Study (2024, *Biological Conservation*)

Of all threatened species detected:
- 11% found by **both methods**
- 70% exclusively by **metabarcoding**
- 18% exclusively by **fruit body surveys**

### Dacrymycetes Study (2020, *Scientific Reports*)

eDNA detected 27 OTUs; fruiting-body and culture surveys uncovered only approximately **50% of the OTUs** found by eDNA.

### Conservation Implication

The consensus is that metabarcoding and fruit body surveys should be **combined** — each detects a unique set of species. Neither method alone captures the full picture.

## Air Sampling for Fungal Spores

Aerial eDNA is an emerging frontier:

- Airborne eDNA metabarcoding detects approximately **three times more genera** than traditional microscopic analysis of pollen and spores
- A 2024 study (*Journal of Applied Ecology*) demonstrated that aerial eDNA contributes vital information for fungal biodiversity assessment
- A 2025 study (*Nature Communications*) showed airborne eDNA can capture **three decades of ecosystem biodiversity trends**

The atmosphere is a largely unexplored reservoir of fungal genetic material — spores are continuously dispersed and can be sampled with standard air filtration equipment.
