# Citizen Science Platforms

Community mycology has been transformed by digital platforms that aggregate observations, connect amateurs with experts, and feed data into global biodiversity databases. Each platform serves a different niche, and their reliability varies significantly for fungi.

---

## iNaturalist

The dominant general-purpose biodiversity platform: 345,000+ species, ~85 million observations.

**Computer vision accuracy for fungi**: iNaturalist estimates Research Grade observations are **95% correct overall**, but accuracy varies dramatically for fungi. A 2023 field study comparing iNaturalist lichen records with expert determinations found **fewer than half of species logged by users matched specialist IDs**, and roughly **70% of species-level identifications** that appeared only on iNaturalist were wrong. The CV model performs best for conspicuous, frequently-photographed macrofungi and poorly for look-alikes, corticioid fungi, and small ascomycetes.

**GBIF integration**: Research Grade observations (verified by three independent users) are automatically uploaded to GBIF, making iNaturalist one of the largest data providers to the global biodiversity network.

**Fundamental limitation**: Many fungi cannot be identified to species from photographs alone — they require microscopy, chemical reagents, or DNA sequencing (see [[DNA Barcoding]], [[Sequencing Accessibility]]).

---

## Mushroom Observer

A specialized mycology-focused platform, purpose-built for mushroom identification:

- Structured fields for microscopic features, chemical reactions, habitat data, and substrate information
- Strong community of experienced identifiers and professional mycologists
- Used extensively by local mycological societies to post observations
- Data has contributed to formal taxonomic publications

---

## FunDiS (Fungal Diversity Survey)

A nonprofit citizen science organization that emerged from a 2012 Mycological Society of America meeting at Yale. Originally the North American Mycoflora Project (NAMP, co-founded 2017), rebranded 2020.

**Mission**: Document the diversity and distribution of fungi across North America, creating a comprehensive "funga" supplemented by maps, images, voucher specimens, and sequences for every species.

**Rare Fungi Challenges**: Four structured programs targeting 20 rare, threatened, or under-documented species, investigating distribution, fruiting periods, habitats, and temporal changes.

**Data quality**: FunDiS trains participants to provide higher-quality documentation than general platforms and ensures observations get expert identification.

---

## MycoMap

A DNA-validated macrofungi observation platform with geospatial, temporal, and taxonomic analytics:

- ~5,000 specimens documented and sequenced, with results posted on MycoMap, MyCoPortal, and GenBank
- Expanded free sequencing program for 2025 covering most of the Rocky Mountains, Florida, Alabama, and the Caribbean
- Integrates iNaturalist project records with DNA sequence data and herbarium records

---

## BioBlitz Events

**Great North American FungiQuest**: Over **30,000 volunteers** collected nearly **150,000 fungi sightings** identifying almost **4,400 species** — the largest-ever fungi bioblitz.

**MycoBlitz events** are BioBlitz-format events focused exclusively on fungi, organized by local societies, FunDiS, and other organizations in partnership with parks and protected areas.

---

## The "Dark Taxa" Problem

"Dark taxa" (or "dark matter fungi") are organisms known only from eDNA sequences with no corresponding physical specimen, formal description, or scientific name. Over **90% of estimated fungal species** are undescribed, and environmental sequences far outpace the ability to formally name and describe taxa.

GBIF has begun adding sequence-based identifiers to its backbone taxonomy to accommodate dark taxa, making them visible in biodiversity databases. Citizen science initiatives engaging the public to provide fungal samples are considered crucial for accelerating the discovery pipeline.

## Local Mycological Societies

The **North American Mycological Association (NAMA)** is a 501(c)(3) nonprofit with over **90 affiliated mycological societies** in the US, Canada, and Mexico. These societies organize field walks, workshops, and annual forays, and are the backbone of community mycology. Notable societies include the Bay Area Mycological Society, New York Mycological Society, and many regional groups that contribute to conservation and biodiversity programs.

**NAMA's DNAMA committee** (DNA Sequencing Committee) conducted its first mass barcoding event at the 2024 PNW NAMA Foray, generating **803 ITS barcodes** (83.7% success rate) for **513 unique taxa** in 209 genera using Oxford Nanopore technology (see [[Sequencing Accessibility]]).
