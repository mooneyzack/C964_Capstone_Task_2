# Sterile Technique

## Overview

Sterile technique is the set of practices and equipment used to prevent microbial contamination during mushroom cultivation lab work. The goal is to create and maintain a clean workspace where inoculations, transfers, and culture work can be performed without introducing competing organisms. All lab operations — from pouring agar to inoculating grain — require some form of sterile environment, either a laminar flow hood (LFH) or a still air box (SAB).

## Details

### Laminar Flow Hoods (LFH)

A laminar flow hood creates a sterile workspace by drawing ambient air through a HEPA (High-Efficiency Particulate Air) filter rated to capture 99.97% of particles ≥0.3 microns, then projecting a uniform horizontal or vertical curtain of ultra-clean air across the work surface. This positive pressure prevents ambient airborne contaminants from entering the work zone.

**Types:**
- Horizontal flow (blowing toward you): Most common for mycology; creates an unobstructed clean zone in front of the filter
- Vertical flow (blowing downward): Better for hazardous work (used in biosafety cabinets); less common in home mycology

**Construction/Setup:** Minimum fan/filter specs for home builds: 200–400 CFM blower + 24"x24" HEPA filter. Operating the hood: run for 15 minutes before working to flush ambient air. Wipe the work surface with 70% isopropyl alcohol. Never work across the clean air path — keep materials downwind of the filter.

**Best for:** Pouring agar plates, making spore syringes, transferring cultures, inoculating grain jars/bags, preparing liquid culture.

---

### Still Air Box (SAB)

A large, clear plastic storage tote with two arm-sized holes cut near the base serves as a low-cost alternative to an LFH. Construction time: ~15 minutes, cost: ~$15. The principle relies on air stratification — undisturbed still air inside the box is free of turbulent air currents that carry spores. Work slowly and deliberately; rapid movements create turbulence and defeat the purpose.

**Setup:** Spray inside surfaces with 70% isopropyl, allow to settle 5–10 minutes before beginning work. Tools, jars, and materials should be pre-loaded and wiped down before hands enter. SABs are effective for grain jar inoculation and agar transfers but less reliable than an LFH for high-risk work.

### Agar Work

**Why agar?** Agar-based media provides a solid, visible platform to observe mycelial growth patterns, identify contamination early, isolate genetics, maintain cultures long-term, and produce clean spawn without the risk associated with spore syringes.

**Agar Media Recipes:**

**Malt Extract Agar (MEA):**
- 20g light malt extract (LME)
- 20g agar-agar powder
- 1 liter distilled water
- Optional: 2g yeast extract for added nutrition

Dissolve ingredients in water, heat to boiling while stirring, sterilize in jars/flasks at 15 PSI for 20–25 minutes. Do not exceed 30 minutes — caramelization occurs.

**Potato Dextrose Agar (PDA):**
- Boil 200g peeled potatoes in 1 liter water for 30 minutes
- Strain, retain liquid
- Add 20g dextrose + 15–20g agar powder
- Bring back to 1 liter with distilled water
- Sterilize 15 PSI / 20 minutes

PDA is rich and supports fast growth; slightly more contamination-prone than MEA.

**Dog Food Agar (DFA):**
- 5–10 pieces plain dry dog food (avoid flavored, no garlic/onion)
- 20g agar
- 1 liter water
- Boil dog food in water 30 minutes, strain and proceed as above

DFA is nutritionally rich and economical. Popular in community mycology.

**Oatmeal Agar:**
- Boil 40g rolled oats in 1 liter water for 1 hour
- Strain, add 20g agar
- Sterilize as above

A milder medium; good for slower-growing species.

**Pouring Plates:**
Allow sterilized agar to cool to 115–125°F (46–52°C) before pouring — if too hot, condensation drowns plates; if already gelled, reheating risks contamination. Pour 15–25ml per 90mm Petri dish in a single smooth pour. Flame-sterilize a scalpel handle and use it to waft the dish partially closed during pouring in an LFH. Stack plates face-down (agar side up) once solidified (20–30 minutes) to prevent condensation dripping onto the agar surface. Store refrigerated (35–40°F) for up to 4 weeks. Optimal use within 2 weeks.

**Culture Transfers:**
Heat a scalpel to glow-red with an alcohol lamp or butane lighter, allow to cool 3–5 seconds, then cut a small wedge of actively growing mycelium from the leading edge of a healthy culture. Transfer to the center of a fresh plate, close lid. Incubate at 75–80°F. Observe for 3–7 days. The "sector" method: cut only specific hyphal sectors that show vigorous, clean growth to isolate the most productive genetics.

**Isolating Clean Cultures / Single-Spore Isolation:**
From a spore syringe: inject 0.5–1ml diluted spore solution onto a plate, spread to isolate individual spores. Single spores germinate as distinct colonies after 4–10 days. Each colony is a genetic individual (F1 hybrid). Perform serial transfers picking from the fastest, cleanest sectors. "Agar wedge to grain": cut a sector with clean mycelium and place it onto sterilized grain to start spawn. This is faster and more reliable than spore syringes for consistent results.

### Grain Spawn Preparation: Step by Step

**Grain types and tradeoffs:**
- Rye berries: tough husks, resist clumping, industry standard, slightly lower contamination rate
- Wheat berries: economical, widely available, high yield, slightly more prone to clumping
- Millet: fastest colonization (5–10 days), small size = maximum surface area, slightly fragile
- Oats (whole or wild bird seed): economical, good water retention, very popular for home cultivation
- Corn: slow colonization, large kernels, used in commercial applications

**Step 1 — Rinse:** Wash grain 3–5 times in cold water until runoff is nearly clear. Removes surface debris and contaminants.

**Step 2 — Soak:** Soak 12–24 hours at room temperature. Purpose: fully hydrate grain interior AND allow dormant Bacillus endospores to germinate into vegetative cells (which will be killed in subsequent sterilization). Endospores can survive sterilization; germinated cells cannot.

**Step 3 — Simmer:** Drain soaked grain, cover with fresh water. Bring to boil, simmer 10–20 minutes. Goal: fully hydrate grain to "al dente" texture — tender but not split or mushy. Broken grains provide excessive starch for contamination.

**Step 4 — Drain and Surface-Dry:** Strain grains through colander. Spread on clean surface or towels for 20–45 minutes to allow surface moisture to evaporate. Grains must be surface-dry but internally moist — surface water in jars promotes Bacillus.

**Step 5 — Load Containers:** Fill quart mason jars 2/3 full, or mushroom grow bags half-full. Do not overfill — leave headspace. Add 1/4 tsp gypsum per quart (prevents clumping). Cover jars with lids with polyfill/Tyvek filters; leave bags open with filter patches facing outward.

**Step 6 — Sterilize:** Pressure cook at 15 PSI. Quart jars: 90 minutes minimum. Half-pint jars: 60–75 minutes. 5 lb bags: 150–180 minutes. Start timing only when full pressure is reached. Let cool inside cooker naturally for 6–12 hours or overnight.

**Step 7 — Inoculate:** In LFH or SAB, wipe jar lids with 70% isopropyl. Inject 1–2ml liquid culture or inoculate with agar wedge per quart jar. Seal ports with micropore tape.

**Step 8 — Colonize:** Incubate at species-appropriate temperature in darkness. Shake/break up grains after 20–30% colonization to distribute mycelium. Colonization complete: 7–21 days depending on species and temperature.

### Liquid Culture (LC) Preparation and Use

**Purpose:** Liquid culture contains actively growing, living mycelium suspended in a nutrient solution. It colonizes grain 2–4x faster than spore syringes, produces more uniform results, and allows virtually unlimited propagation from a single verified clean culture.

**Common Recipes (all at ~4% sugar-to-water ratio):**
- **Light Malt Extract:** 20g LME + 2g yeast extract + 500ml distilled water
- **Honey:** 16g (4 tsp) honey + 400ml water
- **Karo Light Corn Syrup:** 16g + 400ml water
- **Combined LME + Karo:** 16g Karo + 4g LME + 500ml water

**Preparation:**
1. Mix ingredients in a mason jar or LC flask with magnetic stir bar or injection port lid
2. Sterilize at 15 PSI for 20–25 minutes (no longer — caramelization degrades sugars)
3. Cool completely before inoculating
4. In LFH or SAB, inject 1–2ml of clean agar culture or spore syringe through injection port
5. Incubate at 70–78°F with daily gentle swirling/shaking to distribute mycelium and oxygenate

**Timeline:** With living mycelium: visible growth in 2–7 days, fully colonized in 1–2 weeks. With spores: 5–15 days for initial formation, 7–21 more days for full colonization.

**Healthy appearance:** Clear or very slightly hazy liquid with distinct white clumps of mycelium. Clarity is the key indicator.

**Contamination signs:**
- Uniformly cloudy liquid where nothing is distinctly visible = bacterial contamination
- Green/black/pink coloration = mold contamination
- Foul odor = bacterial contamination
- No growth after 21 days at proper temperature = dead spores or sterile issue

**Storage:** Refrigerate (35–45°F) in sealed container for 6–12 months. Shake 2–4 times per month to prevent mycelium from settling and dying. Allow to warm to room temperature before use.

**Using LC to inoculate grain:** Shake thoroughly, load into sterile syringe, inject 1–2ml per quart jar of grain. Mix and distribute. 1ml = sufficient for quart jars; 5–10ml for 5 lb bags.
