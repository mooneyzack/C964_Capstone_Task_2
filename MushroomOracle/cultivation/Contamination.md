# Contamination

## Overview

Contamination is the introduction and establishment of unwanted organisms — molds, bacteria, or competing fungi — in a mushroom substrate or culture. It is the primary cause of failed grows at every scale. Understanding how to identify contaminants, understand their biology, and prevent their entry is fundamental to cultivation success. See also [[Troubleshooting]] for fruiting-stage problems; this note focuses on contaminants that affect colonization and spawn production.

## Details

### Contamination Identification

**Trichoderma spp. (Green Mold)**
The most feared and common contaminant. Appears first as white, fluffy mycelium nearly indistinguishable from mushroom mycelium, then rapidly transitions to bright emerald-green or blue-green powdery spore production within 24–72 hours of initial appearance. Produces enzymes that degrade fungal cell walls — it directly parasitizes mushroom mycelium. Spreads through airborne spores present in all soils and organic matter. Once green appears, the substrate is typically unrecoverable. Remove from growing area immediately in a sealed bag to prevent spore dispersal.

**Cobweb Mold (Hypomyces rosellus / Cladobotryum spp.)**
Gray-white, wispy, three-dimensional growth that appears to hover above the substrate surface in tufts. Distinct damp, musty odor. Unlike mushroom mycelium which grows in the substrate, cobweb mold grows above it. Thrives in low airflow, high humidity, high CO2 environments. Spreads rapidly — can colonize a surface in 24–48 hours. Treatment: spray directly with 3% hydrogen peroxide solution; cobweb mold retreats while mushroom mycelium is unharmed.

**Bacillus spp. (Sour Rot / Wet Spot / Bacterial Blotch)**
Appears as dull gray, slimy wet patches with a distinctive sour or fermented odor, typically concentrated at the bottom of jars or bulk containers. Caused by endospore-forming bacteria that survive typical sterilization if not properly pre-treated. Prevention: soak grain 12–24 hours before sterilization to trigger endospore germination, making them vulnerable to heat. Once present: typically fatal to the batch. Do not attempt to salvage.

**Black Bread Mold (Rhizopus stolonifer)**
Begins as white wispy mycelium, progresses to gray, then develops characteristic tiny black pinhead sporangia. Spreads through airborne spores, often introduced by fungus gnats and fruit flies which are vectors. Inhalation of spores poses a health risk. Dispose of contaminated substrate outside the growing area.

**Orange/Pink Bread Mold (Neurospora crassa — "Lipstick Mold")**
Brilliant neon orange or pinkish-orange powdery growth. Extremely aggressive — can colonize an entire jar in 8–12 hours once sporulation begins. One of the fastest-spreading contaminants in mycology. Often introduced through poorly sterilized grain or contaminated agar. The orange color is unmistakable. Immediately isolate in sealed bags and remove from the grow space. Do not open contaminated containers near other substrates.

**Other molds:**
- Penicillium: Blue-green but more muted than Trichoderma; powdery, often in patches. Common post-harvest contaminant.
- Aspergillus: Ranges from black to yellow-green; powdery. Can be hazardous.
- Pink Oyster contamination: Pink/red patches near harvest that resemble the mushroom but are bacterial rot.

### Contamination Prevention Strategies

1. Always work in an LFH or SAB; never in ambient room air
2. Wipe all surfaces, tools, and container exteriors with 70% isopropyl alcohol before each operation
3. Flame-sterilize metal tools to glowing and allow 5–10 seconds to cool before contact
4. Pre-soak grains 12–24 hours before sterilization to eliminate Bacillus risk
5. Allow sterilized substrates to cool completely (room temperature) before inoculating — hot substrates create steam and increase contamination vectors
6. Wear gloves, change gloves between operations
7. Inoculate at low-traffic times (early morning) when airborne particulates are lowest
8. Never talk, sneeze, or cough over open cultures
9. Maintain a clean, organized lab — clutter harbors spores
10. Source clean genetics: use agar cultures rather than spore syringes where possible
