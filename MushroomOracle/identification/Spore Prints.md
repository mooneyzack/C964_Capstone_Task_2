# Spore Prints

## Overview

A spore print is the accumulation of spores deposited from a mushroom cap onto a surface. It is one of the most reliable and low-cost diagnostic characters in field and amateur mycology, revealing the mass spore color — a character that cannot be assessed from the spore color of a single spore and that remains consistent across a species regardless of individual variation in cap color or other surface features.

See also: [[Spore Biology]] for the biology of spore production, [[Morphological Identification]] for the full suite of identification characters.

## Technique

### Materials

- A mature cap with gills, pores, or teeth fully exposed (remove the stipe at the base of the cap)
- A piece of paper with both a white half and a dark half (folio a sheet to create both backgrounds, or use card stock)
- A bowl or glass to cover the cap and reduce air movement
- 4–12 hours of waiting time

### Procedure

1. Select a mature cap — one that has opened fully and begun releasing spores (the gills should be visible and the cap should be at or past the flattened stage). Immature buttons will not yield a readable print.
2. Place the cap gill-side (or pore-side) down on the paper, centered over the transition between the white and dark halves.
3. Cover the cap with a bowl or inverted glass to protect against air currents and reduce desiccation.
4. Leave undisturbed for **4–12 hours** (overnight is ideal for faint-printing species; 4 hours may suffice for prolific printers).
5. Carefully lift the cap straight up to avoid smearing.
6. The deposit of spores on the paper is the spore print.

A thin film of hairspray or spray fixative can preserve the print on paper for archiving or teaching collections.

## Interpreting Colors

Spore print color must be read against both backgrounds. Some colors are only distinguishable against the correct background:

- **White or cream** spores are only visible against the dark half of the paper.
- **Dark brown or black** spores show best against white.
- **Pink, green, or olive** are distinguishable against either background but differ in appearance.

Observe in good light; colors may appear slightly darker or lighter wet vs. dry. Allow a few minutes for the print to dry before final color assessment.

## Spore Color by Family and Genus

The following table provides the primary diagnostic reference. Spore print color alone does not identify a mushroom but is a critical data point in combination with other characters (cap color, gill attachment, veil presence, habitat).

| Spore Print Color | Associated Families / Genera | Example Species |
|---|---|---|
| **White to cream** | Amanitaceae (*Amanita*), Pleurotaceae (*Pleurotus*), Marasmiaceae, Hydnangiaceae (*Laccaria*) | Death cap, destroying angel, oyster mushroom |
| **Pink to salmon** | Entolomataceae (*Entoloma*) | Entoloma sinuatum (toxic), E. hochstetteri |
| **Brown (clay / cinnamon)** | Bolbitiaceae (*Conocybe*), some *Cortinarius* | Conocybe filaris (deadly), some webcaps |
| **Rusty-brown to ochre** | Cortinariaceae (*Cortinarius*, *Hebeloma*) | Deadly webcap, poison pie |
| **Purple-brown to chocolate** | Strophariaceae (*Psilocybe*, *Stropharia*, *Hypholoma*) | Psilocybe cubensis, Stropharia rugosoannulata |
| **Dark brown to black** | Agaricaceae (*Agaricus*), Psathyrellaceae (*Coprinellus*) | Common button mushroom, ink caps |
| **Green** | Agaricaceae (*Chlorophyllum molybdites*) | Green-spored parasol (toxic; the single most frequent cause of mushroom poisoning in North America) |
| **Olive-brown** | Boletaceae (*Boletus* and allies) | King bolete, devil's bolete |

### Color-Based Safety Shortcuts

- A **white spore print** on a white-gilled mushroom growing from a volva should immediately trigger concern for *Amanita* species, including the death cap and destroying angel. A white print never rules out deadly species.
- A **green spore print** is diagnostic for [[Chlorophyllum molybdites]] — this character alone distinguishes it from the edible parasol mushrooms it resembles. No other common species produces a green print.
- A **pink spore print** shifts suspicion toward *Entoloma* and away from white-spored species; it is a key differentiator when the cap and gills appear similar to chanterelles or other edibles.
- A **chocolate brown** print in a white-gilled, button-stage mushroom is reassuring evidence of *Agaricus bisporus* and against *Amanita*.

## Limitations

Spore prints have real constraints that every forager and field mycologist must understand:

1. **Requires a mature cap.** Immature button-stage mushrooms may not have exposed gills and may yield insufficient deposits. Young *Amanita* buttons in the egg stage are impossible to print without destroying the specimen.

2. **Environmental interference.** Wind, low humidity, and high temperature all reduce spore deposition. Very dry or very wet conditions may distort apparent color.

3. **Requires time.** A print takes at least 4 hours to be reliable — not suitable for rapid field verification before harvest.

4. **Color variability within a species.** Print color can shift slightly across a gradient (e.g., pale lavender-brown vs. definite chocolate brown) based on specimen age, humidity, and geographic race. The table above reflects typical ranges, not absolute values.

5. **Color matching is subjective.** Terms like "rusty-brown," "cinnamon," and "clay" overlap in practice. Cross-reference with printed field guide color charts when possible.

6. **Not a standalone test.** Spore color must be combined with morphological characters — cap shape, gill attachment, veil remnants, stipe features, bruising reactions, habitat, and smell — to reach a reliable identification. See [[Morphological Identification]].

7. **Does not replace microscopy for definitive ID.** Professional mycological identification uses spore shape, ornamentation, and size under a microscope; these are not assessable from a print alone.

## Field Work-Around: Gill Scrape

When time or conditions prevent a full spore print, scraping a small quantity of gill tissue onto white paper and rubbing lightly can sometimes reveal the spore color if the mushroom is actively sporulating. This is far less reliable than a full print but can provide a quick directional indicator.
