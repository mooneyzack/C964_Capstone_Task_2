# Dangerous Look-Alikes

## Overview

Mushroom poisoning kills and hospitalizes people every year, predominantly because edible species were confused with toxic or deadly look-alikes. This note is a detailed, unambiguous guide to the most dangerous pairs and groups. Each entry covers the distinguishing features that separate safe from deadly, the symptoms of poisoning, and the geographic context.

**Core principle**: Never eat a wild mushroom you cannot positively identify using ALL distinguishing characters, not just one or two. When in doubt, throw it out.

See also: [[Foraging Safety]] for safe foraging rules, [[Morphological Identification]] for character vocabulary, [[Spore Prints]] for spore color methodology.

## 1. Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*) and Its Look-Alikes

### The Deadly Species

[[Amanita phalloides]] — **Death Cap**

- **Cap:** 5–15 cm; pale yellow-green, olive-green, or yellowish (occasionally near-white); smooth; often striate at margin with age
- **Gills:** Pure white; free from stipe; do not change color with age
- **Spore print:** White
- **Stipe:** White; 5–15 cm; with a **large, pendulous white ring (annulus)** and a **prominent white sac-like volva** at the base — the volva is buried; you must dig the entire base out of the soil to see it
- **Flesh:** White throughout; does not stain or discolor when cut
- **Habitat:** Woodland — especially oak, beech, and mixed deciduous-conifer forest; ectomycorrhizal; typically solitary or scattered
- **Range:** Native to Europe; **introduced to North America** (California, Pacific Northwest, East Coast) through imported tree saplings; now established and expanding; also Australia, New Zealand, South America, South Africa

**Toxicology:** Contains amatoxins (primarily α-amanitin) and phallotoxins. Amatoxins inhibit RNA polymerase II, halting protein synthesis in liver and kidney cells. A single cap (50g fresh weight) contains enough toxin to be lethal to an adult. **There is no antidote.** Treatment is supportive. Liver transplant may be required in severe cases. Mortality rate without treatment: ~10–30%; with modern critical care: ~5–10%.

**Symptom timeline:**
- 6–24 hours (up to 36 hours): **NO symptoms** — the latent phase; this delay is characteristic of amatoxin poisoning and means victims often consume multiple meals before noticing
- 24–72 hours: severe cholera-like gastrointestinal phase (violent vomiting, profuse watery diarrhea, abdominal cramping, dehydration)
- 72–96 hours: apparent recovery phase — the patient stabilizes; this is deceptive
- 4–8 days: fulminant hepatorenal failure; jaundice, coagulopathy, encephalopathy; possible death

### Look-Alike 1: Paddy Straw Mushroom (*Volvariella volvacea*)

**Why they are confused:** Both have a volva and a similar pale coloring. Southeast Asian immigrant communities in North America and Europe have been fatally poisoned after foraging straw mushrooms — a species they knew well in their home country — and picking death caps by mistake.

| Character | *A. phalloides* (DEADLY) | *Volvariella volvacea* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Gills | White; never change color | **Pink to salmon** when mature; start white |
| Spore print | **White** | **Pink to salmon** |
| Ring | **Present** (large, pendulous) | **Absent** |
| Volva | White, sac-like | Similar sac-like volva; gray to brown |
| Habitat | Woodland (ectomycorrhizal) | Straw/compost/rice paddies; tropical/subtropical |
| Range | Temperate (worldwide, introduced) | Tropical Asia; grown commercially |

**Key test:** Take a spore print. Pink = *Volvariella*; White = danger.

### Look-Alike 2: Green-Spored Russula (*Russula virescens* and allies)

**Why they are confused:** Some green-capped *Russula* species have a similar overall appearance. *R. virescens* (green brittlegill) is edible.

| Character | *A. phalloides* (DEADLY) | *Russula virescens* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Volva | **Present; buried in soil** | **Absent — no volva, no ring** |
| Ring | Present | Absent |
| Gills | Free; white | Attached; white |
| Flesh | Does not crumble | **Brittle, crumbles** (characteristic of all *Russula*) |
| Stipe | Tall, slender | Stout, brittle |

**Key test:** Dig up the base. A volva rules in *Amanita*; a brittle stipe rules in *Russula*.

### Look-Alike 3: Young Button Mushroom (*Agaricus bisporus*)

**Why they are confused:** In the egg stage, *Amanita phalloides* buttons are completely encased in the white universal veil and resemble both puffballs and large white *Agaricus* buttons.

| Character | *A. phalloides* egg stage (DEADLY) | *Agaricus bisporus* button (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Interior when sliced vertically | Reveals the developing cap in olive-green and white stipe inside the white egg | Solid, all-white flesh; no internal structure of cap-in-egg |
| Gills when exposed | Will be white throughout development | **Pink when young** — if you expose gills, they should be pink |
| Spore print (mature) | White | **Chocolate brown** |
| Volva | Present | Absent |

**Rule:** Always slice puffball-like buttons vertically before consuming. An intact puffball interior is pure white and homogeneous. An *Amanita* egg reveals the ghost of the developing fruiting body inside.

## 2. Destroying Angel (*Amanita bisporigera* / *A. virosa* / *A. verna*) and Its Look-Alikes

### The Deadly Species

The destroying angels are a complex of all-white *Amanita* species:
- [[Amanita bisporigera]] — Eastern North America
- *A. ocreata* — Western North America
- *A. virosa* — Europe
- *A. verna* — Europe (spring)

**Appearance:** Cap pure white; gills white; spore print white; ring white, fragile, often skirt-like; volva white, sac-like, buried in soil. No distinctive smell in most specimens. The most innocent-looking of all deadly mushrooms — completely white, clean, and unremarkable.

**Toxicology:** Identical to *A. phalloides* — amatoxins; same symptom timeline; same mortality profile. North America's most deadly mushroom, responsible for the majority of fatal mushroom poisonings on the continent.

### Look-Alike 1: Button Mushroom / Field Mushroom (*Agaricus campestris*, *A. bisporus*)

**Why they are confused:** The destroying angel can fruit in grass near tree lines, and its button stage resembles *Agaricus* in rough appearance.

| Character | *Amanita bisporigera* (DEADLY) | *Agaricus campestris* / *A. bisporus* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Gills | **Always white** — at ALL stages of development | **Pink when young**, turning brown at maturity |
| Spore print | **White** | **Brown to chocolate** |
| Volva | **Present** — sac at base (dig it up!) | **Absent** |
| Ring | Present; fragile, may disappear | Present; often more robust |
| Habitat | Near trees (ectomycorrhizal association) | Grassland, meadow, lawns (saprotrophic) |

**Safety rule:** In any white-gilled, button-stage mushroom found near trees, dig up the entire base and look for a sac-like volva. If it's there, do not eat it under any circumstances.

### Look-Alike 2: Meadow Mushroom (*Agaricus campestris*)

See the table above — same distinguishing features apply. Additionally: *A. campestris* bruises slightly yellow-orange at the stipe (not chrome-yellow as in the toxic *A. xanthodermus*), and has a mild pleasant smell. Destroying angel has no distinctive smell.

## 3. Deadly Galerina (*Galerina marginata*) and Its Look-Alikes

### The Deadly Species

[[Galerina marginata]] (formerly *G. autumnalis*) — **Deadly Skullcap / Funeral Bell**

- **Cap:** 1–5 cm; brown to tawny, hygrophanous (paler at center when dry); smooth
- **Gills:** Brown; adnate to slightly decurrent
- **Spore print:** **Rusty brown**
- **Stipe:** Slender; with a ring (which may disappear); fibrous; brown below the ring
- **Habitat:** **Grows in clusters on dead wood** — logs, stumps, buried wood debris, particularly in conifer forests but also deciduous; widespread
- **Range:** North America, Europe, Asia — very widespread

**Toxicology:** Contains amatoxins (α-amanitin) in concentrations comparable to *Amanita phalloides*. Lethal dose possible from a small number of caps. Same delayed-onset hepatotoxic syndrome as the death cap. Particularly dangerous because of its small size and unremarkable appearance, and because it grows in the same habitat as edible wood-inhabiting species.

### Look-Alike 1: Honey Mushroom (*Armillaria* spp.)

**Why they are confused:** Both grow in clusters on or near wood, especially stumps. *Armillaria* is a prized edible; *Galerina marginata* has killed people who mistook it for honey mushrooms.

| Character | *Galerina marginata* (DEADLY) | *Armillaria* spp. (edible with caution) |
|---|---|---|
| Cap size | Small: 1–5 cm | Larger: 3–15 cm typically |
| Spore print | **Rusty brown** | **White** |
| Gills | Brown | White to cream; may develop spotting |
| Ring | Present; fragile, fibrous, thin; often disappears | Present; more robust, cottony to membranous |
| Stipe base | No basal cluster attachment | Often in tight clusters attached at the base |
| Cap center | Often darker brown center (hygrophanous) | Variable; often has fibrous or scaly surface |

**Key test:** Spore print. Rusty brown = Galerina; white = Armillaria. Never skip this step when harvesting cluster-growing wood mushrooms.

### Look-Alike 2: Velvet Shank / Enoki (*Flammulina velutipes*)

**Why they are confused:** Both can grow on dead wood in cooler temperatures; both are small brown mushrooms.

| Character | *Galerina marginata* (DEADLY) | *Flammulina velutipes* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Spore print | **Rusty brown** | **White** |
| Stipe surface | Fibrous; ring (often faint) | **Velvety / fuzzy dark brown** toward base; distinctive |
| Season | Summer–autumn; also overwinter in mild climates | **Late autumn through winter and early spring** (cold-loving) |
| Gills | Brown | Pale cream to white |

### Look-Alike 3: *Kuehneromyces mutabilis* (Brown Stew Mushroom, Sheathed Woodtuft)

**Why they are confused:** *K. mutabilis* is edible and also grows on stumps in clusters; it overlaps significantly with *G. marginata* in appearance and habitat. This is one of the most dangerous species pairs in European mycology.

| Character | *Galerina marginata* (DEADLY) | *Kuehneromyces mutabilis* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Spore print | **Rusty brown** | **Brown** (similar; both brown) |
| Stipe surface **below ring** | Smooth to fibrous | **Distinctly scaly** — covered with dark recurved scales below the ring zone |
| Ring | Thin, fragile, may be absent | More evident ring zone |
| Spore size (microscope) | 8–10 × 5–6 μm; roughened surface | Smaller; smooth |

**Critical note:** The stipe scaling below the ring in *K. mutabilis* is the most reliable macroscopic differentiator, but it can be subtle. Take a spore print. When in doubt, do not eat.

## 4. False Morel (*Gyromitra esculenta*) vs. True Morel (*Morchella*)

### The Deadly Species

[[Gyromitra esculenta]] — **False Morel / Brain Mushroom / Beefsteak Morel**

- **Cap:** Irregular, brain-like, saddle-shaped, or wrinkled — **NOT** a regular honeycomb of pits and ridges; lobed and convoluted; reddish-brown to dark brown
- **Interior:** **Chambered** — when sliced longitudinally, the interior shows cotton-filled chambers separated by partitions; NOT fully hollow
- **Range:** Europe, North America; spring fruiting

**Toxicology:** Contains gyromitrin, which metabolizes to monomethylhydrazine (MMH) — a volatile, hepatotoxic, hemolytic compound related to rocket fuel. Symptoms: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea 6–12 hours after ingestion; then jaundice, hemolysis, liver failure. Fatality rate in untreated severe cases reported up to 30% historically in Europe. Partially detoxified by parboiling and discarding the water; raw and lightly cooked consumption is dangerous. **Considered unsafe to eat; do not forage.**

### The Edible Species

[[Morchella]] — **True Morel**

- **Cap:** Regular, well-defined **honeycomb pattern of pits and vertical ridges**; conical to elongated ovoid; gray, tan, yellow-brown, or black depending on species and age; cap margin attached to stipe at its base margin (in most *Morchella* species)
- **Interior:** **Fully hollow** throughout — slice longitudinally; both cap and stipe are completely hollow with no chambers or cotton filling
- **Smell:** Pleasant, earthy, mushroomy

| Character | *Gyromitra esculenta* (DANGEROUS) | *Morchella* (edible, must cook) |
|---|---|---|
| Cap shape | Irregular, brain-like, lobed, wrinkled | Regular honeycomb of pits and ridges |
| Interior | **Chambered / cotton-filled** | **Fully hollow** |
| Cap attachment | Cap hangs or drapes; not neatly attached to stipe margin | Cap attached at base margin to stipe |
| Color | Reddish-brown to dark brown | Gray, tan, yellow-tan, black (depends on species) |
| Season | Spring (overlaps with morels) | Spring |

**Test:** Slice the mushroom from top to bottom through the center. A true morel is empty like a hot-air balloon. A false morel shows chambers, cotton, or partial walls.

## 5. Jack-o'-Lantern (*Omphalotus olearius* / *O. illudens*) vs. Golden Chanterelle (*Cantharellus cibarius*)

### The Toxic Species

[[Omphalotus olearius]] / [[Omphalotus illudens]] — **Jack-o'-Lantern Mushroom**

- **Cap:** 5–15 cm; bright orange to deep orange-yellow; convex becoming broadly funnel-shaped; smooth
- **Gills:** **True gills** — thin, blade-like, sharp-edged plates that can be separated from cap flesh; bright orange; close-packed; **decurrent** (running down the stipe); in fresh specimens, emit a faint bioluminescence visible in complete darkness (this is not always obvious or reliable in the field)
- **Growth:** **Clustered at the base of trees or from buried wood roots**, especially oaks and olive trees; appears to grow from soil but is attached to buried wood
- **Spore print:** White to pale cream

**Toxicology:** Contains illudin sesquiterpenes — not amatoxins. Causes severe gastrointestinal illness: rapid-onset (within 1–3 hours) profuse vomiting, cramps, diarrhea lasting 1–3 days. Not fatal in adults but extremely unpleasant; has caused hospitalizations.

### The Edible Species

[[Cantharellus cibarius]] — **Golden Chanterelle**

- **Hymenium:** **False gills** — blunt, forking, shallow ridges that are **continuous with the cap flesh** and cannot be peeled away cleanly; fork repeatedly; lighter than cap color or same
- **Growth:** **Solitary or scattered on soil** in forest; never in tight clusters from a single base
- **Cap:** Golden to egg-yolk yellow; margin often wavy and lobed; surface smooth
- **Smell:** Distinctive fruity apricot aroma

| Character | *Omphalotus* (TOXIC) | *Cantharellus* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Hymenium | **True gills**: thin, blade-like, peelable | **False gills**: blunt ridges, continuous with flesh, cannot be separated |
| Growth habit | **Clustered from wood base** | **Solitary or scattered on soil** |
| Color | Deep orange to orange-yellow | Golden to egg-yolk yellow (more muted) |
| Smell | No distinctive aroma | **Apricot / fruity** — quite distinctive |
| Bioluminescence | Present in fresh specimens (gills glow faintly in dark) | Absent |

**Key test:** Try to peel a "gill" from the cap flesh. If it comes away cleanly like a blade, it is a true gill (not a chanterelle). If it tears the flesh and cannot be separated, it is a false gill (chanterelle characteristic). Also check whether mushrooms are growing singly from soil (chanterelle) or clustered from wood (jack-o'-lantern).

## 6. Green-Spored Parasol (*Chlorophyllum molybdites*) vs. Edible Parasol (*Macrolepiota procera*)

### The Toxic Species

[[Chlorophyllum molybdites]] — **Green-Spored Parasol / False Parasol / Vomiter**

- **Cap:** 8–30 cm; white to pale tan; covered with brownish scales; broadly convex becoming flat; margin may be shaggy
- **Gills:** White when young; gradually turning **green to grayish-green** as spores mature — this color change can be subtle in young specimens
- **Spore print:** **Green** — uniquely diagnostic; no other common look-alike produces a green print
- **Stipe:** White; has a ring; no volva; base slightly bulbous
- **Habitat:** Lawns, parks, golf courses, disturbed ground — particularly common in warm temperate and subtropical regions of North America, common in the southeastern US and southern California

**Toxicology:** The toxin(s) are not fully characterized but cause rapid-onset (1–3 hours) severe gastrointestinal illness: explosive vomiting, profuse diarrhea, abdominal cramping. *C. molybdites* is the single most common cause of mushroom poisoning in North America by case count, though rarely fatal in otherwise healthy adults. Can cause severe illness requiring hospitalization.

### The Edible Species

[[Macrolepiota procera]] — **Parasol Mushroom**

- **Cap:** Large — 10–30+ cm fully open; covered with brownish scales on a white background; conical when young, opening to a flat disc with a central brown umbo
- **Gills:** White; free; do not change color
- **Spore print:** **White** — never green
- **Stipe:** Very tall (10–40 cm); covered in a distinctive **snakeskin pattern** of brown scales; double ring that is moveable up and down the stipe; no volva; stipe base slightly bulbous
- **Habitat:** Open woodland edges, pastures, hedgerows; does not typically grow on manicured lawns

| Character | *C. molybdites* (TOXIC) | *Macrolepiota procera* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Spore print | **GREEN** | **White** |
| Gills | White when young; **turn greenish with age** | White throughout; do not turn green |
| Stipe pattern | Smooth to slightly fibrous; no snakeskin pattern | **Distinctive snakeskin/zigzag brown pattern** |
| Habitat | **Lawns, parks, disturbed ground** | Woodland edges, pastures; less on manicured lawns |
| Geography | Especially abundant in warm/subtropical regions | More common in temperate Europe; less common in southeastern US |

**Key test:** A green spore print is absolutely diagnostic for *C. molybdites*. Wait for the print. Also examine the stipe for the distinctive snakeskin pattern of *Macrolepiota* — its absence is a warning sign.

**Note on *Chlorophyllum* genus:** *C. brunneum* and *C. rachodes* (shaggy parasols) also exist and have caused some GI illness; they are sometimes confused with *M. procera* as well but have rough, shaggy cap and lack the snakeskin stipe pattern. Always check spore print.

## General Warnings

1. **Do not rely on color alone.** Color varies within species and with age, drying, cooking, and ambient light. The green-spored parasol can look pure white when young.

2. **The latent phase of amatoxin poisoning is a medical emergency.** If someone reports eating unidentified wild mushrooms and then develops severe GI symptoms 6–24 hours later, assume amatoxin poisoning until proven otherwise. Contact poison control immediately (US: 1-800-222-1222).

3. **"The death cap has no taste or smell."** This is essentially true — there is nothing about the flavor or aroma of *Amanita phalloides* to warn the consumer. Folk tests (taste, silver spoon, garlic clove method) are myths and provide no safety information.

4. **Introduced ranges are expanding.** *Amanita phalloides* is now established in California, the Pacific Northwest, and is spreading. Do not assume a look-alike is safe because "death caps don't grow here."

5. **Collect a reference specimen.** If anyone becomes ill after eating wild mushrooms, preserve a sample of what was eaten (refrigerate or photograph in detail). This is critical for medical treatment.
