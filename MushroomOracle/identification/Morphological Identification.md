# Morphological Identification

## Overview

Morphological identification is the systematic examination of a fungus's physical features to determine its species. No single character is sufficient — reliable identification requires assembling a "character suite" across multiple feature categories. This note defines the standard vocabulary and describes each character class used in field and microscopic identification of Basidiomycota fruiting bodies (gilled and polypore mushrooms).

See also: [[Fruiting Body Anatomy]] for anatomical definitions, [[Spore Prints]] for spore color methodology, [[Dangerous Look-Alikes]] for applied safety use of these characters.

---

## 1. Cap (Pileus) Shape

Cap shape changes with developmental age. Observing several specimens at different stages improves accuracy.

| Term | Description |
|---|---|
| **Convex** | Domed, broadly rounded (typical of many young mushrooms) |
| **Campanulate** | Bell-shaped; common in *Mycena*, young *Psilocybe* |
| **Umbonate** | Convex with a central raised boss or nipple (umbo); common in *Inocybe*, *Marasmius*, mature *Amanita* |
| **Papillate** | With a small, sharp pointed nipple |
| **Plane / flat** | Fully expanded cap with no dome |
| **Depressed** | Sunken in center, like a shallow bowl; common in *Russula*, *Lactarius* |
| **Infundibuliform (funnel)** | Deeply funnel-shaped; classic in *Cantharellus cibarius* with age, *Clitocybe* |
| **Umbilicate** | With a small central navel-like depression |

Cap margin features are also diagnostic: entire (smooth), wavy, lobed, striate (with radial grooves), appendiculate (with veil remnants hanging from edge).

---

## 2. Cap Surface (Pileus Surface)

The surface texture and condition of the cap pileus:

| Term | Description |
|---|---|
| **Smooth (glabrous)** | No surface ornamentation; dry or moist |
| **Viscid / glutinous** | Slimy or sticky when moist; dry and shiny when dry (e.g., *Suillus*, *Cortinarius* subgenus *Phlegmacium*) |
| **Dry / pruinose** | Dry, may be powdery or dull |
| **Fibrous / silky** | Surface covered in fine fibers (e.g., *Inocybe*) |
| **Scaly / squamulose** | Bearing flat or erect scales — either fibrillose scales (from splitting surface) or discrete scales (e.g., *Pholiota*, *Lepiota*) |
| **Warted** | Bearing discrete warts or pyramidal protuberances — usually universal veil remnants (diagnostic for *Amanita*) |
| **Velvet / tomentose** | Fine, densely felty surface; e.g., *Suillellus luridus* |
| **Hygrophanous** | Changes color as it absorbs or loses water — typically two-tone appearance at margin vs. center when partially dried; important character for many *Psilocybe*, *Hypholoma*, and brown-rot species |

## 3. Gill Attachment

How the gills connect to the stipe is one of the most taxonomically significant characters. Examine a fresh specimen by slicing vertically through the center of the cap and stipe.

| Term | Description | Example Taxa |
|---|---|---|
| **Free** | Gills do not touch the stipe; a gap exists | *Amanita*, *Agaricus*, *Lepiota* |
| **Adnate** | Gills attach broadly to the full depth of the stipe | *Stropharia*, many *Hypholoma* |
| **Adnexed** | Gills attach to only a portion of the stipe depth (notched upward near stipe) | Many *Mycena*, some *Inocybe* |
| **Sinuate / notched** | Gills dip downward just before touching the stipe, creating an indentation | *Tricholoma*, *Hebeloma* |
| **Decurrent** | Gills run down the stipe | *Omphalotus*, *Clitocybe*, *Cantharellus* (as ridges) |

False gills (blunt forking ridges, as in *Cantharellus*) are continuous with cap flesh and cannot be separated from it — true gills are discrete blade-like plates that can be peeled away cleanly. This distinction is critical for chanterelle vs. jack-o'-lantern identification.

---

## 4. Gill Characters

### Spacing

- **Crowded**: gills densely packed, many short gills (lamellulae) between primary gills
- **Close**: typical moderate packing
- **Subdistant / distant**: widely spaced gills, easily visible gaps

### Color

Gill color changes with maturity as spores develop. Always note the color in young, mid-aged, and mature specimens if possible. Pink-to-chocolate-brown transition in *Agaricus bisporus* is a classic example. White gills that stay white throughout development are characteristic of *Amanita*.

### Edge Characteristics

- **Entire**: smooth-edged gills
- **Eroded / fimbriate**: ragged, toothed, or fringe-edged gills — sometimes due to age or insect damage, but can be consistent in some species
- **Crenulate**: minutely scalloped edge

## 5. Stipe (Stem) Features

### Ring (Annulus)

A remnant of the partial veil — the membrane that protected the developing gills. Rings may be:
- **Superior**: high on the stipe
- **Inferior**: near the base
- **Double**: appearing as two concentric rings (e.g., some *Amanita*, large *Lepiota*)
- **Moveable / pendant**: hanging loosely like a skirt
- **Cortinate**: cobweb-like silky fibers in *Cortinarius* (not a true ring; disappears early, may leave a rust-brown zone from spore deposit)
- **Absent**: many species lack any veil

### Volva (Cup at Base)

The remnant of the universal veil at the stipe base. **Always dig up the entire base of any white-gilled mushroom to check for a volva.** Volva forms include:
- **Free cup-shaped / sac-like volva**: classic *Amanita phalloides*, *A. bisporigera* — loose sac encircling the stipe base; the base of the stipe sits inside the cup and can be pulled free of it
- **Adhering / girdling volva**: concentric rings or ridges at the bulb (e.g., *Amanita muscaria* — ridged bulb, not a free cup)
- **Absent**: most non-Amanita species lack a volva

### Bulb

- **Basal bulb**: rounded swelling at stipe base; found in many *Amanita*
- **Clavate**: club-shaped stipe, widening gradually toward base
- **Equal**: stipe of uniform diameter throughout

### Surface Texture

- **Smooth**
- **Fibrous / longitudinally fibrillose**: *Inocybe*, many *Cortinarius*
- **Reticulate (netted)**: raised network pattern on upper stipe; diagnostic for king bolete (*Boletus edulis*) and many edible boletes
- **Scabrous / granular**: with granules or glandular dots — characteristic of *Leccinum* species
- **Hollow vs. solid**: slice stipe longitudinally; hollow stipes in *Mycena*, *Psilocybe*, morels; solid in *Agaricus*, *Amanita* (solid in young stage)

---

## 6. Veil Remnants

Understanding veils explains most of the surface ornamentation seen on caps and stipes.

**Universal Veil (velum universale)**: A membrane encasing the entire immature fruiting body like an egg. As the cap expands, it tears and leaves:
- **Volva** at the base (see above)
- **Warts or patches** on the cap surface (e.g., the white warts of *Amanita muscaria*; these may be washed off by rain)

**Partial Veil (velum partiale)**: A membrane connecting cap margin to upper stipe, protecting gills. It tears and leaves:
- **Annulus (ring)** on the stipe (see above)
- **Appendiculate fragments** hanging from the cap margin (some *Cortinarius*, *Galerina*)

Universal veil structures are strongly associated with *Amanita* — the most dangerous mushroom genus. Presence of a volva and cap warts in any white-gilled mushroom should trigger maximal caution.

## 7. Bruising Reactions

Color changes of the flesh when cut, bruised, or scratched:

| Reaction | Significance | Key Species |
|---|---|---|
| **Blue / blue-green staining** | Oxidation of psilocybin/psilocin to blue-colored quinoid products (not universal — absence of bluing does not rule out psilocybin) | *Psilocybe* spp. (most); some *Boletus* (stipe blue when cut) |
| **Yellow at stipe base** | Phenol reaction from 1-[4-hydroxyphenyl]-2-(methylamino)ethanol-related compounds | *Agaricus xanthodermus* (toxic; avoid) — scratching the stipe base gives chrome-yellow flash |
| **Yellow on cap / flesh** | May indicate quinone compounds; present in some *Amanita* | *Amanita phalloides* flesh yellows slightly under KOH |
| **Red staining** | Bolete identification — some *Neoboletus*, *Rubroboletus* stain red/orange rapidly when cut; indicates presence of variegatic/xerocomic acid | *Rubroboletus satanas* (devil's bolete — toxic), *Neoboletus erythropus* (edible after cooking) |
| **No change** | The default; alone carries no diagnostic weight | Many edible and toxic species alike |
| **Lilac / purple** | Some *Cortinarius*, *Inocybe*, *Lepista* | Note color and location (flesh, gills, stipe) |

## 8. Smell

Olfactory characters are underutilized but often immediately diagnostic. Train your nose by smelling known species repeatedly.

| Smell | Associated Species / Groups |
|---|---|
| **Anise / licorice** | *Clitocybe odora*, *Marasmius* spp., *Pleurotus ostreatus* (mild) |
| **Radish / horseradish** | *Russula* spp., *Marasmius scorodonius* |
| **Bleach / fishy / chemical** | *Mycena* spp. (bleach), *Clitocybe* (some); strongly phenolic in *Agaricus xanthodermus* (inky smell) |
| **Pleasant earthy/mushroomy** | Generic; most good edibles |
| **Apricot / fruity** | *Cantharellus cibarius* (apricot), *Lactarius deliciosus* (mild) |
| **Foul / fetid / sulfurous** | *Phallus impudicus* (stinkhorn — deliberately attracts flies for spore dispersal), some *Clitocybe*, *Tricholoma inamoenum* |
| **Almond** | *Agaricus* (pleasant species); faintly in *A. campestris* |
| **Inky / phenolic** | *Agaricus xanthodermus* (toxic); warning character |
| **Flour / mealy** | *Inocybe*, some *Cystoderma*, some *Tricholoma* |

## 9. Chemical Spot Tests

Used by serious amateurs and professional mycologists to confirm characters invisible to the naked eye. Apply with a cotton swab or drop to fresh flesh or surface.

| Reagent | Reaction | Use |
|---|---|---|
| **KOH (potassium hydroxide, 10%)** | Color reaction with cap cuticle or flesh; yellow/red/black | Differentiates *Russula* spp., confirms *Amanita* cap flesh reaction, checks bolete pores |
| **Iron salts (FeSO₄)** | Greenish/blue on fresh flesh | Russula identification; green reaction on flesh distinguishes R. aeruginea and allies |
| **Melzer's reagent** (IKI — iodine-potassium iodide) | **Amyloid** (blue-black) vs. **inamyloid** (unchanged) vs. **dextrinoid** (red-brown) | Spore amyloidity is a key microscopic character; important for *Amanita* — deadly section *Phalloideae* has amyloid spores, *muscaria* section has inamyloid spores |
| **Ammonia** | Color reaction with cap surface | Differentiates some *Amanita* species |
| **Phenol** | Positive reaction (brown/red) in some toxic *Agaricus* | *A. xanthodermus* test |

## 10. Context (Flesh) Color and Changes

- **White and unchanging**: common in *Amanita*, most *Agaricus* (edible species)
- **White turning pink**: some *Russula*, some *Lactarius*
- **Exuding latex when cut**: *Lactarius* species — latex color and taste are diagnostic (white, watery, blue-green (*L. indigo*), orange-red (*L. deliciosus*), acrid vs. mild)
- **Stuffed then hollow stipe**: morels are fully hollow; false morels (*Gyromitra*) are chambered inside — always slice to verify
- **Blue/blue-green bruising in flesh**: psilocybin-containing species; also some boletes on stipe

---

## Bringing It Together: The Character Suite Approach

A reliable identification requires:

1. **Macroscopic characters**: cap shape + surface, gill attachment + color, stipe features (ring, volva, reticulation), veil remnants, bruising, smell
2. **Spore print**: color from a 4–12 hour print (see [[Spore Prints]])
3. **Substrate and habitat**: tree associations, deadwood vs. soil, dung, grassland (see [[Habitat Guide]])
4. **Geographic and seasonal context**: range maps and fruiting season
5. **Cross-reference multiple field guides** for your region

No single character is sufficient. A white-gilled mushroom without a volva could still be dangerous (*Galerina*). A mushroom with a ring is not automatically an *Amanita*. Always build the full picture.
