# Habitat Guide

## Overview

The habitat where a mushroom is found — substrate, surrounding tree species, soil type, disturbance history, and season — narrows identification dramatically. Most mushrooms are not generalists; they are tied to specific substrates, host trees, or ecological conditions by their nutritional strategy (mycorrhizal, saprotrophic, parasitic). Knowing the habitat conditions of a find is a critical component of the character suite used for identification.

See also: [[Morphological Identification]] for the full identification workflow, [[Mycorrhizal Relationships]] for ecology of tree-fungal associations, [[Foraging Safety]] for responsible harvest practices.

---

## 1. Deciduous Forest — Oak, Beech, and Mixed Hardwood

**Ecological context:** Temperate deciduous forests with mature oaks (*Quercus*), beeches (*Fagus*), birches (*Betula*), hornbeams (*Carpinus*), hazels (*Corylus*), and chestnuts (*Castanea*) support the richest ectomycorrhizal diversity of any habitat. The deep litter layer and complex soil chemistry support dense mycelial networks.

### Characteristic Species

**Ectomycorrhizal (associated with living tree roots):**
- **[[Cantharellus cibarius]] (Golden Chanterelle)** — oak, beech, spruce; fruiting July–November; in moss and leaf litter of mature forest floors; golden-yellow; fruity apricot smell; false gills; one of the most reliable edibles
- **Boletes (*Boletus edulis* and allies)** — king bolete / porcini associated with beech, oak, and spruce; broad deciduous-conifer interface zones; fruiting summer–autumn; distinctive reticulate stipe, pores instead of gills
- ***[[Amanita muscaria]] (Fly Agaric)*** — birch, oak, pine; very broad host range; the red-and-white warts iconic species; psychoactive/toxic; volva present; fruiting summer–autumn
- ***Amanita phalloides* (Death Cap)** — oak, beech, introduced worldwide with tree nurseries; **deadly**; olive-green cap; white gills; sac volva; see [[Dangerous Look-Alikes]]
- ***Russula* spp.** — enormous genus; oak and beech specialists especially; brittle flesh; no ring or volva; many edible (R. cyanoxantha, R. virescens), some acrid and unpleasant, a few mildly toxic
- ***Lactarius* spp.** — associates with oak (L. quietus), birch (L. pubescens), and beech; exude latex when cut; L. deliciosus (saffron milk-cap, orange latex) is edible and prized
- ***Cortinarius* spp.** — vast genus; many deadly species (C. rubellus, C. orellanus — orellanine nephrotoxin); cobweb-like cortina veil; rusty-brown spore print; oak, beech, conifer associates; avoid all unless expert-identified

**Saprotrophic (decomposing deadwood or litter):**
- ***[[Grifola frondosa]] (Maitake / Hen of the Woods)*** — grows at the base of oaks, sometimes chestnuts; large overlapping gray-brown fronds from a central stipe cluster; white pores; fruiting late summer–autumn; edible and medicinal
- ***[[Hericium erinaceus]] (Lion's Mane)*** — on dead or dying hardwood, especially beech and oak; pure white cascading spine clusters; edible and medicinal
- ***Trametes versicolor* (Turkey Tail)** — on dead wood of many deciduous species; colorful concentric-zoned thin brackets; white to cream pores; common year-round; medicinal polysaccharide source
- ***Pleurotus ostreatus* (Oyster Mushroom)** — dead or dying beech, elm, poplar, alder; fan-shaped gray caps in overlapping clusters; white gills decurrent on lateral stipe; fruiting autumn–spring

## 2. Conifer Forest — Pine, Spruce, Fir, Larch

**Ecological context:** Boreal and montane conifer forests support a different ectomycorrhizal community from deciduous forests. Many specialist boletes and agarics are obligate conifer associates; some are circumpolar in distribution.

### Characteristic Species

**Ectomycorrhizal:**
- ***Suillus* spp.** — slimy-capped yellow-brown boletes; almost all are obligate conifer associates (S. luteus with pine, S. grevillei with larch, S. brevipes with pine); ring often present; pores angular; edible but soft and not highly regarded
- **Matsutake (*Tricholoma matsutake*)** — pine associate; very high commercial value especially in Japan; white with fibrous stipe and partial veil; fruiting autumn; aromatic, spicy smell distinctive; range: pine forests of Japan, Korea, North America (T. magnivelare), Scandinavia
- ***Amanita muscaria* (Fly Agaric)** — see above; also strongly associated with spruce and pine in boreal regions; the conifer-associated populations may differ in toxin levels from deciduous-forest specimens
- ***Gomphidius* spp.** — pin-shaped; slimy viscid cap; decurrent dark gills; black spore print; pine associates; edible but not popular
- ***Hygrophorus* spp. (Wax Caps)** — waxy-feeling gills; moist to viscid cap; associated with conifers and some deciduous trees; mixed edibility; important for ecological health of conifer forests
- **Fly Agaric allies (*Amanita* section *Amanita*)** — multiple white-volva species in conifer forest; extreme caution warranted

**Saprotrophic:**
- ***Galerina marginata* (Deadly Skullcap)** — **deadly**; on conifer stumps and logs (and deciduous); brown, small, clustered; rusty-brown spore print; contains amatoxins; see [[Dangerous Look-Alikes]]
- ***[[Trametes versicolor]]***, *Phlebia radiata*, *Stereum spp.* — wood decomposers common on conifer deadwood
- *Pleurotus* spp. — less common on conifers but occurs on pine and other softwoods in some regions

## 3. Grassland and Pasture

**Ecological context:** Old unimproved grasslands, permanent pastures, meadows, and traditional hay meadows support a distinct and often conservation-important fungal community. Many grassland fungi are sensitive to nitrogen enrichment (fertilization) and plowing — their presence often indicates historically unimproved soils.

### Characteristic Species

- ***Agaricus campestris* (Field Mushroom / Meadow Mushroom)** — grassland, lawns; pink gills when young turning dark chocolate; chocolate-brown spore print; pleasant almond smell; edible; compare carefully with destroying angel (wrong habitat, but volva check still required)
- **Fairy Ring Mushroom (*Marasmius oreades*)** — forms rings or arcs in lawns and pastures; tan to buff cap; free pale gills; tough flexible stipe (does not snap cleanly); pleasant smell; edible; distinct from poisonous fairy-ring-forming species (*Clitocybe dealbata*)
- **Field Blewit (*Lepista personata* / *L. saeva*)** — lilac-tinged stipe; buff-brown cap; adnate gills; pink-buff spore print; autumnal; grassland; edible
- **Wax Caps (*Hygrocybe* spp.)** — indicator species of ancient, unimproved grasslands in Europe (CHEG grasslands); brilliant red, yellow, orange, or white; waxy gills; most not edible in quantity; ecologically important
- **Puffballs (*Calvatia*, *Vascellum* spp.)** — various sizes; grassland puffballs edible when interior is completely white
- ***Psilocybe semilanceata* (Liberty Cap)** — small; pointed nippled cap; olive-brown when moist (hygrophanous); psilocybin-containing; found in unfertilized sheep pastures and similar environments in autumn; northern and central Europe, Pacific Northwest; controlled substance in most jurisdictions

## 4. Deadwood and Stumps

**Ecological context:** Decaying wood — fallen logs, stumps, standing snags, buried root systems — is the substrate for the majority of saprotrophic macrofungi. Species range from early colonizers (white-rot and brown-rot fungi attacking fresh wood) to late colonizers of highly decayed substrates.

### Characteristic Species

- ***[[Pleurotus ostreatus]] (Oyster Mushroom)*** — dead hardwood; see above; one of the most recognizable and beginner-safe species
- ***[[Laetiporus sulphureus]] (Chicken of the Woods)*** — bright sulphur-yellow and orange shelf brackets; on oak, beech, cherry, yew, sweet chestnut; brown-rot fungus; young flesh edible; striking coloration — nearly unmistakable
- ***[[Trametes versicolor]] (Turkey Tail)*** — colorful concentric-banded thin polypore; white pores; on dead hardwood; year-round; medicinal; no edible value but widely harvested for extract
- ***[[Hericium erinaceus]] (Lion's Mane)*** — white cascading spines; on dead or dying hardwood; edible and medicinal; increasingly cultivated
- **Honey Mushroom (*Armillaria* spp.)** — clustered on stumps and buried roots; often with a ring; white spore print; edible (must be cooked thoroughly); aggressive parasite of living trees as well as decomposer; confused with deadly *Galerina marginata* — check spore print (white in *Armillaria*; rusty brown in *Galerina*)
- ***[[Ganoderma lucidum]] (Reishi / Lingzhi)*** — woody, varnished-looking red-brown polypore; growing at base of oaks and other hardwoods; bitter; not for eating raw but widely used as health supplement; white pores; brown spore print
- ***Pholiota* spp.** — clustered on wood; scaly caps; brown spore prints; most are inedible or mildly toxic; can be confused with honey mushroom and *Galerina*
- ***[[Inonotus obliquus]] (Chaga)*** — black-crusted sterile conk on living birch; the actual fruiting body (rare) is different; harvested as health supplement

## 5. Dung and Enriched Substrates

**Ecological context:** Herbivore dung (cattle, horse, sheep, deer, elephant) and highly nitrogen-enriched soils create a specialized niche for coprophilous (dung-loving) fungi. These species have evolved to germinate after gut passage or after rain wets dung in the field.

### Characteristic Species

- ***Psilocybe cubensis* (Golden Teacher / Magic Mushroom)** — on cattle and horse dung in tropical and subtropical regions; golden-brown cap; white to lilac-gray gills; purple-brown spore print; bluing when bruised; psilocybin-containing; controlled substance in most jurisdictions; natural range: subtropical Americas, Southeast Asia, Pacific; spread with cattle ranching globally
- ***Panaeolus campanulatus* / *P. cinctulus*** — on dung and enriched soil; dark gills mottled with spores; black spore print; some species psilocybin-containing (*P. cyanescens*); small, fragile
- ***Coprinus comatus* (Shaggy Mane / Lawyer's Wig)** — on lawns, roadsides, disturbed ground; white shaggy cap autodigesting to inky black liquid from bottom to top; edible when young and pure white, before autodigestion begins; harvest promptly — decays within hours
- ***Coprinellus micaceus* (Glistening Ink Cap)** — clustered on stumps and enriched soil; shiny mica-flecked cap; autodigests; edible when young; contains coprine in some *Coprinus* relatives (causes disulfiram-like reaction with alcohol — avoid alcohol for 3 days if eating)
- ***Stropharia rugosoannulata* (Wine Cap / King Stropharia)** — on straw mulch, wood chips, compost; large red-brown cap; purple-brown spore print; edible; can be cultivated in garden beds

## 6. Urban and Disturbed Ground

**Ecological context:** Gardens, parks, lawns, roadsides, urban forests, and disturbed habitats support a surprising number of fungal species — including both excellent edibles and dangerous species. Do not assume that urban settings make mushrooms safer.

### Characteristic Species

- ***Agaricus* spp.** — field mushrooms and related species in lawns and parks; check for pink-to-brown gills and chocolate spore print; avoid those turning chrome-yellow at stipe base (*A. xanthodermus*)
- ***Coprinus comatus* (Shaggy Mane)** — roadsides, lawns, disturbed ground; see above
- ***Macrolepiota procera* / *Chlorophyllum molybdites*** — large parasol mushrooms on lawns; *C. molybdites* especially common on lawns in warm climates; green spore print = dangerous; see [[Dangerous Look-Alikes]]
- ***Stropharia rugosoannulata* (Wine Cap)*** — wood chip mulch in parks and gardens; large and edible; purple-brown spore print
- ***Marasmius oreades* (Fairy Ring Mushroom)*** — fairy rings in lawns; see above
- **Dryad's Saddle (*Cerioporus squamosus*)** — on dead wood in urban parks; large flat bracket with snakeskin-patterned cap; white pores; edible when young and fresh; watermelon smell

**Urban caution:** Mushrooms near roadsides may accumulate heavy metals (lead, cadmium) and other environmental pollutants. The general guideline is to forage no closer than 50–100 metres from busy roads, and to avoid industrial zones entirely. See [[Safety and Contraindications]].

## 7. Seasonal Timing by Region

Fruiting phenology varies dramatically by climate zone. These are general guidelines; local conditions (rainfall, temperature) drive actual timing.

### Temperate Northern Hemisphere (UK, Central Europe, Eastern North America, Pacific Northwest)

| Season | Key Species |
|---|---|
| **Early spring (March–April)** | True morels (*Morchella*); St. George's mushroom (*Calocybe gambosa*); Dryad's saddle (early flush) |
| **Late spring (May–June)** | Morels continuing; oyster mushroom second flush; early boletes at altitude |
| **Summer (July–August)** | Chanterelles (beginning); first boletes; summer ceps (*Boletus* spp.); *Russula* and *Lactarius*; *Amanita muscaria* early |
| **Autumn — peak season (September–November)** | Peak diversity: chanterelles, boletes, hedgehog mushrooms (*Hydnum*), honey mushrooms, maitake, lion's mane, turkey tail flushes, fly agaric, *Cortinarius* spp., all wax caps, field mushrooms |
| **Late autumn / winter (November–February)** | Velvet shank (*Flammulina velutipes*) — frost-tolerant; oyster mushrooms (second and third flushes in mild spells); wood ear (*Auricularia auricula-judae*) |

### Pacific Coast North America (California, Oregon, Washington)

Wet winters rather than wet autumns drive the season. Main foraging season is **November–March** for most species; summer drought suppresses fruiting. Chanterelles peak December–February in California.

### Subtropical / Tropical

Year-round fruiting dependent on rainfall. Dry season brings little activity; wet season triggers major flush events. *Psilocybe cubensis* and *Termitomyces* spp. are examples of wet-season tropical specialists.

## Using Habitat in Identification

Habitat serves as a rapid pre-filter:

- A white-gilled mushroom growing **in woodland near oaks** raises the prior probability of *Amanita* significantly — dig the base
- A yellow-orange mushroom growing **clustered from a tree base** is more likely *Omphalotus* than *Cantharellus* — check for false vs. true gills
- A brown mushroom growing **clustered on conifer stumps** — check for *Galerina marginata* before assuming it is a honey mushroom; take a spore print
- A large parasol mushroom on a **manicured lawn** in warm climates is more likely *Chlorophyllum molybdites* than the edible *Macrolepiota* — take a spore print

Habitat is never sufficient for identification on its own, but combined with morphological characters and spore print, it is a powerful tool.
