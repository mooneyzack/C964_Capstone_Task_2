# Biotech Applications

Fungi are the dominant workhorses of industrial biotechnology. From the discovery of penicillin in 1928 to the multibillion-dollar industrial enzyme market, the pharmaceutical pipeline, and the global food fermentation industry, fungal metabolism has been harnessed at an extraordinary scale. Fungi offer a combination of properties that make them ideal biotechnology platforms: they secrete large quantities of proteins directly into growth medium (simplifying purification), they grow rapidly on cheap substrates, they are amenable to genetic manipulation, and they naturally produce a staggering chemical diversity evolved over hundreds of millions of years of competition and co-evolution.

This note covers four major domains: industrial enzyme production, pharmaceutical manufacturing, fermentation-based food and fuel production, and emerging genome engineering applications.

## Industrial Enzyme Production

Fungi are the primary source of industrial enzymes — biocatalysts used in food processing, textile manufacturing, paper production, detergents, and biofuel production. The global industrial enzyme market exceeded $6 billion USD by the early 2020s, and fungal species dominate it.

### The Key Production Organisms

**Aspergillus niger:**
- One of the most extensively used industrial microorganisms in history
- Produces: citric acid (>2 million tonnes/year globally; used in food/beverages as acidulant); glucoamylase (breaks starch to glucose; essential for glucose syrup and bioethanol); glucose oxidase (food preservation, biosensors); pectinases (fruit juice clarification); proteases
- GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe) status in the USA for food applications
- Sequenced genome (2007) revealed >1,000 genes encoding secreted hydrolytic enzymes — a remarkable secretory capacity

**Trichoderma reesei (anamorph: Hypocrea jecorina):**
- Isolated from the Solomon Islands during World War II when it was found degrading US Army cotton uniforms; reclassified and industrialized for enzyme production
- The most prolific **cellulase producer** known; industrial strains can secrete 100+ grams of protein per liter of culture medium (extraordinary in biotechnology)
- Primary enzyme products: cellobiohydrolase I and II (CBH I/II), endoglucanases, β-glucosidases — the complete cellulase system required to convert cellulose to glucose
- Critical for: **cellulosic bioethanol** (breaking down agricultural waste into fermentable sugars), textile processing (biostoning of denim), paper manufacturing

**Aspergillus oryzae:**
- The koji mold; domesticated in East Asia over >2,000 years; see the Fermentation section below
- Industrial enzymes: amylases (starch processing), proteases (protein hydrolysis, flavor enhancement), lipases
- Considered safe for food use across multiple regulatory jurisdictions

### Industrial Enzyme Applications

| Enzyme | Fungal Source | Application |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| Glucoamylase | *Aspergillus niger* | Glucose syrup, bioethanol from starch |
| Cellulase | *Trichoderma reesei* | Cellulosic ethanol, textile biostoning, paper |
| Amylase | *Aspergillus oryzae*, *A. niger* | Baking (dough conditioning), brewing |
| Laccase | *Trametes versicolor*, *Myceliophthora thermophila* | Textile dye decolorization (see [[Fungal Dyes]]), paper bleaching |
| Pectinase | *Aspergillus niger* | Fruit juice clarification, wine processing |
| Lipase | *Candida rugosa*, *Thermomyces lanuginosus* | Biodiesel production, detergents, food flavors |
| Protease | *Aspergillus niger*, *A. oryzae* | Detergents, cheese ripening, meat tenderizing |
| Xylanase | *Trichoderma reesei*, *Aspergillus niger* | Paper bleaching, baking, animal feed |
| Phytase | *Aspergillus niger* | Animal feed (releases phosphate from phytic acid, reducing phosphorus pollution) |

### Production Methods

Industrial enzyme production typically uses **submerged liquid fermentation (SmF)** in stirred-tank bioreactors (5,000–200,000 liter scale):
1. Selected fungal strain inoculated into sterile medium (complex carbon/nitrogen sources)
2. Fermentation for 3–7 days under controlled pH, temperature, and aeration
3. Mycelium separated by filtration or centrifugation
4. Enzyme-rich broth concentrated and formulated (as liquid concentrate or dried powder)
5. Quality tested for enzyme activity, purity, and safety

**Solid-state fermentation (SSF)** — growing fungi on solid substrates (wheat bran, rice bran, corn cobs) — is used for some enzymes, particularly in Asian food/enzyme production, and produces higher enzyme concentrations per unit mass of substrate for some applications.

## Pharmaceutical Manufacturing

Fungi have provided some of the most important drugs in medical history, and continue to be a source of pharmaceutical compounds.

### Antibiotics: Penicillin

The chance 1928 observation by Alexander Fleming that *Penicillium notatum* produced a substance lethal to *Staphylococcus aureus* — and the subsequent development of penicillin by Florey and Chain during World War II — is arguably the most impactful moment in the history of medicine.

**Industrial production:**
- The production strain shifted from *P. notatum* to *Penicillium chrysogenum* (now reclassified as *Penicillium rubens*), which produces penicillin at far higher yields
- Deep-tank fermentation was developed in the 1940s (driven by wartime urgency) — a technological breakthrough that established the modern biopharmaceutical fermentation industry
- Modern high-producing strains, developed through decades of mutagenesis and selection, produce ~50 g/L penicillin — compared to microgram quantities from Fleming's original cultures
- Corn steep liquor — a cheap byproduct of corn starch processing — proved to be a critical medium component, discovered in a 1943 screening program at NRRL (Peoria, Illinois)
- Global penicillin production: ~40,000–50,000 tonnes per year; price reduced from $20/dose in 1943 to cents today

**Semi-synthetic penicillins:** 6-aminopenicillanic acid (6-APA), the penicillin nucleus produced by removing the side chain, is produced by fermentation of *P. chrysogenum* and is the chemical starting point for synthesis of all semi-synthetic penicillins (ampicillin, amoxicillin, etc.).

### Immunosuppressants: Cyclosporine

**Cyclosporine A** is produced by *Tolypocladium inflatum* (formerly *Beauveria nivea*), a soil ascomycete collected in Norway in 1969. Discovered in a routine antibiotic screen at Sandoz (now Novartis) in 1971.

- Mechanism: binds cyclophilin → inhibits calcineurin → blocks IL-2 transcription → prevents T-cell activation
- Clinical significance: cyclosporine made **organ transplantation** clinically practical; before its introduction, rejection rates made most transplants short-lived; it enabled kidney, heart, and liver transplantation to become routine procedures
- Annual global production: hundreds of tonnes
- Produced by submerged fermentation of *T. inflatum*, with the cyclosporine complex extracted from the mycelium (it is intracellular/cell-wall-associated rather than fully secreted)

### Statins: Lovastatin and Compactin

**Lovastatin** was discovered in 1978 by Merck researchers screening *Aspergillus terreus* fermentation broths; it was approved by the FDA in 1987 as the first statin drug.

- Mechanism: inhibits HMG-CoA reductase — the rate-limiting enzyme in cholesterol biosynthesis
- *A. terreus* produces lovastatin via a polyketide biosynthesis pathway; the gene cluster has been fully characterized
- Semisynthetic derivatives: lovastatin is converted chemically to simvastatin; pravastatin is derived from compactin (produced by *Penicillium citrinum*)
- Statins became the best-selling drug class in history; at peak, atorvastatin (Lipitor, synthetic but derived from fungal template research) generated ~$13 billion/year in sales

**Oyster mushrooms and lovastatin**: *[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]* naturally produces lovastatin in edible fruiting bodies (see [[Lovastatin in Oyster Mushrooms]]) — a notable case of a pharmaceutical compound in a common food.

### Other Fungal Pharmaceuticals

| Drug | Producing Fungus | Application |
|------|-----------------|------------|
| **Mycophenolic acid** | *Penicillium stoloniferum* | Immunosuppressant (mycophenolate mofetil = CellCept); organ transplant, autoimmune disease |
| **Griseofulvin** | *Penicillium griseofulvum* | Antifungal; inhibits microtubule formation; dermatophyte infections |
| **Cephalosporins** | *Acremonium chrysogenum* | Broad-spectrum antibiotics; β-lactam class |
| **Ergot alkaloids** | *Claviceps purpurea* | Ergotamine (migraines); ergometrine (obstetric hemorrhage); LSD (semi-synthetic from ergotamine) |
| **Taxol (paclitaxel)** | *Taxomyces andreanae* (endophyte) | Anticancer; originally isolated from Pacific yew tree but the endophytic fungus is a co-producer |
| **Fusidic acid** | *Acremonium fusidioides* | Antibiotic for staphylococcal skin infections |

## Fermentation: Food, Beverage, and Fuel

Fungal fermentation underpins much of human food culture and a growing portion of the global energy economy.

### Saccharomyces cerevisiae: Baker's and Brewer's Yeast

*Saccharomyces cerevisiae* is the most domesticated organism on Earth. Human selection over thousands of years has produced thousands of distinct strains adapted to specific fermentation niches:

- **Bread**: yeast CO₂ production leavens dough; fermentation flavor compounds (esters, alcohols) contribute to aroma
- **Beer**: yeast strains selected for fermentation character, attenuation, flocculation, and flavor — ale strains (top-fermenting, 18–22°C) vs. lager strains (*S. cerevisiae* × *S. eubayanus* hybrid, bottom-fermenting, 5–12°C)
- **Wine**: wild yeasts begin fermentation; *S. cerevisiae* typically dominates and completes it; hundreds of commercial wine yeast strains selected for different regional and varietal character
- **Spirits**: whisky, brandy, rum — *S. cerevisiae* produces the alcohol; distillation concentrates it
- **Bioethanol**: *S. cerevisiae* ferments glucose and fructose to ethanol at ~90% of theoretical maximum; global fuel ethanol production ~100 billion liters/year (primarily from sugarcane in Brazil and corn starch in the USA)
- **Engineered strains for lignocellulosic ethanol**: wild-type *S. cerevisiae* cannot ferment xylose (5-carbon sugar abundant in agricultural residues); engineered strains with xylose isomerase genes from bacteria expand the fermentable sugar spectrum

### Koji: Aspergillus oryzae and A. sojae

**Koji** (*koji* = 麹) is grain or soybean colonized by *Aspergillus oryzae* or *A. sojae* — a technology developed in East Asia at least 2,000–3,000 years ago and the foundation of an enormous range of fermented foods and beverages:

- **Sake**: rice koji produces amylases that convert rice starch to glucose; *S. cerevisiae* then ferments glucose to alcohol
- **Miso**: soybean (or grain) koji broken down by protease and amylase action; mixed with salt and additional soybeans; fermented for weeks to years
- **Soy sauce (shoyu)**: wheat + soybean koji; extended salt fermentation; Maillard reaction products responsible for color and savory depth
- **Doenjang, douchi, tianmian**: Chinese and Korean fermented soybean pastes using related *Aspergillus* and *Rhizopus* species
- **Rice vinegar**: koji-based sake fermented further by *Acetobacter* to acetic acid
- **Mirin, amazake**: sweet rice ferments from koji amylase activity without yeast alcohol fermentation

*Aspergillus oryzae*'s enzyme repertoire — particularly its prolific extracellular proteases and amylases — is what makes it irreplaceable in these fermentations. The umami flavor of fermented Asian foods arises from free amino acids (especially glutamate) released by koji protease activity.

### Mycoprotein: Quorn

**Quorn** is a meat substitute produced from *Fusarium venenatum*, an ascomycete originally isolated from soil in Buckinghamshire, England in 1967 during a Rank Hovis McDougall (RHM) screening program anticipating protein shortages.

**Production process:**
1. *Fusarium venenatum* is grown in a **continuous airlift fermentation** — glucose and mineral salts fed continuously while culture is continuously harvested; the fermenter runs for weeks without stopping
2. Fermentation conditions (pH 6, 30°C, high aeration) favor growth of dense, filamentous mycelium
3. Harvested mycelium is heat-treated to reduce RNA content (regulatory requirement; high RNA consumption can cause gout)
4. The fungal biomass — **mycoprotein** — is textured, flavored, and formed into meat-like products (minced, chunky, whole-cut)

**Nutritional profile:**
- ~45% protein (dry weight)
- All essential amino acids; PDCAAS (protein quality) score >0.9 — similar to meat
- High fiber (chitin and glucans contribute)
- Low saturated fat
- Lower carbon and water footprint than chicken (the most efficient conventional meat protein), and vastly lower than beef

**Scale**: Quorn produces **>15,000 tonnes of mycoprotein per year** (as of mid-2020s) from a facility in Billingham, UK. The product is sold in 17+ countries.

**Competitor landscape**: *Air Protein* (produces protein from *Cupriavidus necator* on CO₂, not a fungus but often compared); *Nature's Fynd* uses *Fusarium* strain from Yellowstone hot springs; *Meati* (formerly Emergy Foods) uses dense mycelium blocks from an unnamed *Neurospora*-related species.

## Biofuel Production

### Cellulosic Ethanol

The largest remaining challenge in sustainable biofuel production is converting **lignocellulosic biomass** — agricultural residues (corn stover, wheat straw, sugarcane bagasse), energy crops (switchgrass, Miscanthus), and forest residues — into fermentable sugars. This requires breaking down crystalline cellulose and hemicellulose, which is expensive and difficult.

Fungal cellulases — primarily from *Trichoderma reesei* — are the primary biocatalysts used commercially for this conversion. The process:

1. **Pretreatment**: dilute acid or steam explosion disrupts lignin structure and makes cellulose accessible
2. **Enzymatic hydrolysis**: *T. reesei* cellulase cocktail (supplemented with *Aspergillus* β-glucosidase) converts cellulose to glucose over 48–72 hours
3. **Fermentation**: *S. cerevisiae* (often engineered for xylose fermentation) converts sugars to ethanol
4. **Distillation and recovery**

**Consolidated bioprocessing (CBP)**: the frontier concept is to engineer a single organism capable of both cellulase production and ethanol fermentation, eliminating the separate enzyme addition step. *Trichoderma* and *Fusarium* species have been engineered toward this goal; no commercial CBP process has yet been deployed at scale.

### Fatty Acids and Oleochemicals

Several oleaginous fungi (capable of accumulating >20% of dry weight as lipids) are candidates for producing lipid-based biofuels and specialty chemicals:

- *Mortierella alpina* (omega-6 PUFA, arachidonic acid — used in infant formula)
- *Cunninghamella* spp. (fatty acid production)
- *Mucor circinelloides* (gamma-linolenic acid, GLA)

## CRISPR and Genome Engineering in Fungi

Fungi have become important platforms for CRISPR-Cas9 genome editing, both as research tools and as production hosts for engineered pathways.

### Key Achievements

- **Pathway engineering in *Aspergillus niger***: CRISPR-enabled knockout of competing metabolic pathways has improved yields of target compounds (organic acids, enzymes) by eliminating byproduct formation
- **Lovastatin pathway transfer**: the biosynthetic gene cluster from *Aspergillus terreus* has been transferred to *Aspergillus nidulans* (a more tractable research host) and partially to *S. cerevisiae*, demonstrating the feasibility of heterologous natural product production
- **Penicillin pathway optimization**: CRISPR editing of *P. chrysogenum* to delete competing pathways and overexpress rate-limiting biosynthetic steps has produced strains with improved penicillin yields
- ***Trichoderma reesei* engineering**: deletion of the transcriptional repressor *ace1* produces constitutively high cellulase expression without the need for cellulose induction
- **Yeast metabolic engineering**: *S. cerevisiae* has been engineered to produce artemisinic acid (antimalarial drug precursor), opioids, cannabinoids, and many other plant natural products — demonstrating that fungal systems can be programmed to produce compounds from any kingdom

### Tools and Challenges

Standard CRISPR-Cas9 works in filamentous fungi but requires adaptation: transformation of mycelium (protoplast transformation, Agrobacterium-mediated), selection systems, and protocols for regenerating colonies from edited cells. Many filamentous fungi have multinucleate cells, requiring selection through enough generations to achieve homokaryosis (all nuclei bearing the edit).

**CRISPR activation (CRISPRa)** — transcriptional activation without gene cutting — has been used to upregulate **silent biosynthetic gene clusters** (BGCs) in *Aspergillus* and related fungi. Many fungi carry dozens of BGCs that are not expressed under laboratory conditions; CRISPRa activation has revealed new natural products not discovered by conventional screening.
