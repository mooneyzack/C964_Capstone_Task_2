# Mycoremediation

Mycoremediation is the use of fungi to degrade, transform, or immobilize environmental contaminants — from petroleum hydrocarbons and heavy metals to pesticides, pharmaceuticals, and radioactive compounds. It exploits the same enzymatic machinery that fungi evolved to decompose lignocellulose (see [[Wood Decay]]) and redirects it at man-made pollutants that the industrial era introduced faster than conventional biology could adapt to.

The field was popularized by mycologist Paul Stamets, whose book *Mycelium Running* (2005) brought it to broad public attention, though the underlying science dates to research on white-rot enzymes in the 1980s. The landmark organism for mechanistic research is *[[Phanerochaete chrysosporium]]*, and the most practically deployed species is *[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]* (oyster mushroom).

---

## The Enzymatic Foundation

White-rot fungi (see [[Wood Decay]], [[Decomposition and Carbon Cycling]]) secrete a battery of oxidative enzymes with exceptionally broad substrate specificity:

| Enzyme | Mechanism | Key Producers |
|--------|-----------|--------------|
| Lignin peroxidase (LiP) | High-redox-potential heme peroxidase; attacks aromatic rings via direct electron abstraction | *[[Phanerochaete chrysosporium]]*, *Trametes versicolor* |
| Manganese peroxidase (MnP) | Oxidizes Mn²⁺ → Mn³⁺; Mn³⁺ chelates with organic acids and acts as diffusible oxidant | *[[Phanerochaete chrysosporium]]*, *Pleurotus ostreatus*, *Irpex lacteus* |
| Versatile peroxidase (VP) | Combines LiP and MnP activity in a single enzyme | *[[Pleurotus eryngii]]*, *Bjerkandera* spp. |
| Laccase | Copper oxidase; oxidizes phenolic compounds; reduces O₂ to water | *[[Trametes versicolor]]*, *[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]*, *Myceliophthora thermophila* |

Because these enzymes evolved to attack the heterogeneous aromatic polymer lignin — a structure with no regular repeating unit — they are unusually non-specific. This is the key insight behind mycoremediation: an enzyme system designed to attack lignin also attacks polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), dioxins, PCBs, and many xenobiotics that share aromatic ring structures.

## Petroleum Hydrocarbon Degradation

Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) — including naphthalene, pyrene, benzo[a]pyrene, and anthracene — are persistent toxic components of petroleum, diesel fuel, and coal tar. They accumulate in soils and sediments, resist conventional bioremediation, and several are classified as probable carcinogens.

### Mechanism

White-rot fungi attack PAHs through two pathways:

1. **Peroxidase-mediated oxidation**: LiP and MnP oxidize the aromatic ring to generate unstable radicals, initiating ring-opening reactions that ultimately mineralize the compound to CO₂ and H₂O
2. **Cytochrome P450 co-metabolism**: intracellular oxidases that initially evolved for sterol metabolism can oxidize a range of xenobiotic substrates

### The Stamets Diesel Experiment (2005)

In a landmark field study at a property in Bellingham, Washington, four large piles of diesel-contaminated soil were treated with different agents: one with oyster mushroom mycelium (*Pleurotus ostreatus*), one with indigenous bacteria, one with standard bioremediation bacteria, and one left untreated as control.

After **eight weeks**:
- The oyster mushroom pile had reduced total PAH concentration by **~95%**, turning from dark, tarry, near-sterile soil to a rich, dark substrate colonized by mushrooms
- The bacterial piles showed moderate reduction
- The control pile showed no significant change
- Insects, birds, and other invertebrates returned to the mushroom pile — absent from the others — suggesting complete ecological restoration rather than merely reduced chemical toxicity

This experiment was not a peer-reviewed controlled study in the scientific literature sense, but it provided compelling proof-of-concept that catalyzed significant formal research.

### Key Species for PAH Remediation

- *[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]* — most studied for field application; produces robust MnP and laccase; grows rapidly on agricultural waste substrates; fruiting bodies can be harvested (if not contaminated), making the process economically self-sustaining
- *[[Phanerochaete chrysosporium]]* — model organism; produces highest LiP activity of any known fungus; does not fruit in field conditions but extensively used in laboratory studies
- *[[Trametes versicolor]]* — effective against benzo[a]pyrene and anthracene; produces laccases and MnP
- *Irpex lacteus* — emerging species; produces VP; effective at lower temperatures than *P. chrysosporium*
- *Bjerkandera adusta* — strong MnP producer; effective against high-molecular-weight PAHs

## Heavy Metal Biosorption

Fungi accumulate heavy metals — lead, cadmium, mercury, arsenic, chromium, uranium — from contaminated water and soil. This process is called **biosorption** (passive, non-metabolic binding to dead biomass) or **bioaccumulation** (active, metabolic uptake into living cells). Both mechanisms are relevant to mycoremediation.

### Binding Mechanisms

The fungal cell wall (see [[Mycelium Structure]]) is rich in chitin, glucans, mannoproteins, and melanins — all of which carry functional groups capable of binding metal ions:

- **Chitin/glucan**: amine (-NH₂) and hydroxyl (-OH) groups coordinate metal cations through electrostatic interactions and ion exchange
- **Melanins**: polyphenolic pigments with carboxylate, hydroxyl, and amine groups; extremely high metal-binding capacity per unit mass
- **Phosphate groups**: in cell membrane components; strong affinity for uranyl (UO₂²⁺) and thorium ions

### Most Effective Species

- *Aspergillus niger* — exceptional biosorption of lead, cadmium, and copper from acidic solutions; produces citric acid that may facilitate metal mobilization for uptake
- *Rhizopus arrhizus* — high uranium and lead uptake; one of the first organisms studied for nuclear waste remediation
- *[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]* — effective cadmium accumulator; a significant consideration for wild-harvest oyster mushrooms growing near industrial sites
- *Mucor rouxii* — strong chromium(VI) reduction, converting the more toxic hexavalent form to the less toxic trivalent form

### Biosorption vs. Phytoremediation

Fungal biosorption operates at pH ranges and metal concentrations (up to hundreds of mg/L) where plant-based phytoremediation fails. Dead fungal biomass (biosorbent) can be used in engineered systems like packed-bed reactors to treat industrial effluents, then stripped and regenerated with acid wash — a cyclic process amenable to engineering scale-up.

## Pesticide and Herbicide Degradation

Agricultural pesticides and herbicides persist in soil and water long after application. White-rot fungi degrade many of the most problematic:

| Compound | Class | Degrading Fungi | Products |
|----------|-------|-----------------|---------|
| Atrazine | Triazine herbicide | *[[Phanerochaete chrysosporium]]*, *Pleurotus pulmonarius* | Hydroxyatrazine, CO₂ |
| DDT | Organochlorine insecticide | *[[Phanerochaete chrysosporium]]*, *Trametes hirsuta* | DDE, DDD → further oxidized products |
| Lindane (γ-HCH) | Organochlorine insecticide | *[[Phanerochaete chrysosporium]]*, *Ganoderma* spp. | Chlorophenols → ring-opened products |
| Glyphosate | Phosphonate herbicide | *Aspergillus* spp., *Trichoderma* spp. | AMPA (aminomethylphosphonic acid) → phosphate |
| Chlorpyrifos | Organophosphate insecticide | *Phanerochaete sordida*, *Aspergillus niger* | 3,5,6-trichloropyridinol → CO₂ |

The broad specificity of laccase and peroxidase enzymes means that many aromatic-ring-containing pesticides are susceptible. The critical limitation is that enzymatic mineralization (complete breakdown to CO₂) is not always achieved — partial degradation may produce metabolites that are more or less toxic than the parent compound, requiring careful monitoring.

## Mycofiltration: Wastewater and Agricultural Runoff

**Mycofiltration** applies mycelial mats as a biological filter medium. As water passes through densely colonized substrate, the mycelium physically filters particles and simultaneously degrades dissolved organics.

### E. coli and Fecal Coliform Reduction

Research by Stamets and colleagues at Fungi Perfecti (Washington State) demonstrated that wood chip beds colonized by *Pleurotus* spp. reduced *E. coli* in agricultural runoff by **10,000-fold** (4-log reduction) under field conditions at a farm in Snohomish County, Washington. The mechanism combines:

- Physical entrapment in the mycelial mat
- Antibacterial compounds secreted by *Pleurotus* (including ostreatin and pleurotomycin)
- Competition for nutrients that inhibits bacterial growth

### Design

A mycofilter bed consists of:
- Agricultural waste (wood chips, straw) as substrate
- Fungal spawn inoculated at 10–20% by weight
- A 2–4 week pre-colonization period before receiving runoff
- Optional layering with burlap or permeable geotextile

Mycofilter beds require periodic replacement as the mycelium exhausts the substrate, but the spent substrate itself is an excellent soil amendment. The low cost of oyster mushroom spawn and substrate makes this economically accessible for small-scale agricultural operations.

## Radiotrophic Fungi and Radioactive Waste

One of the more extraordinary mycological discoveries of the 1990s and 2000s is the existence of **radiotrophic fungi** — species that not only tolerate ionizing radiation but appear to use it as an energy source.

### The Chernobyl Discovery

Following the 1986 Chernobyl nuclear disaster, robotic cameras surveying the reactor interior documented black, melanin-rich fungi colonizing the walls of the damaged reactor — moving *toward* the radiation source rather than away from it. Species recovered included *Cladosporium sphaerospermum*, *Cryptococcus neoformans*, *Wangiella dermatitidis*, and *Aureobasidium pullulans*.

A 2007 study (Dadachova et al., *PLOS ONE*) demonstrated that:
- Melanized fungi grew significantly faster in the presence of ionizing radiation than in its absence
- Radiation caused changes in the electronic structure of melanin, analogous to how chlorophyll responds to photons
- The energy appeared to be channeled into metabolic processes (specifically, increased NADH production)

This is mechanistically distinct from all previously known biological energy capture: not photosynthesis, not chemosynthesis, but **radiosynthesis**.

### Implications for Waste Remediation

While radiotrophic fungi cannot remove radioactive isotopes (biology cannot change atomic composition), they offer:
- **Biosorption of radioactive metals**: melanin's exceptional metal-binding capacity makes radiotrophic species candidates for sequestering radioactive cesium, strontium, and uranium from contaminated water
- **Extreme environment colonization**: ability to grow where other organisms cannot, enabling bioremediation in highly contaminated zones
- **Potential space applications**: NASA has investigated radiotrophic fungi for shielding materials in deep space

Experiments with *Cladosporium* spp. in simulated nuclear wastewater have shown promising biosorption of Cs-137 and Sr-90, though the work remains at research scale.

## Plastic Degradation

Plastics represent a severe and growing environmental contamination challenge. Fungi have emerged as unexpected candidates for plastic degradation.

### Pestalotiopsis microspora

In 2011, Yale researchers (Russel et al., *Applied and Environmental Microbiology*) isolated *Pestalotiopsis microspora* from the Amazon rainforest and demonstrated it could grow using **polyurethane as its sole carbon source** — in both aerobic conditions and **anaerobically** (significant because landfill interiors are anaerobic). The key enzyme was identified as a serine hydrolase.

Polyurethane is one of the most common plastics in foams, coatings, and synthetic textiles. The discovery opened the possibility of targeted biodegradation.

### Other Fungal Plastic Degraders

| Plastic | Fungal Species | Mechanism |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| Polyurethane (PU) | *Pestalotiopsis microspora*, *Aspergillus tubingensis* | Serine hydrolases, esterases |
| Polyethylene (PE) | *Aspergillus clavatus*, *Phanerochaete chrysosporium* | Laccase + alkane hydroxylase after UV pre-treatment |
| Polystyrene (PS) | *Aspergillus* spp. | Partial oxidation; significant UV sensitization required |
| PET | *Fusarium solani*, *Aspergillus* spp. | Cutinase-like enzymes; slow but measurable |

The challenge across all fungal plastic degradation is rate — most studies demonstrate degradation over weeks to months under laboratory conditions, not the hours or days that industrial processing requires. Genetic engineering of fungi for enhanced plastic-degrading enzyme production is an active research area.
