# Mycelium Construction

Mycelium-based construction materials extend the principles described in [[Myco-Materials]] into load-bearing, insulating, and acoustic building applications. The market reached **$910.3 million in 2024**, projected to reach **$2.55 billion by 2033** (CAGR 12.1%), with roofing and insulation as the largest segment at 31.7% of revenue.

---

## Thermal and Fire Properties

**Thermal insulation**: Mycelium composites achieve thermal conductivity of **0.031–0.05 W/m·K**, comparable to mineral wool and expanded polystyrene. R-value ranges **R-3 to R-4 per inch**.

**Fire resistance**: Mycelium chars rather than igniting, outperforming polystyrene in fire safety tests. Composite plates achieved **EI15 classification** per EN 13501-2:2003. Mogu's acoustic panels earned a **B-s1-d0 fire rating** (very limited combustibility, no smoke, no flaming droplets).

## Landmark Projects

### MycoHAB (Namibia, May 2024)

The **first house built from mycelium bricks** — a collaboration between Standard Bank Group, MIT Center for Bits and Atoms, MIT Label Free Research Group, and architecture firm Redhouse Studios (Cleveland, Ohio). A landmark demonstration of load-bearing mycelium construction.

### Self-Healing Living Building Materials (April 2025)

Published in *Cell Reports Physical Science*: engineers created materials using *Neurospora crassa* mycelium mineralized by *Sporosarcina pasteurii* bacteria, capable of **self-repair for over a month**. The EU-funded "Fungateria" initiative is advancing this further. If scalable, this represents a fundamental shift — building materials that heal cracks autonomously.

### UWE Smart Fungal Building

£2.5 million EU-funded project at the University of the West of England: walls infused with live mycelium that detect pollutants, light levels, and respond to human presence. Bridges [[Fungal Biocomputing]] and construction.

## Key Companies

### Biohm (UK)

Building a bio-manufacturing facility in Watchet, Somerset. Uses food waste (orange peel, cocoa husks) as substrate for mycelium insulation panels. Claims carbon-negative manufacturing. Panels are biodegradable and outperform traditional foam insulation. Holds 10 patents. Especially relevant as UK's Future Homes Standard takes effect from 2025.

### Mogu (Italy)

Raised **€11M Series A** (January 2024), co-led by ECBF and CDP Venture Capital, with Kering participating. Produces the first commercially available **acoustic panels** made entirely of fungal mycelium and upcycled textile residues. Also produces mycelium-based resilient floor tiles. Second brand, EPHEA, targets luxury fashion and automotive interiors.

### Other Players

- **GROWN.bio** (Netherlands) — first European mycelium composite producer; planning 10M-unit factory
- **Myconom Bio Materials**, **MIMBIOSIS**, **Mykor**, **MycoFoundry**, **COMU Labs Inc.**

## Mechanical Properties of Mycelium Composites

| Property | Value | Comparison |
|----------|-------|------------|
| Tensile strength (conventional) | ~6.3 MPa avg; best species (*Daedaleopsis confragosa*) at 6.51 MPa | EPS: 0.15–0.7 MPa |
| Tensile strength (ultra-high-pressure, 100 MPa processing) | 12.5 MPa | Approaching engineering plastics |
| Flexural strength (semi-wet hot-pressing) | 37.6 MPa | — |
| Density range (high-density MBC) | 440–680 kg/m³ | Could replace plywood/particleboard |
