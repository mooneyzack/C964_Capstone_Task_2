# Fungal Biocomputing

Fungal biocomputing is an emerging field exploring the use of living mycelium networks as substrates for sensing, signal processing, and computation. The primary researcher is **Andrew Adamatzky**, Professor in Unconventional Computing at the University of the West of England (UWE) Bristol, whose lab has produced the foundational work.

---

## Electrical Signaling in Mycelium

Mycelium generates electrical spikes that shift in response to light, pressure, and chemical stimuli, allowing it to act simultaneously as sensor, circuit, and processor.

**Key findings (Adamatzky lab):**

- Spike trains recorded at up to **50 distinct spikes per hour** in ghost fungi and caterpillar fungi, propagating at **0.5–2.6 mm/s**
- Statistical analysis of spike cluster lengths matched **Zipf's Law distribution** of human language words, with a fungal "lexicon" of up to 50 words (core lexicon of 15–20). The researchers caution that structural similarity to language **does not demonstrate meaning**
- Computation can be implemented in mycelium networks using fruit bodies as interfaces — fungi can implement **Boolean logic gates**, with the logical structure determined by mycelium geometry

## Mycelium as Electronic Components

### MycelioTronics (Graz University of Technology, Austria)

Published in *Science Advances* (2022): mycelium skin from *[[Ganoderma lucidum]]* can serve as a **biodegradable substrate for electronics**, replacing plastic in circuit boards.

### Mushroom Memristors (Ohio State University)

Common edible fungi (shiitake, button mushrooms) can be grown and trained to act as organic **memristors** — data processors that "remember" past electrical states, demonstrating memory effects similar to semiconductor chips.

### Bioprinted Living Fungal Bioelectronics (2025)

Living fungal bioelectronics fabricated using graphene-based electrodes integrated with fungal bioink via additive bioprinting. Living mycelium networks function as **thermal sensation systems** with rapid response and **intrinsic self-healing** — the networks autonomously grow into hard-to-reach environments and restore sensing capabilities after damage.

### Cross-Kingdom Interfacing

Researchers established a functional **reflex arc** by interfacing fungal electrical signals with cockroach neuromuscular systems via bioelectronic stimulation — demonstrating biological signal translation across kingdoms (*Global Challenges*, Wiley, 2024).

## Smart Fungal Buildings

**UWE Smart Fungal Building**: Received **£2.5 million EU funding** to develop walls infused with live mycelium that can detect pollutants, light levels, and respond to human presence. This connects directly to [[Mycelium Construction]] and [[Myco-Materials]].

---

## Applications and Outlook

Potential applications include:
- **Fungal biosensors** for environmental monitoring (moisture, pollutants, mechanical stress)
- **Sustainable wearables** with living, self-healing electronics
- **Biohybrid robots** incorporating living mycelium as sensory substrates
- **Smart building materials** that sense and respond to their environment

The field remains early-stage. Adamatzky's 2023 book *Fungal Machines: Sensing and Computing with Fungi* (Springer) consolidates the current state.
