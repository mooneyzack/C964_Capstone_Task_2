# Fungal Dyes

Fungi produce an extraordinary range of pigments — from vivid reds and purples to warm yellows, rich browns, and unexpected blues and greens. The use of fungi as natural dyes has a history stretching back centuries in Scandinavia and Northern Europe, and has experienced a significant revival as interest in sustainable, non-synthetic textiles has grown. Fungal pigments are structurally distinct from those of plants and insects, and several offer color ranges difficult or impossible to achieve with conventional natural dyes.

Fungal dyeing sits at the intersection of mycology, chemistry, and craft. Unlike synthetic textile dyes — most derived from coal tar or petroleum — fungal pigments are renewable, biodegradable, and produced without the heavy metal catalysts and toxic reagents of industrial dye synthesis. The limitations (scalability, reproducibility, and for some species, lightfastness) remain active areas of research and innovation.

---

## Historical Context

### Scandinavian and Northern European Traditions

The best-documented historical use of fungal dyes comes from Scandinavia, where foragers and textile artisans developed extensive knowledge of which species yielded usable colors on wool. The tradition was primarily preserved in **Finland, Sweden, and Norway**, where the combination of rich forest fungal diversity and strong textile heritage (particularly for natural wool dyeing) supported its continuation.

Significant species in this tradition included:
- *Phaeolus schweinitzii* (dyer's polypore) — yellows, greens, and khaki on wool
- *Pisolithus arhizus* (dead man's fingers / dyer's puffball) — rich brown-gold tones
- *Tapinella atrotomentosa* (velvet roll-rim) — blues and purples
- *Hapalopilus nidulans* (cinnamon bracket) — spectacular violet-purple
- *Cortinarius* species — reds, oranges, and purples from anthraquinone compounds

The 1970s natural dye revival — partly driven by counterculture interest in traditional crafts and partly by the work of researchers such as Miriam Rice in California — brought renewed documentation and experimentation with fungal dyes, and Rice's 1974 book *Let's Try Mushrooms for Color* became a foundational text.

## Key Dye Species and Their Pigments

### Cortinarius (Webcap Mushrooms)

*Cortinarius* is the largest genus of agaric fungi and an exceptionally rich source of **anthraquinone pigments** — a compound class also responsible for the red dye of cochineal (from the insect *Dactylopius coccus*) and madder (from *Rubia tinctorum* root).

Key pigments:
- **Emodin** and related hydroxyanthraquinones — orange to red tones
- **Dermocybin** (7-chloro-1-hydroxy-3-methylanthraquinone) — red; found in *C. sanguineus*, *C. phoeniceus*
- **Dermoglaucin** — bluish-green; *C. glaucopus*
- **Mephisto red** pigment — from *C. mephisto*

*Cortinarius sanguineus* (blood-red webcap) and *Cortinarius phoeniceus* (scarlet webcap) are particularly prized, yielding rich scarlet-to-crimson tones on alum-mordanted wool. These species are also notable because they produce the same or closely related anthraquinone pigments as expensive traditional dye plants and insects.

**Critical note**: Many *Cortinarius* species are severely toxic, containing **orellanine** — a nephrotoxic compound that causes delayed, often irreversible kidney failure. Dyeing with these species requires careful species identification and the understanding that the toxic compound (orellanine) is distinct from the anthraquinone pigments and does not transfer significantly to dyed fiber. Nevertheless, extreme caution is required; misidentification is dangerous.

### Pisolithus arhizus (Dyer's Puffball / Dead Man's Fingers)

One of the most widely used fungal dyes. The brown powder-like gleba (spore mass) inside mature fruiting bodies yields:
- **Rich amber, gold, and dark brown** tones without mordant
- **Warmer, more reddish browns** with iron mordant
- **Yellow-gold** with alum mordant

The pigment is **pisosterol** — a unique triterpenoid. Dyeing is straightforward: the fruiting body material is simmered in water with the fiber to produce excellent color. Lightfastness is moderate to good.

*Pisolithus arhizus* is also notable ecologically as a **ectomycorrhizal species** associated with oaks, pines, and eucalypts — important for understanding sustainable harvest considerations.

### Phaeolus schweinitzii (Dyer's Polypore)

A bracket polypore found on the bases of conifers, *Phaeolus schweinitzii* is considered one of the most dye-versatile fungi. A single species produces a remarkable range of colors depending on mordant and pH:

| Mordant | Color on Wool |
|---------|--------------|
| None (pH ~7) | Yellow-green |
| Alum | Bright yellow-green to chartreuse |
| Iron | Dark green to olive |
| Copper | Bronze-green |
| Tin | Bright gold-yellow |
| Chrome | Deep olive-gold |
| Ammonia modifier | Shifts toward greener tones |

The primary pigments are **grevillins** — terpenoids — and **schweinitzin**. The polypore can be collected in large quantities from conifer logs, making it one of the more practically scalable fungal dye sources. Younger (softer, yellow-brown) fruiting bodies yield brighter yellows; older specimens (darker, harder) yield deeper olive-greens.

### Hapalopilus nidulans (Cinnamon Bracket)

*Hapalopilus nidulans* produces one of the most dramatic and distinctive colors in natural dyeing: a **vivid violet-purple** unlike anything produced by conventional plant dyes. The pigment responsible is **polyporic acid** — a unique quinone compound.

Polyporic acid is sensitive to pH:
- **Acidic conditions** (pH <5): reddish-purple
- **Neutral**: true violet
- **Alkaline conditions** (pH >8): shifts toward blue-purple

With alum mordant on wool: a rich, clear violet that many dyers describe as superior in clarity to plant-derived purples.

**Important toxicity note**: *Hapalopilus nidulans* is **toxic** — polyporic acid is an inhibitor of mitochondrial function and has caused severe poisoning when the fungus was mistakenly consumed. As with *Cortinarius* species, dyeing is conducted with the understanding that the toxic compound is primarily a concern via ingestion, not skin or fiber contact, but appropriate caution (gloves, good ventilation) is recommended.

### Tapinella atrotomentosa (Velvet Roll-Rim)

This distinctive brown, velvety-capped mushroom yields a palette of **blue, blue-grey, and violet-grey** tones — the blue pigment being particularly unusual in natural dyeing.

Key pigments:
- **Atromentin** — a yellow-orange terpenoid precursor that, under alkaline conditions, converts to blue terphenylquinone derivatives
- The blue develops when the dye bath is raised to alkaline pH (ammonia, wood ash water)

With wool:
- Acidic/neutral bath: golden-yellow to olive
- Alkaline bath: blue-grey to slate blue
- Iron mordant + alkaline: deep blue-black

The unpredictable nature of the pH-sensitive color shift makes *Tapinella* particularly fascinating to dyers experimenting with process variables.

### Hydnellum and Phellodon (Tooth Fungi)

Several tooth fungi in these genera produce pigments of particular scientific interest:
- **Atromentin** and its variants (shared with *Tapinella*): blue tones
- **Thelephoric acid**: purplish-brown; widespread in tooth fungi
- **Variegatic acid**: yellow; light-sensitive

*Hydnellum aurantiacum* and *Phellodon tomentosus* yield earthy purples and browns. These fungi are rare in many regions and protected in parts of Europe, making them unsuitable for harvest-based dyeing programs — they are studied more for their pigment chemistry than for craft application.

## Mordanting and Process

### What Is Mordanting?

A mordant is a metallic salt that forms a coordination complex with both the dye pigment and the fiber, anchoring the color and frequently modifying the shade. Most protein fibers (wool, silk) mordant readily; cellulose fibers (cotton, linen) require a tannin pre-treatment before mordanting to accept color.

**Standard mordants used in fungal dyeing:**

| Mordant | Chemical | Effect |
|---------|---------|-------|
| Alum | Potassium aluminum sulfate (KAl(SO₄)₂·12H₂O) | Brightest, most neutral colors; most widely used |
| Iron | Ferrous sulfate (FeSO₄·7H₂O) | "Saddens" colors — shifts toward grey-green, darker, more somber tones |
| Copper | Copper sulfate (CuSO₄·5H₂O) | Enhances and shifts toward green; better lightfastness |
| Chrome | Potassium dichromate | Rich, deep tones; excellent lightfastness; **toxic** — use with great care |
| Tin | Stannous chloride | Brightens and intensifies; particularly vivid on anthraquinone dyes |

Mordanting is typically done before dyeing (pre-mordant) at 5–15% weight of fiber (WOF) by simmering fiber in mordant solution for 45–60 minutes.

### Basic Fungal Dyeing Process

1. **Harvest or obtain the fungal material** — fresh, dried, or frozen specimens can be used; dried material is more stable for storage
2. **Prepare the dye bath**: simmer chopped/broken fungal material in water at moderate heat (70–80°C) for 45–60 minutes; ratio typically 2:1 to 4:1 (weight of fungi to weight of fiber)
3. **Strain out fungal material**
4. **Add pre-mordanted, wetted fiber** to the dye bath
5. **Simmer at 80–85°C** for 45–60 minutes, stirring gently
6. **Cool slowly** in the bath (rapid temperature change can felt wool)
7. **Rinse**, wash gently with mild soap, rinse again
8. **Dry away from direct sunlight**

pH modifiers (acid = cream of tartar, citric acid; alkaline = ammonia, sodium carbonate, wood ash water) can shift colors before or during the dye bath.

## Lightfastness and Stability

Lightfastness — resistance to fading when exposed to light — is the most significant technical challenge for many fungal dyes.

| Species / Pigment | Lightfastness Rating | Notes |
|-------------------|---------------------|-------|
| *Cortinarius* anthraquinones | 4–6 / 8 (ISO 105-B02) | Good to very good; comparable to madder |
| *Pisolithus* pisosterol | 3–5 / 8 | Moderate; better on wool than cellulose |
| *Phaeolus* grevillins | 3–5 / 8 | Moderate; iron mordant improves |
| *Hapalopilus* polyporic acid | 4–6 / 8 | Good; one of the more stable fungal dyes |
| *Tapinella* atromentin-derived | 2–4 / 8 | Fair; blue tones fade faster |

Anthraquinone pigments (from *Cortinarius*) generally have the best lightfastness among fungal dyes — unsurprising given that anthraquinones are among the most lightfast natural dye compounds across all kingdoms. Terpenoid and polyphenolic pigments are generally less stable.

## Modern Applications

### Sustainable Textile Dyeing

The textile and fashion industry is under growing pressure to reduce its enormous environmental footprint — synthetic dyes contribute significantly to water pollution (many are carcinogenic or mutagenic), and conventional mordanting with chromium salts produces hazardous waste.

Fungal dyes offer:
- **Renewable and biodegradable pigments** — no persistent organic pollutant concerns
- **Unique colors** — particularly the violet range from *Hapalopilus*, which has no close synthetic equivalent in terms of the exact hue
- **Marketing differentiation** — "dyed with wild mushrooms" carries significant appeal in sustainable/artisanal fashion contexts

Several small-scale and luxury textile producers have incorporated fungal dyes, particularly in **Nordic countries** (Finland, Iceland, Norway) where the tradition is strongest and the supply of dye fungi most reliable.

### Art and Craft

Fungal dyeing is a significant niche within the natural dye arts community worldwide. Organizations including the **Natural Dye Network** (UK), the **Wild Colors Project** (US), and multiple Scandinavian craft societies maintain documentation, run workshops, and advocate for sustainable harvest practices.

### Cosmetics

Some fungal pigments — particularly those with demonstrated safety and stability — have attracted interest for cosmetic applications:
- *Monascus purpureus* (red yeast rice fungus) produces **monascin**, **rubropunctatin**, and related azaphilone pigments in red and orange; used as food colorant in Asia and under investigation for cosmetic use
- Melanins from *Ganoderma* and related species as natural skin pigments

### Industrial Scale Production

The primary barrier to scaling fungal dyes is **production volume**. Wild harvest of species like *Cortinarius* or *Hapalopilus* is inherently limited and ecologically constrained. Two paths toward scaling are under investigation:

1. **Submerged fermentation**: growing pigment-producing fungi in liquid fermenters without forming fruiting bodies; *Cortinarius*, *Phaeolus*, and others produce pigments in mycelial culture, though often at lower concentrations than in fruiting bodies
2. **Heterologous expression**: transferring the biosynthetic gene clusters for desirable pigments into fast-growing industrial hosts (*Aspergillus*, *Pichia*) for controlled fermentation production

Both approaches remain at research stage for most fungal pigments. See [[Biotech Applications]] for the broader context of fungal metabolite production.

## Sustainability and Harvest Ethics

- **Do not harvest rare or protected species**: *Hydnellum* and *Phellodon* tooth fungi are protected in UK and parts of northern Europe; *Cortinarius* webcaps, while not protected, should be harvested only where abundant
- **Bracket fungi** (*Phaeolus*, *Pisolithus*) are generally more abundant and suitable for larger-scale harvest
- **Cultivated or purchased sources**: some dye fungi can be cultivated (several *Cortinarius* species form mycorrhizal associations and cannot be commercially cultivated without their host trees); others can be grown in culture for pigment extraction
- **Color reproducibility**: wild-harvested fungi vary significantly in pigment concentration between specimens, seasons, and locations — a challenge for consistent production
