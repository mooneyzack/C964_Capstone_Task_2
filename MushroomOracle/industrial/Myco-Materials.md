# Myco-Materials

Myco-materials are products manufactured from fungal mycelium — the dense, three-dimensional network of hyphae described in [[Mycelium Structure]] — grown on agricultural waste substrates and processed into durable, compostable, functional materials. The field emerged from the intersection of materials science, sustainability, and mycology, and has attracted significant commercial investment and major brand partnerships within a remarkably short period.

The core insight is that mycelium, growing through and binding together lignocellulosic substrates (corn stalks, hemp hurds, wood chips, straw), naturally creates a composite material whose properties depend on the fungal species, the substrate, the growth conditions, and post-processing steps. Unlike conventional plastics derived from fossil fuels, mycelium composites are fully compostable, carbon-sequestering during growth, and produced at ambient temperature without energy-intensive manufacturing.

## Mycelium Packaging

### Ecovative Design

The commercial myco-materials industry effectively began with **Ecovative Design**, founded in 2007 in Troy, New York by Eben Bayer and Gavin McIntyre — then Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute students. Their insight was that mycelium could replace expanded polystyrene (EPS/Styrofoam) packaging.

**Manufacturing process:**

1. Agricultural waste (corn stalks, hemp hurds, wood chips, oat hulls) is cleaned and pasteurized
2. Fungal spawn is mixed in at 1–2% by weight
3. The mixture is loaded into molds of the desired shape (custom to each product's packaging geometry)
4. Mycelium colonizes for **5–7 days** at ambient temperature (~22–25°C), binding the substrate into a solid composite
5. The material is **heat-killed** (dried at ~60°C) to halt growth and stabilize the product
6. The finished piece is fire-resistant, water-resistant, and ready to ship

**Performance comparison with EPS:**

| Property | Mycelium Packaging | Expanded Polystyrene |
|----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Compressive strength | Comparable (tunable by species/density) | High |
| Thermal insulation | Good | Excellent |
| Moisture resistance | Moderate (improved by surface coatings) | Excellent |
| Compostability | 30–45 days in home compost | Centuries (non-biodegradable) |
| Carbon footprint | Net carbon-sequestering during growth | ~70 kg CO₂e per kg |
| Cost | Competitive at scale | Very low at established scale |

### Adoption

- **Ikea** announced in 2016 plans to switch to mycelium packaging for large fragile items (mirrors, flat-pack furniture), making it one of the largest early adopters
- **Dell** tested mycelium packaging for server and hardware shipment
- Multiple wine and luxury goods brands adopted it for premium, sustainable packaging positioning
- Ecovative licensed its technology to regional manufacturers globally, including in Europe and Asia

## Mycelium Leather and Textiles

The most commercially prominent myco-material application has become **mycelium leather** — a material that resembles animal leather in texture, drape, and workability, grown from fungal mycelium rather than animal hides.

### Key Companies and Products

**Bolt Threads — Mylo:**
- Founded 2009; developed Mylo from *Ganoderma* species mycelium grown on corn stalks
- Material is harvested as a thin sheet (~1–3 mm), tanned, and finished similarly to conventional leather
- Partnership with **Stella McCartney** (sustainable fashion leader): a one-of-a-kind Mylo bag exhibited at the Victoria and Albert Museum, London (2021)
- Partnership with **Adidas**: prototype sneakers; potential footwear line
- Partnership with **Lululemon**: yoga mat applications
- Partnership with **Kering** (parent of Gucci, Balenciaga): materials research collaboration

**MycoWorks — Reishi:**
- Developed "Fine Mycelium" technology: grows *Ganoderma* mycelium under controlled compression, creating a denser, more isotropic material with properties closer to full-grain leather
- Partnership with **Hermès** (2021): Sylvania — a limited-edition Victoria bag made from Reishi mycelium material; represents the most prestigious luxury fashion adoption of a myco-material to date
- The Hermès collaboration required MycoWorks to scale to commercial production volumes while maintaining the material quality standards required by one of the world's most demanding luxury brands

**Ecovative — Forager:**
- The same company that pioneered packaging extended into leather alternatives
- Produces MycoFlex for apparel and footwear applications

**Muskin:**
- Produced by Grado Zero Espace (Italy) from *Phellinus ellipsoideus*, a large bracket fungus
- Natural texture retained (not coated to resemble leather); used in luxury watch straps, bag components, shoe insoles
- Entirely natural finish without PU coating — closer to cork than smooth leather in aesthetic

### Growth and Processing

1. Mycelium is grown for 1–2 weeks on agricultural substrate in controlled temperature/humidity
2. The colonized mat is compressed and pressed to achieve desired thickness and density
3. Heat-treated to halt growth
4. Surface treated: cleaned, potentially surface-coated with natural resins or PU (a compromise some companies are working to eliminate)
5. Tanned (chromium-free, plant-based tannins for sustainable options)
6. Finished: embossed, colored, or left natural

### Challenges vs. Animal Leather

- **Durability**: mycelium leather currently falls short of full-grain bovine leather in abrasion resistance and longevity; improving with densification techniques
- **Water resistance**: natural mycelium is somewhat hydrophilic; requires coating treatments
- **Scale**: growing to the hundreds of thousands of square meters per year required by major fashion houses is a significant manufacturing challenge
- **Cost**: currently 2–5× more expensive than conventional leather; prices are falling as scale increases

## Building Materials: Insulation and Composites

### Mycelium Insulation

Mycelium composites grown in panel or board form function as thermal and acoustic insulation. Key properties:

- **Thermal conductivity**: 0.03–0.05 W/(m·K) — comparable to mineral wool and expanded polystyrene
- **Fire behavior**: mycelium composites **char rather than burn** — they self-extinguish when the flame source is removed, without producing toxic fumes (unlike polystyrene, which melts and releases styrene)
- **Acoustic absorption**: porous structure provides sound dampening, particularly in mid-to-high frequency ranges
- **Density**: adjustable from 50–300 kg/m³ depending on species and substrate
- **Structural strength**: compressive strength typically 15–50 kPa — sufficient for non-load-bearing panel applications

### Structural Composites and Bricks

Researchers at institutions including **Delft University of Technology** and the **Materials Research Institute** have demonstrated mycelium-bonded bricks, tiles, and structural panels. These are produced by:

1. Loading a mold with substrate + spawn
2. Allowing colonization (7–14 days)
3. Heat-killing and drying
4. Optional surface pressing or laminating

Compressive strengths approaching 0.3 MPa have been achieved — comparable to some conventional bricks, though well below concrete. Current applications focus on lightweight non-structural panels, furniture components, and architectural features.

### NASA and Space Applications

A notable research direction is **NASA's mycelium habitat program**: the idea of shipping fungal spores and agricultural waste to the Moon or Mars, then growing mycelium habitats *in situ* rather than transporting prefabricated structures from Earth. Launched myco-materials could theoretically self-assemble into structural forms in the target environment.

NASA's Ames Research Center funded preliminary research (2018–2020) under the Innovative Advanced Concepts (NIAC) program. Key challenges include the radiation environment (see [[Mycoremediation]] — radiotrophic fungi as a potential solution), low-gravity behavior of hyphal growth, and the need for a water and substrate source.

## Fungal Chitin Derivatives: Chitosan

The fungal cell wall is rich in chitin — a polymer of β-1,4-linked N-acetylglucosamine (see [[Mycelium Structure]]). Deacetylation of chitin produces **chitosan**, a versatile biopolymer with numerous industrial and medical applications.

### Fungal vs. Crustacean Chitosan

Chitosan is conventionally derived from crustacean shells (shrimp, crab) — a seafood processing byproduct. Fungal chitosan offers significant advantages:

| Property | Fungal Chitosan | Crustacean Chitosan |
|----------|----------------|---------------------|
| Source | *Rhizopus*, *Mucor*, *Aspergillus* mycelium | Shrimp/crab shells |
| Allergens | Shellfish-free | Shellfish allergen risk |
| Degree of deacetylation | Controllable (65–95%) | Variable, batch-dependent |
| Year-round availability | Yes (fermentation) | Seasonal, fishery-dependent |
| Vegan status | Vegan | Not vegan |
| Regulatory | Simpler (no heavy metals concern) | Potential heavy metal contamination |

### Applications

- **Wound dressings**: chitosan promotes hemostasis (blood clotting) and accelerates wound healing; chitosan-based wound dressings are FDA-cleared medical devices
- **Water purification**: positively charged chitosan (at pH < pKa ~6.5) binds negatively charged suspended particles, dyes, and heavy metals in water treatment
- **Food preservation**: chitosan films have antifungal and antibacterial properties; used as edible coatings on fruits and vegetables
- **Agriculture**: chitosan as a plant biostimulant — elicits defense responses and promotes root growth
- **Drug delivery**: chitosan nanoparticles as carriers for oral and mucosal drug delivery
- **Cosmetics**: thickening agent and film former; antimicrobial in personal care

Key producing fungi include *Rhizopus arrhizus*, *Mucor rouxii*, and *Aspergillus niger*. Industrial production typically involves growing the mycelium in submerged fermentation, then extracting and deacetylating the cell wall fraction.

## Species Used in Myco-Materials

| Species | Application | Notes |
|---------|------------|-------|
| *Ganoderma* spp. | Leather (Mylo, Reishi), packaging | Dense mycelium; good physical properties |
| *[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]* | Packaging, insulation, filtration | Fast growth; easy to work with |
| *Trametes* spp. | Research composites | High laccase; stiff composite |
| *Rhizopus arrhizus* | Chitosan production | High chitin/chitosan content |
| *Mucor rouxii* | Chitosan production | Controllable deacetylation |
| *Phellinus ellipsoideus* | Muskin leather | Natural bracket form used directly |
