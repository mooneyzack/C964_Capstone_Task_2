# Classification Methods

## Overview

Fungal classification has undergone a dramatic transformation over the past three decades, driven by advances in molecular biology. Two major paradigms have shaped how mycologists classify and identify fungi: traditional morphological classification and modern molecular phylogenetics. Today, these are combined in the **polyphasic approach**. See [[Kingdom Fungi]] for the full taxonomic hierarchy and [[Classification Methods]] context.

## Morphological vs. Molecular Phylogenetics Classification

### Morphological Classification

**Traditional/morphological classification** grouped fungi based on observable characteristics: reproductive structure type (gills, pores, teeth, cups), spore color, cap shape, presence of ring and volva, chemical reactions (amyloid spores, color changes with KOH), and growth substrate. This approach successfully organized fungi for centuries but proved unreliable for:

- Species complexes with high phenotypic plasticity (e.g., *Pleurotus ostreatus* complex)
- Convergently evolved structures (e.g., gills evolved independently multiple times)
- Cryptic species (morphologically identical but genetically distinct)

### Molecular Phylogenetics

**Molecular phylogenetics** uses DNA sequence data — initially single genes (ITS = Internal Transcribed Spacer, the universal barcode for fungi), then multi-gene datasets, now **genome-scale phylogenies** comparing thousands of orthologous genes. Key findings from molecular work:

- Proved the **paraphyly of Zygomycota**, necessitating its dissolution into Mucoromycota and Zoopagomycota (see [[Minor Phyla]])
- Revealed that many classically recognized genera are **polyphyletic** (do not share a single common ancestor)
- Demonstrated convergent evolution of mushroom morphology: the gilled fruiting body has evolved independently at least **eight times** within Agaricomycetes
- Enabled the recognition of approximately **20 phylogenetic species** within the previously confused [[Pleurotus ostreatus]] complex
- Reclassified Microsporidia as highly derived fungi rather than primitive eukaryotes
- Enabled the separation of [[Flammulina velutipes]] (North American/European) from [[Flammulina filiformis]] (East Asian) in 2018

### The Polyphasic Approach

The **polyphasic approach** — combining morphological, biochemical, ecological, and molecular data — is now the standard. The ITS (Internal Transcribed Spacer) region sequenced from a mushroom specimen can typically identify it to species level within hours using public databases like UNITE and GenBank.

## Notable Genera and Distinguishing Features

| Genus | Phylum | Key Features | Notable Species |
|---|---|---|---|
| *Amanita* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Universal veil producing volva + warts on cap; partial veil forming annulus; free white gills; many deadly species | [[Amanita phalloides]] (death cap), [[Amanita muscaria]] (fly agaric), [[Amanita caesarea]] (Caesar's mushroom) |
| *Agaricus* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Free gills turning pink then chocolate-brown; annulus present; often with anise/almond scent; spore print brown | [[Agaricus bisporus]] (button), [[Agaricus campestris]] (field mushroom), [[Agaricus xanthodermus]] (toxic) |
| *Pleurotus* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Eccentric to lateral stipe; decurrent gills; white spore print; white-rot on hardwoods; nematophagous | [[Pleurotus ostreatus]] (oyster), [[Pleurotus eryngii]] (king oyster) |
| *Boletus* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Pores instead of gills; stout stipe; reticulate pattern; ECM; often with trees | [[Boletus edulis]] (porcini), [[Boletus aereus]] |
| *Cantharellus* | [[Basidiomycota]] | False gills (forked ridges); fruity apricot aroma; ectomycorrhizal; cannot be cultivated | [[Cantharellus cibarius]] (golden chanterelle) |
| *Ganoderma* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Laccate (shiny) bracket; woody; white pore surface; brown spore print | [[Ganoderma lucidum]] (reishi), [[Ganoderma tsugae]] |
| *Trametes* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Thin, leathery brackets; pore surface; white-rot; multi-zoned cap | [[Trametes versicolor]] (turkey tail) |
| *Hericium* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Tooth fungi; white cascading spines; grows on hardwood wounds | [[Hericium erinaceus]] (lion's mane) |
| *Psilocybe* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Small brown mushrooms; blue staining when bruised; psilocybin/psilocin content | [[Psilocybe cubensis]], [[Psilocybe semilanceata]], [[Psilocybe azurescens]] |
| *Morchella* | [[Ascomycota]] | Honeycomb-pitted cap; hollow stipe; spring ephemeral; Ascomycete | [[Morchella esculenta]], [[Morchella elata]] |
| *Grifola* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Large overlapping shelf clusters; grows at base of oaks; polypore | [[Grifola frondosa]] (maitake) |
| *Ophiocordyceps* | [[Ascomycota]] | Entomopathogenic; stroma emerging from insect host; high altitude | [[Ophiocordyceps sinensis]] (caterpillar fungus), [[Ophiocordyceps unilateralis]] (zombie ant) |
| *Cordyceps* | [[Ascomycota]] | Entomopathogenic/saprotrophic; orange-red fruiting bodies; cultivated | [[Cordyceps militaris]] |
| *Inonotus* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Parasitic conk on birch; sterile mycelial sclerotium (chaga); white-rot causing | [[Inonotus obliquus]] (chaga) |
| *Lentinula* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Brown cap with white veil remnants; white gills; ECM-free; log cultivated | [[Lentinula edodes]] (shiitake) |
| *Flammulina* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Orange-brown slimy cap; velvety dark stipe; winter fruiting; dramatic wild vs. cultivated dimorphism | [[Flammulina velutipes]], [[Flammulina filiformis]] |
| *Laetiporus* | [[Basidiomycota]] | Bright orange-yellow brackets; overlapping shelves; brown-rot | [[Laetiporus sulphureus]] (chicken of the woods) |
