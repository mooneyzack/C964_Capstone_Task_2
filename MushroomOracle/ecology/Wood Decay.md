# Wood Decay

Wood decay by fungi is the primary mechanism by which the lignocellulosic biomass of dead trees is broken down and recycled. It is an extension of the broader processes described in [[Decomposition and Carbon Cycling]], and the specific fungi involved are important both ecologically and in applied contexts such as cultivation (see [[Substrates]]) and [[Mycoremediation]].

Wood is composed of three interlocked structural polymers:

- **Cellulose** (40–50%): linear chains of β-1,4-linked glucose; forms crystalline microfibrils
- **Hemicellulose** (20–35%): branched polysaccharides (xylan, mannan, glucan)
- **Lignin** (15–35%): aromatic polymer of phenylpropanoid units; the "glue" reinforcing the cell wall and the most recalcitrant component

Different fungal lineages have evolved distinct strategies for accessing the energy and nutrients locked in these polymers. All wood-decay fungi belong to [[Basidiomycota]] or [[Ascomycota]], and all operate through secreted extracellular enzymes and chemical oxidants mediated by the [[Mycelium Structure]].

## White Rot

**White-rot fungi** are capable of completely mineralizing all three major components of wood: cellulose, hemicellulose, **and lignin**. They are the **only organisms capable of efficiently degrading lignin**.

### Evolutionary Origin

White-rot fungi evolved during the **late Permian period** (~250 million years ago). Their emergence coincides with a sharp decline in carbon accumulation in geological strata — the end of the Carboniferous coal-forming era. Before white-rot fungi existed, dead wood accumulated for millions of years because nothing could break it down efficiently.

### Enzymatic Arsenal

| Enzyme | Abbreviation | Mechanism |
|--------|-------------|-----------|
| Lignin Peroxidase | LiP | Attacks phenylpropanoid units in lignin's heme pocket region |
| Manganese Peroxidase | MnP | Oxidizes Mn²⁺ → Mn³⁺; Mn³⁺ acts as a diffusible oxidant |
| Versatile Peroxidase | VP | Combines properties of LiP and MnP |
| Laccase | — | Copper-containing oxidase; cleaves lignin through phenolic ring oxidation |
| Cellobiohydrolases | CBH | Hydrolyze glycosidic bonds in crystalline cellulose |
| LPMOs (GH61) | — | Copper-dependent lytic polysaccharide monooxygenases; oxidatively cleave cellulose |

### Visual Result

Wood decayed by white-rot becomes **pale, bleached, soft, spongy, and stringy**. The pale color results from lignin removal, revealing the lighter cellulose fibers.

### Examples

- *[[Trametes versicolor]]* (turkey tail) — one of the most widespread white-rot species worldwide
- *[[Phanerochaete chrysosporium]]* — model research organism for lignin degradation
- *[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]* (oyster mushroom) — white-rot species; cultivated on [[Substrates]] including straw and hardwood
- *[[Ganoderma]]* spp. (reishi and relatives) — polypore white-rot fungi on hardwoods and conifers
- *[[Hericium erinaceus]]* (lion's mane) — white-rot on hardwoods, also cultivated

## Brown Rot

**Brown-rot fungi** degrade cellulose and hemicellulose but **cannot efficiently degrade lignin**. They lack the high-redox-potential class II peroxidases (LiP, MnP) of white-rot fungi and have lost more than 60% of carbohydrate-active enzyme genes (CAZys) compared to their white-rot ancestors.

### Mechanism: Fenton Chemistry

Rather than producing ligninolytic peroxidases, brown-rot fungi primarily use **Fenton chemistry** — generating highly reactive hydroxyl radicals (•OH) through the reaction:

> **Fe²⁺ + H₂O₂ → Fe³⁺ + OH⁻ + •OH**

The small size of hydroxyl radicals allows them to diffuse into the crystalline structure of cellulose before enzymatic hydrolysis, resulting in rapid depolymerization. Brown-rot fungi also produce oxalic acid, which chelates iron and helps drive the Fenton reaction.

### Evolutionary Origin

Brown-rot evolved **multiple times independently** from white-rot ancestral clades through convergent gene loss events — it is a derived, simplified strategy, not an ancestral one.

### Visual Result

Wood decayed by brown-rot becomes **dark brown, cubically cracked, and brittle**. The brown color comes from the oxidized, chemically modified lignin remaining after cellulose is removed. The characteristic cubic cracking pattern results from the loss of the cellulose scaffold.

Brown-rot residues (oxidized lignin fragments) contribute significantly to stable soil organic matter and are an important source of soil carbon.

### Examples

- *[[Serpula lacrymans]]* — "dry rot"; the most destructive wood decay fungus in buildings; produces extensive mycelial cords that transport water to dry wood
- *[[Coniophora puteana]]* — "wet rot"; common in damp timber
- *[[Fibroporia vaillantii]]* — mine timber decay
- *[[Laetiporus sulphureus]]* (chicken of the woods) — brown-rot polypore on hardwoods; edible when young

## Soft Rot

**Soft-rot fungi** are primarily [[Ascomycota]], with some [[Basidiomycota]]. They degrade cellulose and hemicellulose but do not efficiently attack lignin, similar to brown-rot fungi. Their distinguishing feature is the formation of characteristic **diamond-shaped (T-shaped) cavities** within secondary cell walls, visible microscopically.

Soft-rot fungi thrive in conditions where white- and brown-rot fungi cannot: very wet or aquatic environments, or very dry/extreme-temperature conditions. They are important in decay of:

- Submerged wood (docks, ships, archaeological timbers)
- Cooling tower timber
- Soil contact zones
- Agricultural waste composting

## Comparison Summary

| Feature | White Rot | Brown Rot | Soft Rot |
|---------|-----------|-----------|---------|
| Cellulose degraded | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Hemicellulose degraded | Yes | Yes | Yes |
| Lignin degraded | Yes (efficiently) | No (modified only) | No |
| Primary mechanism | Peroxidases + laccases | Fenton chemistry | Cellulases, cavitation |
| Visual appearance | Pale, spongy, stringy | Dark brown, cubic cracks | Surface softening |
| Dominant phylum | Basidiomycota | Basidiomycota | Ascomycota |
| Preferred habitat | Hardwoods and conifers | Mostly conifers | Wet/extreme environments |

## Ecological and Applied Significance

- White-rot fungi are essential for **global carbon cycling** — without them, lignin would accumulate indefinitely
- Brown-rot residues are a major source of **humic substances** in boreal forest soils
- The enzymatic systems of white-rot fungi are exploited in **[[Mycoremediation]]** for degrading persistent organic pollutants (PAHs, dioxins, PCBs)
- Understanding wood decay informs **cultivation substrate preparation** (see [[Substrates]]) — oyster mushrooms grow on white-rot substrates; species selection must match decay capability
