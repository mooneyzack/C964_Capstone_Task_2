# Decomposition and Carbon Cycling

**Saprotrophic (saprophytic) fungi** obtain nutrients from dead and decaying organic matter. They are the **primary decomposers of lignocellulosic material** on Earth — the dominant structural material of plant cell walls. The mechanisms by which they break down wood are detailed further in [[Wood Decay]]; the role of fungal decomposition in soil health is covered in [[Soil Health and Ecosystem Services]].

Saprotrophic fungi belong to both [[Basidiomycota]] and [[Ascomycota]], and their activity is mediated by the [[Mycelium Structure]] that threads through [[Substrates]] such as wood, leaf litter, and soil organic matter.

## Enzymatic Arsenal

Saprotrophic fungi secrete a range of powerful extracellular enzymes:

| Enzyme | Target | Mechanism |
|--------|--------|-----------|
| **Cellulases** | Cellulose | Break β-1,4-glycosidic bonds in cellulose chains, releasing glucose |
| **Hemicellulases** | Hemicellulose | Degrade xylans, mannans, glucan polymers |
| **Laccases** | Lignin | Copper-containing oxidases that attack lignin aromatic rings |
| **Peroxidases (LiP, MnP, VP)** | Lignin | High-redox-potential oxidoreductases that depolymerize lignin |
| **Pectinases** | Pectin | Degrade pectin in the middle lamella of plant cell walls |

## Scale of Activity

A single cubic inch of forest soil may contain kilometers of saprotrophic hyphae. As saprotrophs grow through decomposing wood and leaf litter, they also provide food for **fungivorous invertebrates** (springtails, mites, nematodes, fly larvae), forming the base of detrital food webs.

## Carbon Cycling

Fungi are the primary agents of **carbon mineralization** in terrestrial ecosystems. Saprotrophic fungi convert complex organic carbon (cellulose, lignin, chitin) into CO₂ and into fungal biomass, which is subsequently consumed or decomposed.

**Dual role of fungi in the carbon cycle:**

- **Saprotrophic fungi** = net carbon **sources** — they release CO₂ through respiration and mineralization of organic matter
- **Mycorrhizal fungi** = net carbon **sinks** — they store fixed carbon as fungal biomass in soil, contributing to stable soil organic matter formation

Plants with mycorrhizal associations (see [[Mycorrhizal Relationships]]) transfer up to **35% more carbon to soil** than non-mycorrhizal plants. Recalcitrant fungal compounds such as melanins and chitin (see [[Soil Health and Ecosystem Services]]) persist in soil for decades, contributing to long-term carbon sequestration.

**The lignin problem:** Lignin constitutes 15–35% of woody plant tissue and is one of the most recalcitrant organic polymers in nature. Only white-rot fungi (and to a lesser extent ericoid mycorrhizal fungi) can efficiently depolymerize it. The evolution of white-rot fungi in the late Permian (~250 million years ago) is thought to have ended the Carboniferous coal-forming era by enabling the decomposition of accumulated woody biomass. See [[Wood Decay]] for details.

## Ecological Context

Decomposition by saprotrophic fungi closes the nutrient cycle begun by photosynthesis:

1. Plants fix atmospheric CO₂ into organic compounds (sugars, cellulose, lignin)
2. Saprotrophic fungi secrete extracellular enzymes that break down plant material
3. Nutrients (N, P, K) are mineralized and returned to the soil solution
4. Mycorrhizal fungi (see [[Mycorrhizal Relationships]]) deliver these nutrients back to plant roots
5. Carbon is either respired as CO₂ or stored as stable fungal biomass

This cycle also underpins [[Mycoremediation]] — the application of fungal decomposition to break down environmental contaminants.

## Key Saprotrophic Groups

| Group | Decay Type | Examples |
|-------|-----------|---------|
| White-rot Basidiomycota | Cellulose + hemicellulose + lignin | *[[Trametes versicolor]]*, *[[Pleurotus ostreatus]]*, *[[Ganoderma]]* spp. |
| Brown-rot Basidiomycota | Cellulose + hemicellulose only | *[[Serpula lacrymans]]*, *[[Laetiporus sulphureus]]* |
| Soft-rot Ascomycota | Cellulose + hemicellulose only | Various, especially in wet/dry extremes |
| Litter-decomposers | Leaf litter, humus | *[[Marasmius]]* spp., *[[Mycena]]* spp. |
