# Parasitic Fungi

Not all fungi are decomposers or mutualists. A substantial portion of [[Kingdom Fungi]] are **parasites** — organisms that obtain nutrients from a living host at the host's expense. Fungal parasitism spans an enormous range of host types (plants, insects, other fungi, and even plants parasitizing fungi) and ecological roles.

Parasitic strategies contrast with the mutualistic strategies described in [[Mycorrhizal Relationships]] and the saprotrophic strategies described in [[Decomposition and Carbon Cycling]]. In all cases, the parasitic [[Mycelium Structure]] penetrates or surrounds host tissue to extract nutrients.

## Biotrophic Plant Parasites

**Biotrophic parasites** require a living host and cannot complete their life cycle on dead tissue. They are some of the most economically significant organisms on Earth.

### Rust Fungi (Pucciniomycotina)

Rust fungi are obligate biotrophic parasites of plants, belonging to [[Basidiomycota]]. They produce multiple spore stages (up to five in macrocyclic species) and often require two unrelated host species to complete their full life cycle (heteroecious rusts).

**Key species:**
- *[[Puccinia graminis]]* (wheat stem rust / black stem rust) — one of the most destructive crop pathogens in history; the "Ug99" lineage remains a major global food security threat
- *[[Puccinia striiformis]]* (yellow/stripe rust) — stripe rust of wheat and barley
- *[[Hemileia vastatrix]]* — coffee leaf rust; devastated the Sri Lankan coffee industry in the 1870s, prompting a shift to tea cultivation

**Morphology:** Produce characteristic rust-colored urediniospore masses (uredinia) on infected plant surfaces. The name "rust" refers to this reddish-brown coloration.

### Powdery Mildews (Ascomycota: Erysiphales)

Powdery mildews are [[Ascomycota]] obligate biotrophs. Unlike most fungal pathogens, they grow **on** the plant surface (epiphytically) rather than penetrating deep into tissue. Their hyphae produce **haustoria** — specialized feeding structures that penetrate individual epidermal cells to extract nutrients while leaving the cell alive.

**Characteristics:**
- Produce abundant white powdery conidia visible on leaf surfaces
- Can infect a wide range of crop plants (grapes, cucurbits, cereals, roses)
- Adapted to warm, dry conditions (unlike downy mildews, which require humidity)

### Smut Fungi (Ustilaginomycotina)

Smut fungi are [[Basidiomycota]] parasites that replace plant reproductive tissues with masses of dark teliospores ("smut").

**Key species:**
- *[[Ustilago maydis]]* (corn smut) — parasitizes maize, producing large galls on ears and tassels; considered a delicacy in Mexico (*huitlacoche*)
- *[[Ustilago tritici]]* — loose smut of wheat
- *[[Tilletia* spp.]]* — bunt/stinking smut of wheat

## Entomopathogenic Fungi

Entomopathogenic fungi parasitize and kill insects. They are important natural regulators of insect populations and are increasingly used as **biological control agents** in agriculture and forestry.

**Infection mechanism:**
1. Fungal spores (conidia) contact the insect cuticle
2. Spores germinate and penetrate the cuticle using enzymatic and mechanical pressure
3. Hyphae proliferate in the hemolymph (insect "blood"), consuming lipids and proteins
4. Host dies; fungus produces new conidia from the cadaver

### Key Species

**[[Ophiocordyceps unilateralis]]** (zombie ant fungus) — the most famous entomopathogen. Infects *Camponotus* carpenter ants, manipulates host behavior to cause the ant to climb vegetation and bite-grip a leaf vein at a precise height (optimized for spore dispersal), then kills the ant and produces a fruiting body from the head. Behavior manipulation is achieved through fungal metabolites acting on the ant's nervous system and muscles; the brain itself appears largely uninfected.

**[[Beauveria bassiana]]** — generalist entomopathogen used commercially as a biocontrol agent against thrips, whiteflies, aphids, and many other crop pests. Also being researched for malaria vector control (targets *Anopheles* mosquitoes).

**[[Metarhizium anisopliae]]** — another widely used biocontrol agent; also shows potential for [[Mycoremediation]] of soils contaminated with hydrocarbons.

**[[Cordyceps militaris]]** — parasitizes moth pupae; produces fruiting bodies rich in cordycepin and used in traditional medicine. Unlike *Ophiocordyceps*, it can be cultivated on grain substrates, making it commercially available.

## Mycoparasitic Fungi

Some fungi parasitize other fungi — a strategy called **mycoparasitism**.

**[[Trichoderma]]** spp. — *Trichoderma harzianum*, *T. viride*, *T. atroviride*, and relatives are aggressive mycoparasites and hyperparasites. They:
- Coil around and penetrate host hyphae using enzymatic dissolution of cell walls
- Produce antibiotics (trichodermin, gliotoxin, viridian) that suppress competitors
- Are used extensively as commercial biocontrol agents against soilborne pathogens (*Botrytis*, *Fusarium*, *Pythium*, *Rhizoctonia*)
- Also improve plant growth directly through hormone production and phosphate solubilization

## Mycoheterotrophic Plants

A remarkable ecological inversion: some plants parasitize mycorrhizal fungi rather than forming mutualistic associations with them.

**[[Monotropa uniflora]]** (ghost pipe / Indian pipe) — a completely non-photosynthetic plant (lacking chlorophyll; pure white) that obtains all carbon from [[Mycorrhizal Relationships|ectomycorrhizal fungi]] connected to nearby trees. The plant effectively "cheats" the forest's carbon-sharing network described in [[Common Mycorrhizal Networks]], inserting itself as an unreciprocating sink.

*Monotropa* parasitizes ECM fungi that are simultaneously in mutualistic association with *Tsuga*, *Pinus*, and other conifers — meaning the carbon it extracts ultimately originates from photosynthesis in nearby trees. This multi-trophic parasitism is called **epiparasitism** when the plant's host (the fungus) is itself a mutualist of another organism.

Other fully mycoheterotrophic plants include many orchid species and *Pyrola* spp.

## Ecological Significance

| Parasite Type | Primary Hosts | Ecological Role |
|--------------|--------------|-----------------|
| Rust fungi | Vascular plants (esp. grasses) | Population regulation of host plants; major agricultural impact |
| Powdery mildews | Broad plant range | Reduce plant fitness; drive plant immune evolution |
| Smut fungi | Grasses, sedges | Sterilize host plants; drive host evolution |
| Entomopathogenic fungi | Insects, arachnids | Natural insect population control; potential biocontrol |
| Mycoparasites (*Trichoderma*) | Other fungi | Regulate soil fungal community; biocontrol agents |
| Mycoheterotrophs | Mycorrhizal networks | Exploit forest carbon networks; represent extreme niche specialization |
