# *Amanita phalloides* (Death Cap)

> **DEADLY POISONOUS. Responsible for approximately 90% of all fatal mushroom poisonings worldwide. A single cap contains enough amatoxin to kill an adult human. There is no antidote. Do not touch, taste, or consume under any circumstances.**

## Identification

- **Cap:** 5–15 cm diameter; initially hemispherical, becoming broadly convex to nearly flat; surface typically **pale yellow-green to olive-green**, sometimes yellowish or near-white depending on light conditions and age; smooth, slightly viscid when wet; often faintly striate at the margin with age; color highly variable — some specimens are nearly white
- **Gills:** Pure white; free from stipe (not attached to stipe surface); crowded; do not change color at any stage of development
- **Spore print:** White
- **Spores:** Broadly ellipsoid to subglobose; 8–10 × 7–9 μm; amyloid (stain blue in Melzer's reagent — distinguishes from *Amanita muscaria* section)
- **Stipe:** 5–15 cm × 1–2 cm; white to pale; smooth to slightly fibrous; with a **prominent, pendulous, skirt-like white ring (annulus)** that persists and hangs freely; **base enclosed in a prominent white sac-like volva** (cup) — the volva is buried in the soil and must be fully excavated to confirm; stipe may be slightly greenish near top
- **Flesh:** White throughout; does not discolor or stain when cut or bruised
- **Smell:** Faint sweetish-honey aroma when young; occasionally described as slightly unpleasant in older specimens; nothing strongly warning
- **Taste:** Mild, inoffensive — no bitter or unpleasant taste (this is part of the danger)

**Key diagnostic combination:** Olive-green cap + white gills + white spore print + pendant ring + sac-like volva at base (must dig up). No single character is sufficient; all must be confirmed.

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (volva, annulus identification), [[Spore Prints]] (white spore print procedure), [[Dangerous Look-Alikes]], [[Foraging Safety]]*

## Biology

Obligate [[Mycorrhizal Relationships|ectomycorrhizal]] species — cannot be cultivated and does not grow without a compatible host tree. Forms symbiotic associations with oak (*Quercus*), beech (*Fagus*), hornbeam (*Carpinus*), chestnut (*Castanea*), pine (*Pinus*), and other tree genera. Fruiting typically summer through autumn (June–November in temperate zones); may fruit earlier or later depending on local climate.

**Invasive spread:** *A. phalloides* is native to Europe but has been introduced worldwide through the importation of tree saplings with European soil attached. It arrived in California via imported cork oaks and European horticulture in the early 20th century and is now firmly established throughout the California coast ranges and Pacific Northwest. It continues to expand its range. In Australia and New Zealand it was introduced with European oak and pine plantations. This global spread is an ongoing public health concern because it appears in areas where local populations have no cultural familiarity with it and may forage nearby edible species.

Fruiting bodies emerge singly or scattered; sometimes in arcs or rings where mycorrhizal mycelium fans outward. The entire fruiting body begins as a white egg encased in the universal veil — identical in appearance at this stage to puffballs or *Agaricus* buttons.

## Toxicology

**This is the most important section of this profile. Read carefully.**

### Toxins

*A. phalloides* contains two primary classes of cyclopeptide toxins:

**1. Amatoxins (primary lethal agents)**
- **α-Amanitin** (predominant): most toxic; ~0.2–0.4 mg per gram of fresh mushroom weight
- **β-Amanitin, γ-Amanitin, ε-Amanitin**: present in lesser concentrations; same mechanism
- Total amatoxin content: a single average cap (50 g fresh) contains approximately **10–12 mg α-amanitin** — the estimated lethal dose for an adult is **0.1 mg/kg body weight** (approximately 7 mg for a 70 kg person)
- **Mechanism:** Amatoxins are potent, selective inhibitors of **RNA polymerase II (RNAP II)** — the enzyme responsible for transcribing most protein-coding genes in eukaryotic cells. Inhibition halts messenger RNA synthesis, causing progressive cellular protein depletion and cell death. Tissues with highest replication/metabolic demand are most vulnerable: **liver (hepatocytes) and kidney (proximal tubule cells)**
- Heat-stable: cooking, drying, and freezing do not destroy amatoxins
- Tasteless and odorless: provide no sensory warning
- Not absorbed through skin contact; oral ingestion required

**2. Phallotoxins (contributory)**
- **Phalloidin** (predominant): binds to and stabilizes actin filaments in hepatocytes, disrupting cytoskeletal function; contributes to hepatotoxicity but is poorly absorbed in the gut, so its role in human poisoning is less clear than in animal studies
- Present in higher concentrations than amatoxins (~20 mg per cap) but considered secondary in human toxicity due to poor absorption

**3. Virotoxins**
- Minor cyclopeptides; structural relatives of phallotoxins; limited contribution to human toxicity

### Symptom Timeline

The **delayed onset is the most dangerous characteristic** of amatoxin poisoning. People feel fine for 6–24 hours (sometimes up to 36 hours) after eating a lethal dose, during which time they may eat more mushrooms, delay seeking help, and the toxins continue to be absorbed and cause irreversible cellular damage.

| Phase | Timing | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Latent phase** | 0–6 to 24 hours | **No symptoms** — complete apparent health |
| **Gastrointestinal phase** | 6–24 hours onset, peaks 24–36 hours | Sudden severe onset: profuse watery (cholera-like) diarrhea, violent vomiting, abdominal cramping, dehydration, electrolyte imbalance |
| **Apparent recovery** | ~48–72 hours | GI symptoms improve; patient appears to stabilize — **this is deceptive; cellular damage is occurring** |
| **Hepatorenal failure** | 4–8 days post-ingestion | Jaundice (icterus), coagulopathy (uncontrolled bleeding), hepatic encephalopathy (confusion, coma), acute kidney injury; multi-organ failure; death |

### Treatment

- **No specific antidote exists**
- Seek emergency medical care immediately if amatoxin poisoning is suspected — do not wait for symptoms
- US Poison Control: **1-800-222-1222**
- Gastric decontamination (lavage, activated charcoal) if presented within hours of ingestion
- Intensive supportive care: IV fluid replacement, electrolyte management, monitoring of liver and kidney function
- **Silibinin (milk thistle derivative):** administered IV; interrupts hepatocyte uptake of amatoxins via OATP transporters; available in Europe and as compassionate use in North America; most effective if given early
- **N-acetylcysteine (NAC):** used as hepatoprotective adjunct
- **Liver transplantation** may be required in severe cases with fulminant hepatic failure; timing is critical and prognosis for transplant patients is guarded
- Hemodialysis not effective at removing amatoxins

### Prognosis and Mortality

- Without treatment: historical mortality rate up to 10–30%; some sources cite higher rates in early 20th-century European outbreaks
- With modern critical care: estimated 5–10% case fatality rate; higher in elderly, young children, and those with pre-existing liver disease
- Children are disproportionately affected due to lower body weight (lower absolute lethal dose)
- Survivors may have permanent hepatic or renal impairment

## Confusion Risk

*A. phalloides* kills primarily because foragers mistake it for edible species. The following look-alikes are responsible for the majority of documented fatalities.

### Paddy Straw Mushroom (*Volvariella volvacea*)

Fatal misidentification is particularly documented in Southeast Asian immigrant communities who forage *Volvariella* (a familiar edible from their home countries) and mistake death caps for them. Both have a volva.

| Character | *A. phalloides* (DEADLY) | *Volvariella volvacea* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Gills | **White** at all stages | **Pink to salmon** when mature |
| Spore print | **White** | **Pink to salmon** |
| Ring | **Present** (pendant, white) | **Absent** |
| Volva | White, deep sac | Gray to brown sac |
| Habitat | Woodland (needs trees) | Straw, compost, rice paddies; no trees needed |

**Key test:** Spore print. Pink = *Volvariella*; white with woodland habitat = danger.

### Young Agaricus (Button Mushroom, Field Mushroom)

In the egg stage, *A. phalloides* is encased in a white universal veil and resembles *Agaricus* buttons (including commercially grown *A. bisporus*).

| Character | *A. phalloides* egg/button (DEADLY) | *Agaricus bisporus* / *A. campestris* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Gills (when exposed) | **White** at all stages | **Pink when young**, darkening to chocolate brown |
| Spore print | **White** | **Chocolate brown** |
| Volva | **Present** (must dig up base) | **Absent** |
| Habitat | Woodland only | Grassland, lawns, gardens; not woodland |
| Interior if sliced | Shows developing cap structure in white veil | Solid white homogeneous flesh |

### Green Russula (*Russula virescens* and allies)

Green-capped *Russula* species are edible and can superficially resemble the olive-green death cap.

| Character | *A. phalloides* (DEADLY) | *Russula virescens* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Volva | **Present** (buried; dig it up) | **Absent** — no volva |
| Ring | **Present** | **Absent** |
| Stipe | Flexible, fibrous | **Brittle** — breaks cleanly (all *Russula* are brittle) |
| Flesh | Does not crumble | **Crumbles readily** — chalky texture |

**Key test:** Dig up the full base and check for a volva. Press the stipe — if it's brittle and crumbles, it is *Russula* (no volva, no ring). If the stipe is flexible and has a sac at the base, do not eat it.

### Caesar's Mushroom (*Amanita caesarea*)

A prized edible *Amanita* of Mediterranean Europe. *A. caesarea* emerges from a similar white egg.

| Character | *A. phalloides* (DEADLY) | *Amanita caesarea* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Cap color | Olive-green, pale yellow-green | **Bright orange to scarlet red** |
| Gills | White | **Orange to yellow** |
| Spore print | White | White |
| Ring | White | **Yellow-orange** |
| Volva | White sac | **White egg-like sac** (similar — must check gill/cap color) |

**Note:** *A. caesarea* does not occur in North America. However, confusion with *A. phalloides* occurs in European foragers and recent immigrants from Mediterranean regions.
