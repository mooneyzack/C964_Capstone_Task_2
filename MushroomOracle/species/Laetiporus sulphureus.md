## Identification

- **Fruiting body:** Large overlapping brackets/shelves in fan-shaped clusters; individual shelves 5–25 cm across; entire cluster can reach 50+ cm and weigh several kilograms
- **Upper surface:** Bright sulphur-yellow to bright orange when fresh; fading to cream-white as it matures; smooth, slightly wavy
- **Pore surface:** White to pale sulphur-yellow; tiny pores (~4 per mm)
- **Flesh:** White; succulent and chicken-like in texture (hence the name) when young; becomes tough, dry, and crumbly with age
- **Spore print:** White; spores ovoid-ellipsoid, smooth, 5–7 × 3.5–5 μm

The bright sulphur-yellow to orange coloration of fresh specimens is highly distinctive and difficult to misidentify.

## Biology

**Taxonomy note:** A 2017 molecular study identified at least **17 distinct species** within the former *L. sulphureus* aggregate worldwide. What constitutes true *L. sulphureus* may be restricted to Europe or eastern North America. The *L. sulphureus* species complex includes:
- *L. cincinnatus* (North American; grows at ground level, often without a distinct host attachment)
- *L. gilbertsonii* (conifer specialist)
- *L. sulphureus* sensu stricto (European; hardwood specialist)

Saprotrophic; causes **[[Wood Decay|brown rot]]** (specifically brown heart rot) in heartwood of living and dead trees. Despite its bright color, it is a brown-rot fungus — it breaks down cellulose and hemicellulose while leaving lignin behind. The mycelium progressively destroys the structural integrity of the heartwood, which can eventually cause the tree to collapse.

Described by Bulliard; transferred to *Laetiporus* by Murrill in 1920.

## Cultivation

Rarely cultivated commercially; occasional cultivation on hardwood logs or stumps. Primarily wild-harvested. Cultivation attempts on sawdust blocks show limited results compared to other species.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **[[Beta-Glucans]]**: immunomodulatory polysaccharides
- **Laetiporic acids**: unique polyene compounds isolated from some specimens
- Various ergosterols
- Eburicoic acid and other triterpenoids

## Culinary

Edible when young and fresh; becomes tough and inedible with age. The young flesh is white, moist, and has a texture genuinely reminiscent of chicken breast — making it one of the best meat-substitute mushrooms.

**Cautions:**
- Some individuals experience gastrointestinal reactions (nausea, vomiting), particularly when collected from conifers, yew, or eucalyptus (the conifer-specialist species may contain different compounds)
- Specimens from yew (*Taxus*) should be avoided — may concentrate taxol-related compounds
- First-time eaters should test a small amount
- Always cook thoroughly — not for raw consumption

Best prepared: sautéed in butter, used as chicken substitute in curries, fried in breadcrumbs, or roasted. The flavor is mild and pleasant; it absorbs surrounding flavors well.
