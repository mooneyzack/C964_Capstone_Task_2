# *Podostroma cornu-damae* (Poison Fire Coral)

> **DEADLY POISONOUS. A rare but exceptionally dangerous East Asian fungus responsible for multiple fatalities in Japan and Korea. Contains trichothecene mycotoxins causing multi-organ failure. The bright red-orange color and finger-like shape make it visually striking and potentially tempting to those unfamiliar with it. Has been confused with edible fungi and traditional herbal preparations. If encountered in East Asia, do not touch or consume under any circumstances.**

## Identification

- **Fruiting body:** 3–10 cm tall; **bright red to orange-red or scarlet**; unbranched or sparingly branched, **finger-like or coral-like projections** (stipitate stroma) arising from a fleshy, pale base; the tips may be blunt or pointed; the color intensifies when fresh
- **Texture:** Firm to hard when fresh; somewhat waxy or resinous-looking surface; not soft or gelatinous
- **Base (stroma):** Pale white to pale yellow; the internal tissue is also pale; the red pigmentation is primarily on the outer surface
- **Spores:** Produced in perithecia (flask-shaped reproductive structures) embedded in the outer layer of the red stroma; ascospores; the detailed microscopy is required for confident genus-level ID
- **Size:** Typically compact; the entire fruiting body including base is 3–10 cm; conspicuous due to color rather than size
- **Smell:** Faint; not distinctive
- **Season:** Summer through early autumn in East Asian temperate forests
- **Ecology:** Found on dead or dying hardwood trees; appears to be either weakly parasitic or saprotrophic; rare — individual sightings are infrequent

**Visual alert:** The bright red color is highly distinctive and not shared by any common edible mushroom in the same habitat. However, its finger-like shape could superficially suggest *Cordyceps*-like fruiting bodies, Clavaria coral fungi, or bright-colored traditional herbal fungi to someone without mycological training.

## Biology

*Podostroma cornu-damae* is an ascomycete in the family Hypocreaceae — the same large family that contains *Trichoderma* (a widely used biocontrol agent) and *Hypocrea* species. Many members of Hypocreaceae produce biologically active secondary metabolites, and *Podostroma* is the extreme example of toxic secondary metabolite production.

The fruiting body (stroma) is a compact structure in which the spore-producing perithecia are embedded. This is typical of the Hypocreales order — the familiar *Cordyceps* (parasitic on insects) is in the same order, and the visual similarity between *Podostroma* and some *Cordyceps*-like fungi may contribute to confusion.

Genuinely rare in the wild; documented cases are concentrated in Japan, where it has received the most systematic attention following fatal poisonings. Sporadic records exist from Korea and China. Its full distribution is incompletely known.

## Toxicology

### Toxins

*Podostroma cornu-damae* contains **trichothecene mycotoxins**, specifically:
- **Satratoxin H**: a trichothecene type D macrocyclic compound; the primary toxic agent identified in *Podostroma*
- **Roridin E** and related trichothecenes: also detected; all act through the same mechanism
- **Additional secondary metabolites**: the full toxin profile is not completely characterized; research is ongoing

**Mechanism of trichothecene toxicity:**
- Trichothecenes are sesquiterpene epoxides that **inhibit protein synthesis** by binding to and inactivating the 60S ribosomal subunit — specifically interfering with peptide elongation and termination
- This mechanism is distinct from but somewhat analogous to amatoxin-mediated RNAP II inhibition: both ultimately halt protein synthesis in actively dividing cells
- Trichothecenes additionally cause **direct membrane damage** to rapidly proliferating cells (gut epithelium, bone marrow), produce **immunosuppression**, and are **dermally irritating**
- The macrocyclic trichothecenes (Type D, including satratoxin H) are significantly more toxic than the simple trichothecenes (Type A/B like deoxynivalenol from *Fusarium*)
- **Route of exposure:** Both ingestion and potentially dermal contact; handling the fruiting body is not definitively established as a toxicity route in humans, but trichothecenes are known to penetrate skin — caution is warranted with any handling

### Symptom Timeline and Clinical Presentation

*Podostroma* poisoning produces a **multi-system toxidrome** affecting the gastrointestinal tract, hematopoietic system (bone marrow and blood cell production), and potentially the central nervous system:

| Phase | Timing | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **GI phase** | Hours to 1–2 days | Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain |
| **Cutaneous effects** | Days 1–5 | Skin peeling (desquamation), erythema (redness), tingling sensation — may affect areas distant from ingestion |
| **Neurological** | Days 1–7 | Numbness, tingling of extremities (paresthesia); peripheral neuropathy; sometimes altered mental status |
| **Hematological failure** | Days 3–14 | **Pancytopenia** — severe reduction in white blood cells (leukopenia), platelets (thrombocytopenia), and red blood cells; bone marrow suppression; hemorrhagic manifestations |
| **Multi-organ** | Days 5–21 | Liver failure, kidney failure, respiratory distress; electrolyte abnormalities |
| **Death** | Days 5–21 in fatal cases | Multi-organ failure |

This pattern — GI onset followed by progressive hematopoietic failure with skin changes — is characteristic of macrocyclic trichothecene poisoning and resembles **acute radiation syndrome** in its effects on rapidly dividing tissues.

Documented Japanese fatality cases reported this progression: skin peeling, hair loss, dramatic leukopenia, followed by death from multi-organ failure in 1–3 weeks.

### Prognosis and Mortality

- Considered one of the most toxic mushrooms in East Asia
- Multiple documented fatalities from Japan; several cases in Korea
- Fatality rate appears high in cases with significant consumption
- Treatment is intensive supportive care: bone marrow support, transfusions, organ support
- **No specific antidote**; no established treatment protocol beyond supportive care
- Survivors may have prolonged convalescence

## Confusion Risk

*Podostroma cornu-damae* is rare; confusion scenarios are specific to East Asian contexts.

### Edible Cordyceps / *Ophiocordyceps* Species

*Cordyceps militaris* and related species are brightly colored (orange to red-orange) and finger-like — a significant visual overlap with *Podostroma*. *Cordyceps militaris* is edible and highly commercially valued in traditional Chinese medicine.

| Character | *P. cornu-damae* (DEADLY) | *Cordyceps militaris* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Color | **Brilliant scarlet to red** | Orange to orange-red (typically less intensely red) |
| Origin | Grows from dead wood (hardwood trees) | **Grows from insect pupae or larvae buried in soil** — the host insect is usually visible at the base |
| Base structure | Pale, fleshy stroma emerging from wood | Parasitized insect body at the base |
| Habitat | Decaying logs and stumps | Soil (where insect hosts are pupating) |
| Rarity | Rare; individual specimens | Can be locally abundant |

**Key test:** Examine the base. Legitimate *Cordyceps* fruiting bodies arise from a recognizable insect (caterpillar, pupa, or beetle) visible at the base. *Podostroma* arises from wood.

### Traditional Herbal Preparations and Dried Products

Some poisoning cases have involved dried or processed *Podostroma* material that was mistaken for a legitimate herbal preparation in East Asian traditional medicine contexts. The red color is associated with beneficial "red" fungi (e.g., *Ganoderma* — reishi). This underscores the importance of sourcing any traditional herbal fungal preparations from reputable, verified suppliers.

### Red Coral Fungi (Clavaria, Ramaria)

Coral fungi (*Clavaria*, *Ramaria* species) can be finger-like and come in bright colors including red-orange. These are saprotrophic and grow from soil or wood.

| Character | *P. cornu-damae* (DEADLY) | Red coral fungi (*Ramaria* / *Clavaria*) |
|---|---|---|
| Color | **Brilliant scarlet/red** | Variable; many are pale, white, yellow; red species exist but are less common |
| Texture | Firm, waxy | More fragile, brittle, or gelatinous |
| Branching | Minimal or unbranched finger-like | Often extensively branched coral-like structure |
| Geographic range | East Asia primarily | Worldwide |
