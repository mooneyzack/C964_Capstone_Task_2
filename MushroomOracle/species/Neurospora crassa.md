# *Neurospora crassa* (Red Bread Mold)

## Identification

*N. crassa* is a filamentous ascomycete mold — not a mushroom. Easily recognized in the wild:

- **Colony morphology**: Rapidly growing; bright **orange to salmon-pink** mycelium; prolific aerial hyphae give a cottony, fluffy appearance
- **Conidia**: Abundant orange macroconidia (multinucleate) and microconidia; spread readily by air
- **Perithecia**: Dark, flask-shaped sexual fruiting bodies; asci contain 8 ordered ascospores — the feature that made this organism famous in genetics
- **Growth rate**: Exceptionally fast — visible colonization within hours; a colony can cross a Petri dish in under 24 hours

## Biology

*N. crassa* is one of the most important model organisms in biology — the "fruit fly of the fungal kingdom." Its contributions to science include:

**One gene–one enzyme hypothesis (1941)**: George Beadle and Edward Tatum irradiated *N. crassa* conidia, screened for nutritional mutants, and demonstrated that each gene controls the production of a single enzyme. This work won the **1958 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** and established the molecular basis of genetics.

**Why *N. crassa* was ideal**: Haploid genome (mutations immediately expressed), ordered ascospores in linear asci (allowing direct meiotic analysis), fast growth, easy cultivation, and sexual reproduction on demand.

**Genome**: 43 Mb across 7 chromosomes; ~10,000 predicted genes. Fully sequenced in 2003 — the first filamentous fungal genome completed. Contains **repeat-induced point mutation (RIP)**, a defense mechanism that mutates duplicated DNA sequences, effectively preventing transposon accumulation.

**Circadian biology**: *N. crassa* is the primary model organism for eukaryotic circadian rhythms. The *frequency* (*frq*) gene and its feedback loop with WC-1 and WC-2 (white collar proteins) established core principles of biological clocks.

## Cultivation

### Traditional: Oncom (Indonesia)

*N. crassa* is traditionally used to ferment **oncom** — a tempeh-like food made from pressed soybean residue (okara), peanut press cake, or coconut press cake. Popular in West Java. The orange mycelium gives oncom its characteristic color.

### Industrial: Meati Foods

Meati uses *N. crassa* for commercial mycoprotein production:

- Spores inoculated into bioreactors with sugar/organic feedstocks
- Dense mycelium biomass forms within days
- Biomass pressed, shaped, and seasoned as whole-cut meat analogs (steaks, cutlets)
- Available in 7,000+ retail locations
- Meati and The Better Meat Co. are in an ongoing legal dispute over IP related to *N. crassa* food cultivation

### Research: Self-Healing Building Materials

Published in *Cell Reports Physical Science* (April 2025): *N. crassa* mycelium mineralized by *Sporosarcina pasteurii* bacteria created living building materials capable of **self-repair for over a month**. See [[Mycelium Construction]].

- **Oncom** (traditional): Sliced and fried; fermented flavor distinct from tempeh
- **Meati products** (modern): Whole-cut meat analogs — steaks, cutlets, ground — with fibrous texture from natural hyphal structure
