# Phellinus linteus (Meshima / Sang Hwang)

## Identification

- **Fruiting body:** Perennial bracket; hoof-shaped to shelf-like; **5–30 cm across**, thick and woody; grows up to several kilograms over decades
- **Upper surface:** Golden-brown to dark reddish-brown to black; concentrically zoned, rough and cracked with age; covered by a thin crust (cuticle)
- **Pore surface:** Golden-yellow to rust-brown; pores tiny (6–8 per mm), circular; pore surface is the primary identification feature
- **Flesh:** Cinnamon-brown to dark brown; woody and fibrous; hard
- **Hymenophore:** Multi-layered (perennial growth adds new tube layers each season — visible as horizontal striations in cross-section)
- **No stipe:** Sessile, attached directly to host wood

**Taxonomy note:** The name *Phellinus linteus* is applied to a species complex. East Asian populations on mulberry may include *P. linteus* sensu stricto and related species (*P. igniarius*, *P. baumii*). Many commercial Korean and Japanese products labeled "sanghwang" or "meshima" may represent different but closely related species. Molecular analysis is required for definitive identification.

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (bracket/polypore form, perennial growth rings), [[Habitat Guide]] (living hardwood hosts, mulberry)*

## Biology

*Phellinus linteus* is a **white-rot saprotroph and weak parasite** — it colonizes live or recently dead hardwood, causing progressive wood decay. It is among the longest-lived and slowest-growing of all cultivated medicinal fungi.

The generic name *Phellinus* derives from Greek for "cork" or "oak bark"; *linteus* means "linen-colored," referring to the pale pore surface of fresh specimens.

**Cultural significance:**
- **Korea (상황버섯, Sanghwang):** Designated a national treasure and medicinal asset; collection is regulated. Traditionally prescribed by Korean herbal medicine practitioners (hanbang) for liver disease, tumors, and as a tonic
- **Japan (メシマコブ, Meshima-kobu):** "Mushroom of Meshima island" — *Phellinus* specimens were historically collected from Meshima Island in the East China Sea, giving rise to the Japanese common name. The PG101 fraction (see below) was developed from Japanese research
- **China (桑黄, Sāng Huáng):** "Yellow of mulberry"; classical TCM applications include stopping bleeding, removing blood stasis, and treating abdominal masses

**Growth rate:** Extremely slow; wild brackets may require 10–20 years to reach significant size. Brackets are perennial, continuing to grow and add new pore layers each season.

*See also: [[Wood Decay]], [[Parasitic Fungi]], [[Traditional Medicine]]*

## Cultivation

Commercial cultivation is extremely difficult and largely unsuccessful. Key challenges:
- Very slow mycelial growth rate (weeks to months to colonize a substrate block)
- Requires specific mulberry wood substrates for authentic production
- Long fruiting-body development time (months to years)

**Current production methods:**
- **Mycelium biomass fermentation**: Submerged liquid fermentation of mycelium in stirred-tank bioreactors; produces mycelial biomass for extraction; much faster than fruiting body cultivation but chemically distinct from wild fruiting bodies
- **Log cultivation**: Mulberry log inoculation produces authentic fruiting bodies but requires 3–7+ years per harvest cycle
- **Sawdust bags**: Mulberry sawdust blocks can produce fruiting bodies but very slowly

**Wild harvest:** Most high-quality material remains wild-harvested; Korean wild sanghwang is one of the most valuable wild mushroom products in East Asia by weight.

*See also: [[Growing Methods]], [[Substrates]], [[Liquid Fermentation]]*

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Beta-glucans**: primarily β-1,3/1,6-linked glucans; immunomodulatory via Dectin-1/TLR4; activate NK cells, macrophages, and T-lymphocytes
- **Hispidin (and hispidin derivatives)**: polyphenolic bibenzyls; the signature compound — **hispidin is one of the most potent natural antioxidants known**, with DPPH radical scavenging superior to many commercial antioxidants; anticancer mechanisms include VEGF inhibition, induction of apoptosis via caspase-3 activation, and PKCβ inhibition
- **Hypholomine B / davallialactone**: styrylpyrone-type compounds; strong anticancer activity in vitro
- **PG101 (Phellinus linteus Glycoprotein 101)**: the most pharmacologically characterized fraction; a proteoglycan complex; demonstrated potent antitumor activity in multiple cancer cell lines and animal models; original patent held by Japanese researchers
- **Protocatechuic acid, caffeic acid**: phenolic acids; anti-inflammatory
- **Inositol, ergosterol**

### Species-Specific Health Research

**Anticancer:**
- Numerous in vitro studies demonstrate cytotoxicity against breast, colon, liver, lung, prostate, and leukemia cell lines
- Animal model studies show tumor suppression and immune potentiation (without direct cytotoxicity on cancer cells — via immune activation)
- PG101 fraction demonstrated significant antitumor effects in sarcoma and melanoma mouse models (Lee et al., 2004, *British Journal of Cancer*)
- Limited human clinical trials; most evidence is preclinical

**Immunomodulation:**
- Stimulates interferon-γ (IFN-γ), interleukin-12 (IL-12), and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α) production
- Enhances NK cell and macrophage cytotoxic activity
- T-cell proliferation enhancement in murine models

**Note on evidence:** Most research is in vitro or in animal models. Human clinical trials are scarce; this is a major limitation on the evidence base compared to species like *[[Ganoderma lucidum]]* or *[[Trametes versicolor]]*.

*See also: [[Beta-Glucans]], [[Phenolic Compounds]], [[Safety and Contraindications]]*

## Culinary

Not consumed as food. Extremely tough, woody texture and indigestible without processing. Consumed exclusively as a medicinal preparation:
- Hot water decoction (tea): simmered for 30–90 minutes
- Dual extraction (water + ethanol): captures both polysaccharides and hispidin/styrylpyrone phenolics
- Capsule or powder of dried mycelium or fruiting body extract

**Quality concern:** Declining wild populations and cultivation challenges mean adulteration is common. Many commercial "sanghwang" or "sang hwang" products contain other *Phellinus* or *Inonotus* species. DNA barcoding is the only reliable verification method.
