## Identification

**What is commonly called "chaga mushroom" is NOT the true fruiting body** — it is a **sclerotium or sterile mycelial conk**, a mass of mycelium erupting from the bark of infected trees.

**Sterile Conk (what is harvested as "chaga"):**
- An **irregular, knobbly, deeply fissured black mass** resembling a burnt piece of charcoal
- Dimensions: **0.5–1.5 m long, 10–15 cm thick**
- **Exterior:** Jet black, heavily cracked; this coloration results from a massive accumulation of **melanin** pigments
- **Interior:** Golden to rust-brown; cork-like texture

**True Fruiting Body (rarely seen):** Flat, resupinate (spreading over wood surface), golden-brown to tan; forms beneath the bark of dying/dead trees in the final stages of infection; rarely encountered because it only forms when the host tree dies.

The host tree is almost always a living birch (*Betula*) in boreal or temperate forest.

## Biology

Parasitic **[[Wood Decay|white-rot]]** fungus; causes **white heart rot** that spreads progressively through the heartwood of infected birch. The sclerotium develops over **5–10 years** before the tree dies. After tree death, the true fruiting body forms beneath the bark and the tree eventually collapses.

First described by Acharius ex Persoon; transferred to *Inonotus* by Pilát in 1942.

**Harvesting sustainability concern:** Wild chaga is harvested destructively — removing the sclerotium kills the remaining viable infection site. With slow development over 5–10 years and increasing commercial demand, sustainability is a genuine concern in boreal forests.

## Cultivation

Very difficult to cultivate commercially. The sclerotium develops over years in nature. Some laboratory protocols exist for mycelium cultivation on grain or liquid media, but the characteristic black melanin-rich conk cannot be easily reproduced in cultivation. Most commercial chaga is wild-harvested, primarily from Russia and Canada.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Melanin complexes**: high molecular weight pigments; strong antioxidant (higher ORAC than many plant-based antioxidants); responsible for the distinctive black exterior
- **Triterpenoids**: betulinic acid (from host birch bark — **uniquely accumulated by the fungus from the host**), inotodiol, ergosterol peroxide
- **Polysaccharides**: [[Beta-Glucans|beta-glucans]] (immunomodulatory)
- **Polyphenols**: catechols, flavonoids; anti-inflammatory
- **Minerals**: manganese (very high), zinc, copper, selenium

**Note on betulinic acid:** Chaga accumulates betulinic acid from its birch host — this compound has demonstrated anticancer activity in vitro and is being researched for HIV and cancer applications.

## Culinary

Consumed primarily as a hot water extract (decoction). Simmered for 15–60 minutes in hot water (not boiling) to extract water-soluble polysaccharides and melanins. The resulting tea is earthy, slightly bitter, and reminiscent of vanilla or coffee. Commercially available as:
- Chunks for decoction
- Powder (for tea or capsules)
- Dual-extract tinctures (water + alcohol)
- Instant tea preparations

Increasingly popular in wellness culture as a "superfood" coffee substitute.
