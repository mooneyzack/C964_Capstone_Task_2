## Identification

- **Cap (pileus):** 2–8 cm; convex to broadly umbonate; smooth; **characteristic water-spotted or marbled appearance** — the species epithet *tessellatus* (tesselated/tiled) refers to the mottled, water-stain-like patterns visible on the cap surface; color varies by variety: **brown (buna-shimeji)** or **white (bunapi-shimeji)**, the latter being an albino cultivar selected commercially
- **Gills:** White to cream; adnate to adnexed; crowded; not discoloring
- **Stipe:** 3–8 cm; 0.5–2 cm thick; central; white; firm; often growing in dense clusters with stipes fused at the base
- **Spore print:** White
- **Flesh:** White; firm; crunchy when raw
- **Smell and taste:** **Distinctly bitter when raw** — a critical identification and culinary note; sweetens to nutty and pleasant on cooking

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (tessellated cap pattern, white spore print, cluster habit), [[Spore Prints]] (white), [[Habitat Guide]] (high on beech trunks), [[Foraging Safety]] (bitter taste raw is distinctive)*

## Biology

Weakly parasitic and saprotrophic on hardwood, particularly *Fagus* (beech), *Acer* (maple), and elm. Unlike most wood-decay mushrooms that fruit from stumps or fallen logs, *H. tessellatus* often appears high up on living or recently dead standing trees — a distinctive field character. Produces a [[Wood Decay|white-rot]] decay pattern.

**Taxonomy:** The "shimeji" category in Japanese cuisine encompasses multiple species. *Hypsizygus tessellatus* (buna-shimeji) is the primary commercial shimeji. *H. marmoreus* (the white bunapi form) is now understood as a commercially selected albino cultivar rather than a separate species, though it is marketed as such. The species *Lyophyllum shimeji* is a separate mycorrhizal species also marketed as "shimeji" in Japan and is far rarer and more expensive.

**Nomenclature confusion:** In the commercial mushroom market, "shimeji" may refer to *H. tessellatus* (most commonly), *H. marmoreus* (albino cultivar), or occasionally *Lyophyllum shimeji* (true shimeji — mycorrhizal, cannot be cultivated). Buyers should note that the cultivated product labeled "shimeji" in Western supermarkets is universally *H. tessellatus* or its albino form.

**Fruiting ecology:** In nature, appears September–November in temperate zones; typically forms clusters of 5–30+ fruiting bodies. The tessellated cap markings are most pronounced in wild specimens grown in variable humidity conditions.

## Cultivation

Beech mushroom is one of the **major commercial cultivated mushrooms in Asia**, particularly Japan and Taiwan, with a highly developed industrial production system. Japan alone produces tens of thousands of metric tons annually, primarily in automated vertical farms.

**Commercial production method:** The dominant commercial system uses **small-format bottles (500–1000 ml)** in fully automated, climate-controlled rooms — a clean, efficient format distinct from the bag-based systems used for oyster mushrooms and shiitake. The small bottle format produces the characteristic tightly-clustered, uniform fruiting bodies seen in commercial packages.

**Substrate:** Hardwood sawdust (beech preferred; mixed hardwood acceptable) + rice bran or wheat bran 15–20%; water at 60–65% moisture content; pH 5.5–6.0. Sterilize at 121°C × 2–2.5 hours. See [[Substrates]].

**Spawn run:** 18–22°C; 30–60 days. Beech mushroom colonization is **slow** — one of the slower commercial species. The extended incubation period requires careful contamination management. Commercial producers use sterile automated inoculation systems for this reason.

**Cold treatment:** After full colonization, a cold shock of 4–10°C for 5–10 days is typically applied, followed by removal of the substrate surface layer ("scraping") to expose fresh mycelium. This surface preparation is essential for uniform pinning.

**Fruiting parameters:** 12–18°C; 90–95% relative humidity; CO2 control (<2000 ppm). Fruiting takes 2–3 weeks post-pinning. The cluster grows vertically in a bottle, producing the dense "bouquet" form sold commercially. See [[Environmental Parameters]].

**Home cultivation:** Feasible but patience-demanding due to slow spawn run (60+ days). Best in supplemented hardwood sawdust bags. Without contamination-control infrastructure, the long incubation window is the main challenge.

**White vs. brown forms:** The albino (bunapi/white shimeji) is slightly milder in flavor; both varieties have identical cultivation requirements.

## Bioactive Compounds

Beech mushroom has a well-developed research literature in Japan, commensurate with its commercial scale:

- **[[Beta-Glucans]]:** β-1,3/1,6-glucans; specific polysaccharide fractions designated ATSO and ATF from *H. tessellatus* have shown tumor inhibition (Sarcoma 180 mouse model) and macrophage activation
- **Hypsiziprenol:** A terpenoid geranylgeranyl derivative unique to *Hypsizygus* — under investigation for antiproliferative activity
- **Ergothioneine:** 0.5–2.0 mg/g DW depending on cultivation conditions and strain — moderate dietary source. See [[Ergothioneine]].
- **Lectins (HML — *Hypsizygus marmoreus* lectin):** Galactose-binding; documented cytotoxic and immunomodulatory properties in vitro; heat labile
- **Phenolic antioxidants:** Protocatechuic acid, p-coumaric acid; moderate DPPH radical scavenging activity

Research note: Several Japanese pharmaceutical studies from the 1990s–2000s documented antitumor activity of hot-water extracts in murine models. Clinical translation has not been fully established.

See [[Beta-Glucans]] for immunomodulatory mechanism.
See [[Ergothioneine]] for cardiovascular and neuroprotective context.

## Culinary

Beech mushroom's defining culinary characteristic is a **radical transformation on cooking** — bitter and astringent raw, but nutty, sweet, and pleasantly mild after heat treatment. This transformation is well-known in Japanese cuisine and makes beech mushroom strictly a cooked product.

**Raw taste:** Distinctly bitter — driven by the compound **fascaplysin-related alkaloids** and other secondary metabolites. The bitterness is not dangerous but is quite pronounced. Always cook. This is also a useful identification trait in the field.

**Cooked flavor profile:** Mild to moderate umami (2–3/5); nutty, slightly sweet, clean. The flavor is more delicate than shiitake or oyster; beech mushroom is valued as a complement that doesn't dominate a dish. See [[Flavor Profiles]].

**Key culinary attributes:**
- Stays firm and retains a pleasant bite throughout cooking — does not become mushy
- Low moisture content relative to oyster mushrooms — browning is easier
- The cluster form is visually appealing; many preparations feature the whole cluster

**Classic Japanese preparations:**
- **Shimeji gohan:** Rice cooked with beech mushrooms, dashi, soy, and mirin — a quintessential autumn rice dish
- **Shimeji nabe:** Hot pot with beech mushroom clusters, tofu, and vegetables in dashi
- **Sautéed with butter and soy:** Simple, direct, excellent
- **Miso soup:** Small clusters added to dashi-based miso soup (compatible with [[Pholiota nameko]] in the same pot)
- **Pasta:** Italian/Japanese fusion — clusters sautéed with garlic, white wine, cream; exceptional texture over linguine
- **Tempura:** Clusters dipped in light batter and deep-fried; delicate and crisp

**Western applications:** Pan-roasted at high heat (200°C+) until edges brown; stir-fry; added to creamy pasta sauces; used as a steak accompaniment. The cluster form adds visual drama to plated dishes.

**Pairings:** Dashi, soy, mirin, sake, miso, butter, garlic, thyme, cream, parmesan, white wine, chicken, seafood, tofu. Pairs with Pinot Gris, dry Riesling, and Chablis. See [[Mushroom Pairings]].

**Preservation:** Fresh clusters keep 1–2 weeks refrigerated in original packaging (the commercial sealed tray system is quite effective). Sauté and freeze; the texture holds better than most mushrooms after freezing. Not ideal for drying — texture becomes chewy and the nutty flavor diminishes. See [[Preservation Methods]].

**Safety:** Must be cooked — raw consumption causes bitterness and may cause gastrointestinal irritation. Cooked beech mushroom is safe and very well tolerated. See [[Safety and Contraindications]].
