## Identification

- **Cap (pileus):** 2–8 cm; convex to broadly convex; smooth; **amber to orange-brown to tawny-orange**; the defining feature is a **thick, transparent gelatinous coating** covering the entire cap and stipe — this mucilaginous layer is immediately distinctive and diagnostic
- **Gills:** Pale cinnamon-buff when young → rusty brown at maturity; adnate; crowded
- **Stipe:** 3–8 cm; 0.5–1.5 cm thick; fibrous; pale above ring, darker and also gelatinously coated below; **ring (annulus)** present but often gelatinous and evanescent, sometimes leaving only a ring-zone stain
- **Spore print:** Rusty brown to cinnamon-brown
- **Flesh:** Pale buff-yellow; firm; mild aroma
- **Smell and taste:** Mild, pleasant, slightly earthy; the gelatinous coating is odorless and tasteless but texturally distinctive

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (gelatinous cap coating — diagnostic), [[Spore Prints]] (rusty brown), [[Habitat Guide]] (beech and oak deadwood), [[Foraging Safety]] (rusty brown spore print distinguishes from toxic lookalikes)*

**The gelatinous coating is the species' most reliable identifier.** No other common edible species presents the combination of amber-orange coloring and thick, slippery, transparent slime coat in this genus. The coating is so distinctive that the Japanese name *nameko* (derived from a word meaning "slimy") refers directly to this property.

## Biology

Saprotrophic [[Wood Decay|white-rot]] decomposer, specializing in hardwood debris. In Japan, the natural substrate is primarily beech (*Fagus crenata* and *F. japonica*) and Japanese oak. Fruits in clusters, often forming large, showy groups of 10–50+ fruiting bodies on logs and stumps.

**Taxonomy:** Placed in *Pholiota* (Strophariaceae), though molecular studies have questioned whether the genus is monophyletic. *Pholiota nameko* is distinguished from other *Pholiota* species by its orange-amber color, consistent gelatinous coating, and association with hardwood in cool, montane environments. Many *Pholiota* species are considered inedible or toxic; *P. nameko* is the primary edible representative.

**Gelatinous coating chemistry:** The slime coat is composed primarily of polysaccharides — specifically a mixture of β-glucans and galactose-containing polysaccharides. This coating protects the developing fruiting body from desiccation and may deter some invertebrate grazers. The coating is entirely edible and is considered a culinary asset in Japanese cuisine, where its thickening properties are appreciated.

**Seasonal ecology:** A cool-season fruiter; in wild Japanese populations, appears September–November at 600–1400 m elevation, typically following autumn rains in montane beech-dominated forests. Populations have declined in some regions due to beech logging and land-use change.

## Cultivation

Nameko is one of Japan's **most commercially important cultivated mushrooms**, with annual production in the hundreds of thousands of metric tons. Its cultivation mirrors that of *[[Hypsizygus tessellatus]]* (beech mushroom) in using automated bottle systems in climate-controlled facilities, though nameko also suits bag cultivation.

**Substrate:** Hardwood sawdust (oak, beech, alder) supplemented with 15–20% rice bran or wheat bran; calcium carbonate 2–3% for pH buffering to 5.5–6.5. Sterilize at 121°C × 2–2.5 hours. See [[Substrates]].

**Spawn run:** 18–22°C; 30–50 days. Colonization speed is moderate; the mycelium is white and forms visible cord-like rhizomorphs. More contamination-sensitive than oyster mushrooms.

**Fruiting initiation:** Cold shock to 10–15°C; increase FAE; maintain 90–95% humidity. Nameko is a cool-preferring species — optimum fruiting temperature is **12–18°C**. Warmer conditions produce pale, misshapen fruiting bodies without proper coating development.

**Gelatinous coating development:** The characteristic slime coat develops during the fruiting phase and is most pronounced at correct temperature and humidity. Too warm = thin or absent coating; too dry = cracked, dessicated appearance. See [[Environmental Parameters]].

**Yield:** Biological efficiency (BE) of 50–80%; 2–3 flushes over 6–8 weeks.

**Bottle vs. bag:** Commercial production favors automated bottle systems (as with beech mushroom). Home growers typically use substrate bags. The bottle system produces the uniform, clustered product seen in Japanese supermarkets (typically sold as whole bag-of-clusters, including stipes).

**Harvesting:** Harvest when caps are convex to flat but before gelatinous coating begins to dissolve (which occurs as spores mature). Post-maturity nameko develops off-flavors and the slime coat becomes unpleasant.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **[[Beta-Glucans]]:** The gelatinous coat polysaccharides (primarily β-glucans with galactose components) have been specifically studied — distinct from the structural β-1,3/1,6-glucans found in the cell walls; both fractions show immunomodulatory activity
- **Namelaner:** A proprietary polysaccharide fraction from *P. nameko* with documented antitumor activity in murine models (Sarcoma 180, Ehrlich ascites carcinoma)
- **Ergothioneine:** Present at 1–2 mg/g DW — moderate dietary source. See [[Ergothioneine]].
- **Phenolic compounds:** Gallic acid, catechol; antioxidant activity in DPPH and ABTS assays
- **Lectin (PNA — *Pholiota nameko* agglutinin):** Galactose-binding lectin; antiproliferative activity in cancer cell line studies; heat labile (destroyed by cooking)
- **Fascaplysin-type alkaloids:** Trace amounts; possibly responsible for bitter notes in immature specimens

Research interest: The galactose-rich coating polysaccharides are structurally distinct from most mushroom polysaccharides and represent a potentially unique bioactive fraction. Japanese food science research on nameko polysaccharides dates to the 1970s.

See [[Beta-Glucans]] for broader immunomodulatory context.
See [[Ergothioneine]] for cardiovascular associations.

## Culinary

Nameko occupies a unique culinary niche driven entirely by its **gelatinous texture** — a feature that is either celebrated or avoided depending on cultural context. In Japanese cuisine, the slippery, thickening quality is considered a gastronomic virtue (*neba-neba* — the Japanese category of desirable slippery foods, alongside natto, okra, and yamaimo).

**The gelatinous coating:** The slime coat functions as a natural thickener in soups and sauces. When nameko is added to miso soup, the coating dissolves slightly into the broth, giving it a slightly velvety, thickened texture. This is not an accident but a valued culinary property. The coating is flavorless but fundamentally alters mouthfeel.

**Flavor profile:** Mild to moderate umami (3/5); earthy, slightly nutty, clean mushroom flavor. The gelatinous coat adds mouthfeel without adding strong flavor — nameko is a textural mushroom first and a flavor mushroom second. See [[Flavor Profiles]].

**Quintessential Japanese preparation — Nameko Miso Soup (*Nameko Jiru*):**
- Bring dashi (kombu + katsuobushi) to a simmer
- Add nameko mushrooms (whole or halved) — do not rinse aggressively; the coating is valuable
- Simmer 2–3 minutes
- Dissolve miso (white or mixed); add cubed silken tofu
- Serve immediately; the coating naturally thickens the soup slightly
This is the canonical preparation and one of Japan's most beloved everyday soups. See [[Cooking Techniques]].

**Additional Japanese preparations:**
- **Nameko oroshi:** Nameko with grated daikon (a fresh, lightly dressed preparation — a rare case where the raw mushroom character is partially present)
- **Nameko soba:** Topping for cold or hot soba noodles in dashi broth
- **Nameko don:** Over rice with a tare (dashi-soy-mirin reduction)
- **Nameko tofu nabe:** Hot pot with silken tofu, nameko, and enoki in light dashi

**Western applications:** Less established but effective — add to ramen, udon, and other broth-based soups as a thickener and umami source; incorporate into cream sauces (the coating integrates smoothly); use whole as a garnish for braised dishes.

**Handling:** Minimal washing recommended — a gentle rinse is sufficient; aggressive washing removes the coating. The coating is the species' most valued attribute; treat it accordingly.

**Pairings:** Dashi, miso (especially shiro/white miso), soy sauce, mirin, tofu, silken tofu, daikon, ginger, sake, soba, udon. See [[Mushroom Pairings]].

**Preservation:** Fresh nameko keeps 5–7 days refrigerated. Freezing is acceptable — the coating becomes more pronounced post-freeze. Nameko is commonly sold canned in Japan (in water or light brine) — the canned version is shelf-stable and widely available. Drying is not traditional and significantly alters the texture character. See [[Preservation Methods]].

**Safety:** Fully edible when cooked. *Pholiota* genus contains inedible and mildly toxic species — identification is important for wild harvest. Cultivated nameko is unambiguous. Always cook; raw consumption may cause mild gastrointestinal irritation. See [[Safety and Contraindications]].
