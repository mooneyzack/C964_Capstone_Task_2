# *Amanita bisporigera* (Destroying Angel)

> **DEADLY POISONOUS. Contains the same lethal amatoxins as the Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*) in identical concentrations. Pure white and entirely innocuous in appearance — the most deceptively dangerous mushroom in North America. There is no antidote. A single cap can be lethal.**

## Identification

- **Cap:** 5–12 cm diameter; pure white throughout; initially ovoid to hemispherical, becoming convex to flat; surface smooth, dry to slightly viscid; margin not striate; entirely white with no green, yellow, or other coloration (unlike *A. phalloides*)
- **Gills:** Pure white; free from stipe; crowded; do not change color at any stage of development — remain white from button stage through full maturity and decay
- **Spore print:** White
- **Spores:** Broadly ellipsoid to globose; 7–10 × 6–8 μm; amyloid (stain blue-gray in Melzer's reagent)
- **Stipe:** 7–15 cm × 0.8–1.5 cm; pure white; smooth to slightly fibrous; with a **fragile, white, skirt-like ring (annulus)** that may disappear with age or rain; **base enclosed in a prominent white sac-like volva** — buried in the soil; must be fully excavated to confirm
- **Flesh:** White throughout; does not stain or discolor when cut or bruised
- **Smell:** Unpleasant, faintly sweet-sickly in some specimens, but not reliably distinctive — cannot be used for identification
- **Taste:** Mild, inoffensive — no warning of toxicity

**Name origin:** *bisporigera* = "two-spored bearer"; the basidia typically bear only 2 spores rather than the usual 4 — a microscopic character.

**Destroying angel complex:** The common name covers several closely related all-white deadly *Amanita* species:
- *A. bisporigera*: Eastern North America (most common eastern US/Canada)
- *A. ocreata*: Western North America (Pacific states)
- *A. virosa*: Europe (the primary "destroying angel" in European mycology)
- *A. verna*: Europe (spring destroying angel; fruits earlier in the season)
All are equally deadly; distinguish by geographic range and subtle microscopic/morphological differences.

## Biology

Obligate [[Mycorrhizal Relationships|ectomycorrhizal]] — cannot grow without host trees. Associates with oaks (*Quercus*), beeches (*Fagus*), hemlocks (*Tsuga*), pines (*Pinus*), and other forest trees. Fruits from summer through autumn (July–October in eastern North America); *A. verna* fruits in spring (April–June) in Europe.

Fruiting bodies emerge singly or scattered across the forest floor, typically near trees. The entire young specimen is enclosed in a white universal veil — the egg stage — which is indistinguishable from a puffball or large *Agaricus* button to the untrained eye. As the cap expands, the universal veil ruptures, leaving the ring on the stipe and the volva at the base.

The species is remarkably widespread and not uncommon in eastern North American forests. It is encountered regularly wherever oaks and other hardwoods grow.

## Toxicology

### Toxins

*A. bisporigera* contains **amatoxins** at concentrations essentially equivalent to *Amanita phalloides*:
- **α-Amanitin** (primary lethal agent): inhibits **RNA polymerase II** — the enzyme responsible for transcribing protein-coding genes in all eukaryotic cells; this halts protein synthesis in the highest-turnover tissues (liver, kidney), causing progressive cell death
- Estimated α-amanitin content: ~1.5–2.5 mg per gram of dried mushroom weight; a single cap of average size contains **well above the estimated lethal dose** for an adult
- Amatoxins are **heat-stable** (cooking does not destroy them), **tasteless**, and **odorless** — they provide no sensory warning of their presence
- Also contains phallotoxins (phalloidin) contributing additional hepatotoxicity

### Symptom Timeline

Identical to *Amanita phalloides*. The defining characteristic is the long latent phase — victims feel completely normal for many hours while lethal cellular damage accumulates.

| Phase | Timing | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Latent phase** | 0 to 6–24 hours | **No symptoms** — complete apparent health; victims may eat more, delay treatment |
| **Gastrointestinal phase** | 6–24 hours onset | Sudden, severe: profuse watery diarrhea, violent vomiting, abdominal cramps, dehydration |
| **Apparent recovery** | ~48–72 hours | GI symptoms ease; patient appears to improve — this is a false recovery |
| **Hepatorenal failure** | 4–8 days | Jaundice, coagulopathy, hepatic encephalopathy, acute kidney injury, multi-organ failure, death |

**Critical note:** The 6–24 hour delay is the key diagnostic indicator of amatoxin poisoning. Mushroom poisoning that produces GI symptoms within 2 hours is rarely amatoxin-mediated. Delayed onset (beyond 6 hours) is a medical emergency.

### Treatment

- **No specific antidote**
- Immediate emergency medical care is mandatory; do not wait for symptoms to progress
- US Poison Control: **1-800-222-1222**
- Supportive care: IV fluids, electrolyte management, liver and kidney monitoring
- **Silibinin (IV)**: most evidence-based specific agent; blocks hepatocyte amatoxin uptake; must be given early
- **N-acetylcysteine (NAC)**: adjunctive hepatoprotection
- **Liver transplant** in fulminant hepatic failure cases; survival rate with transplant is significantly better than without in the most severe cases
- Children are at higher risk due to lower body mass; half-cap consumption can be lethal in a child

### Prognosis and Mortality

- North America's primary cause of fatal mushroom poisoning
- Estimated fatality rate with modern intensive care: 5–10% in adults; higher in children and elderly
- Survivors may have lasting hepatic or renal impairment
- Late presentation (>36 hours) worsens prognosis substantially

## Confusion Risk

The destroying angel kills because it is completely white, unremarkable, and easily confused with multiple common edible mushrooms. Unlike the death cap's distinctive olive-green color, there are no color cues — white is among the most "safe-looking" colors to an untrained forager.

### Button Mushroom / Field Mushroom (*Agaricus bisporus*, *A. campestris*)

The most frequent confusion scenario in North America. *Agaricus* species grow in grassy areas and are often found near woodland edges — close to destroying angel habitat.

| Character | *A. bisporigera* (DEADLY) | *Agaricus campestris* / *A. bisporus* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Gills | **White at ALL stages** | **Pink when young** — always pink before maturity |
| Spore print | **White** | **Brown to chocolate** |
| Volva | **Present** — sac at base (must dig up) | **Absent** — no sac |
| Ring | Present; fragile, white | Present; often more substantial |
| Habitat | **Near trees** (ectomycorrhizal) | Grassland, lawns, fields — **not typically under trees** |

**Safety rule:** Any white-gilled, white-spored mushroom near trees is suspect. Dig up the full base before any other consideration. If there is a sac-like volva, do not eat.

### Meadow Mushroom (*Agaricus campestris*)

Same distinguishing characters as above. *A. campestris* additionally has a pleasant mushroom smell; the destroying angel's odor is faint and may be slightly unpleasant.

### Puffballs (*Calvatia*, *Lycoperdon*, *Calbovista* spp.)

In the button/egg stage, *A. bisporigera* is fully enclosed in a white universal veil and is visually indistinguishable from small puffballs on the soil surface.

| Character | *A. bisporigera* egg stage (DEADLY) | True puffball (edible when all-white inside) |
|---|---|---|
| Interior when sliced vertically | Reveals internal cap, gills, and stipe structure within the white egg — a "ghost mushroom" outline | **Homogeneous white flesh throughout** — no internal structure |
| Shape | Oval to elongated | Usually more spherical; may have a narrow base |
| Attachment | Often attached to thin cord of mycelium | Similarly attached |

**Rule that must never be broken:** Always slice every puffball-like object from top to bottom before eating. If you see any internal structure (an outline of a developing cap, hint of gills, or any differentiated zones), it is not a puffball — do not eat it.

### Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*)

Both are deadly. The distinguishing character is cap color: *A. phalloides* is olive-green to yellowish; *A. bisporigera* is pure white. Both have the same ring, volva, white gills, and white spore print. Toxicity is identical.
