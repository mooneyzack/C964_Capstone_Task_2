# *Aspergillus oryzae* (Koji Mold)

## Identification

*A. oryzae* is a filamentous ascomycete — a mold, not a mushroom. It does not produce macroscopic fruiting bodies. In culture:

- **Colony morphology**: Rapidly growing; olive-green to yellow-green conidia (asexual spores) on long conidiophores with radiate heads
- **Microscopy**: Conidiophores 1–5 mm tall; vesicles globose to subglobose (40–80 µm); uniseriate or biseriate; conidia smooth to finely roughened, globose, 5–8 µm
- **Distinction from *A. flavus***: Morphologically nearly identical. Reliably distinguished only by genomic analysis — *A. oryzae* has lost aflatoxin biosynthetic capability through domestication

## Biology

*A. oryzae* evolved from the toxigenic species *Aspergillus flavus* through human selection of **atoxigenic lineages** over thousands of years. Genomic analysis (Lee et al., 2019, *DNA Research*) confirmed that domestication selected for strains that ceased producing aflatoxins while retaining and enhancing enzyme secretion capacity. The organism is effectively a domesticated fungus — analogous to how dogs were domesticated from wolves.

**Genome**: 37 Mb, 12,074 predicted protein-coding genes (strain RIB40, sequenced 2005 via whole-genome shotgun). The genome encodes an extraordinary secretory capacity:

- **65 endopeptidases** and **69 exopeptidases**
- Multiple amylases (α-amylase, glucoamylase)
- Lipases (first commercial lipase source, 1988)
- Cell wall-bound **glutaminase** — critical for umami production

**Regulatory status**: FDA GRAS (Generally Recognized As Safe), WHO endorsed. In 2006, the Brewing Society of Japan designated it the **national fungus** (*kokkin*) of Japan.

## Cultivation

*A. oryzae* is cultivated industrially on steamed grain (rice, barley, wheat) or legumes (soybeans). The inoculated substrate is called **koji**. Standard parameters:

- **Temperature**: 28–35°C during koji production
- **Humidity**: High (>80% RH)
- **Duration**: 40–50 hours for rice koji
- **Key control variable**: Temperature management is critical — the mold's metabolic heat can overshoot and kill the culture if not managed (frequent turning/mixing in traditional production)

## Bioactive Compounds

*A. oryzae* is valued not for compounds it contains but for compounds it **produces** during fermentation:

| Enzyme | Function | Application |
|--------|----------|-------------|
| α-amylase, glucoamylase | Starch → glucose | Sake, amazake, rice vinegar |
| Neutral/alkaline proteases | Protein → amino acids | Soy sauce, miso |
| Leucine aminopeptidase | Liberates glutamic acid from peptides | Umami development |
| Cell wall-bound glutaminase | L-glutamine → L-glutamate | Primary umami compound in soy sauce |
| Lipases | Fat hydrolysis | Flavor development; industrial detergents |

**Enzyme activities**: Up to 200 U/g dry weight (amylase) and 84.38 U/g dry weight (protease) on soybean koji.

See [[Umami Science]] for the receptor-level mechanism of glutamate perception.

## Culinary

*A. oryzae* is not eaten directly but is the essential organism behind:

- **Soy sauce** (*shoyu*) — koji grown on wheat/soybean drives moromi fermentation
- **Miso** — koji mixed with soybeans, salt; fermented weeks to years
- **Sake** — rice koji provides amylase for parallel saccharification/fermentation
- **Mirin** — sweet rice wine from glutinous rice and koji
- **Amazake** — sweet rice drink from koji amylase alone
- **Koji-aged meats** — proteases tenderize and develop umami in 48 hours (Western application)
- **Koji charcuterie** — accelerated curing via shio koji

See [[Koji Fermentation]] for comprehensive coverage of traditional and Western applications.

## Industrial Applications

Beyond food, *A. oryzae* is one of the most important industrial microorganisms:

- **Enzyme production**: Commercial source of amylases, proteases, lipases for food processing, textiles, detergents
- **Heterologous protein expression**: Used as a host for producing recombinant proteins due to its powerful secretory system
- **Organic acid production**: Citric acid, gluconic acid
