# *Chlorophyllum molybdites* (Green-Spored Parasol)

> **POISONOUS. The single most commonly consumed poisonous mushroom in North America by number of cases. Causes rapid-onset, severe gastrointestinal illness. Grows in lawns and public green spaces, making it highly accessible to curious non-foragers and children. The GREEN spore print is absolutely diagnostic and unlike any common edible look-alike — always take a spore print before eating any large lawn parasol.**

## Identification

- **Cap:** 8–30 cm diameter; broadly convex when young (resembling a drum major's hat), becoming flat with a central raised umbo when mature; surface pale white to pale buff; covered with **brownish scales** — a central brown disc remains while the outer surface cracks into fibrous scales as the cap expands; the scales on a young, unexpanded cap look like a smooth brown button
- **Gills:** **White when young** — this is misleading; they gradually turn **gray-green to distinctly green** as spores mature; color change is progressive and may be subtle in very young or very fresh specimens; in a mature cap, the greenish tinge is usually clear
- **Spore print:** **GREEN** — this is the diagnostic character; greenish-gray to distinctly olive-green; this is unique among common large mushrooms; no edible look-alike produces a green print; **always take a spore print on any parasol-type mushroom before consuming**
- **Spores:** Ovoid to ellipsoid; **dextrinoid** (turn reddish-brown in Melzer's reagent); thick-walled; with a distinct apical germ pore; 9–12 × 6.5–8 μm
- **Stipe:** 10–25 cm × 1–2 cm; white to pale; smooth to slightly fibrous; with a **double ring** that is moveable up and down the stipe in *some* specimens (more characteristic of *Macrolepiota*; the ring of *C. molybdites* is typically single and less moveable); no volva; **base slightly bulbous**
- **Flesh:** White; does not stain significantly when cut (may very faintly stain yellowish to orange-red, but this is variable)
- **Smell:** Unpleasant, sometimes mildly chemical or fishy in mature specimens
- **Taste:** Mild when raw — provides no reliable warning

**Key field note:** Young, unexpanded specimens appear as large white-brown buttons in the lawn. Do not assume a large lawn parasol is edible. The green gill color and green spore print are the definitive tests.

## Biology

*C. molybdites* is a **saprotrophic** species — it decomposes organic matter in soil, particularly in lawns, parks, golf courses, and other managed grasslands. It is strongly associated with **warm temperate to subtropical climates** and is especially abundant in the southeastern United States, Gulf Coast states, Texas, California, Hawaii, and throughout the tropics.

The species frequently forms **fairy rings** — arcs or complete circles of fruiting bodies — as the underground mycelium radiates outward from a central point across seasons. These rings can be very large (several meters in diameter) and fruit prolifically after warm summer rains.

Its occurrence in lawns, public parks, and golf courses places it in high-visibility locations where it is found by people who do not forage intentionally, including children who may pick and taste mushrooms out of curiosity. This contributes to its status as the most frequently reported mushroom poisoning species in North America.

It is **not cold-tolerant**: primarily fruits in late spring through autumn in warm climates; year-round in tropical regions.

Related species in the *Chlorophyllum* genus include *C. brunneum* and *C. rachodes* (shaggy parasols) — both have brown shaggy scales and may cause mild GI upset; both also lack a green spore print (white prints), distinguishing them from *C. molybdites*.

## Toxicology

### Toxins

The precise toxic compounds responsible for *C. molybdites* poisoning have not been fully characterized — this is an unusual gap in mycotoxicology for such a common poisoning species:
- Candidate toxins include **thermolabile proteins** (denatured by thorough cooking in some experiments, but cooking does not reliably prevent poisoning) and possibly uncharacterized **lectins or peptides**
- Toxin(s) cause **direct GI irritation and mucosal inflammation** in the gastrointestinal tract
- **No amatoxins, gyromitrin, orellanine, or other well-characterized deadly toxins** are present — the toxicity is GI-mediated, not systemic organ failure
- Individual sensitivity varies considerably: some people consume this mushroom without obvious effect; others are severely ill after small amounts; children are often more severely affected than adults

### Symptom Timeline

**Rapid onset** — typically 1–3 hours after ingestion:

| Phase | Timing | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Onset** | **1–3 hours** post-ingestion | Nausea, cramping |
| **Peak GI phase** | 1–6 hours | **Explosive vomiting and profuse diarrhea** — often described as extremely violent; abdominal cramping; sweating; dizziness; weakness |
| **Resolution** | 6–24 hours in most cases | Gradual improvement; residual weakness |

**Severity:** Can range from mild (a few hours of nausea) to severe (dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, hospitalization). Rarely fatal in otherwise healthy adults. **Children are at higher risk** for serious dehydration from the volume and violence of GI fluid loss.

Cooking may reduce toxicity in some cases but has not prevented poisoning reliably in documented outbreak reports.

### Treatment

- Supportive care: IV fluid and electrolyte replacement for significant GI losses
- Antiemetics
- Monitor children carefully for dehydration
- US Poison Control: **1-800-222-1222**
- Full recovery expected within 24 hours in most cases; some patients take 2–3 days

## Confusion Risk

### Parasol Mushroom (*Macrolepiota procera*)

The primary confusion species. Both are large, scaly, parasol-shaped mushrooms. *M. procera* is a prized edible; *C. molybdites* is toxic. They may grow in similar habitats.

| Character | *C. molybdites* (POISONOUS) | *Macrolepiota procera* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Spore print | **GREEN** | **White** |
| Gills | White when young; **turn greenish** | White throughout; **never turn green** |
| Stipe pattern | Smooth to slightly fibrous; no prominent snakeskin | **Distinctive snakeskin/zigzag brown mottled pattern** on stipe |
| Ring | Single, may not be moveable | **Double ring; moveable up and down the stipe** |
| Flesh bruising | Faint yellow-orange to no change | **Slowly bruises orange-red** when cut |
| Habitat | **Lawns, parks, golf courses** — highly managed grass | Woodland edges, pastures, hedgerows — less on manicured turf |
| Geography | Abundant in warm/subtropical regions; southeastern US | More common in temperate Europe; less common in southeastern US |

**Absolute rule:** Take a spore print on any large parasol. Green = *C. molybdites* = poisonous. No edible large parasol produces a green spore print.

### Shaggy Parasol (*Chlorophyllum rachodes*, formerly *Lepiota rachodes*)

*C. rachodes* has a brown shaggy cap (more dramatically shaggy than either *C. molybdites* or *M. procera*), a white spore print, and may cause mild GI upset in some individuals.

| Character | *C. molybdites* (POISONOUS) | *C. rachodes* (edible with caution) |
|---|---|---|
| Spore print | **GREEN** | **White** |
| Gills | Turn green | Remain white |
| Cap scales | Brown fibrous scales on pale background | More dramatic, shaggy, prominent brown scales |
| Flesh bruising | Minimal | **Strong orange to saffron bruising** when cut |

### Amanita Species (Button Stage)

Young *C. molybdites* buttons in the lawn can superficially resemble *Amanita* egg stages. Both have white-to-brown exterior appearance. Distinguishing: *Amanita* has a volva (sac at base, buried); *C. molybdites* does not. Spore print: *Amanita* is white; *C. molybdites* is green.
