# Tremella fuciformis (Snow Fungus / Silver Ear)

## Identification

- **Fruiting body:** Gelatinous, brain-like or frond-like lobes; **5–15 cm across**; highly translucent and irregular, resembling a white jelly flower or crinkled snow
- **Color:** White to pale cream when fresh; yellowish when dried; turns almost transparent after rehydration
- **Texture:** Rubbery, gelatinous, and slippery when hydrated; brittle and paper-thin when dried
- **Spore print:** White to pale yellow; basidiospores broadly ellipsoid, 6–8 × 5–7 μm; basidia are tremelloid (with longitudinal or oblique septa)
- **Substrate:** Found on dead hardwood branches, logs, and stumps; almost always associated with an underlying *Annulohypoxylon* (or formerly *Hypoxylon*) species, on which it parasitizes

**Key distinguishing features:** The translucent white jelly fronds, combined with the hardwood substrate and association with black *Annulohypoxylon* zones, are diagnostic. No other common jelly fungus produces this shape in white.

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (jelly fungi, gelatinous texture), [[Habitat Guide]] (hardwood deadwood, tropical substrates)*

## Biology

*Tremella fuciformis* is technically a **mycoparasite** — it does not digest wood directly but instead parasitizes the mycelium of *Annulohypoxylon* (family Xylariaceae) species that are colonizing the same substrate. This obligate relationship is the central challenge in cultivation.

First described by Berkeley in 1856 from specimens collected in Cuba. The species name *fuciformis* means "seaweed-shaped," describing its irregular frond-like lobes.

**Class Tremellomycetes:** Unlike the vast majority of edible fungi (class Agaricomycetes), *T. fuciformis* belongs to Tremellomycetes — a class of mostly jelly fungi with tremelloid basidia. Other members include *Tremella mesenterica* (Yellow Brain Fungus).

**Cultural history:** In China, *T. fuciformis* has been prized for at least 2,000 years. It appears in the 25-volume *Compendium of Materia Medica* (本草綱目, Bencao Gangmu) by Li Shizhen (1596 AD) as a beautifying tonic. Historical records suggest it was so valued in the Tang Dynasty that it was reserved for imperial use.

## Cultivation

Cultivation of *T. fuciformis* requires **co-inoculation** with its host *Annulohypoxylon* species — a key distinction from most cultivated mushrooms. The standard protocol:

1. Substrate: cottonseed hulls, hardwood sawdust, or synthetic logs inoculated with both *T. fuciformis* spawn and *Annulohypoxylon* spawn
2. Colonization: ~25–30°C; 70–80% RH; 30–60 days
3. Fruiting: induced by raising humidity to 90–95% and maintaining 20–28°C
4. Harvest: 3–5 flushes per substrate block typically achieved

China produces approximately **100,000 metric tonnes** of dried snow fungus annually, making it one of the world's highest-volume cultivated mushrooms. Fujian Province is the primary production center.

**Home cultivation** is challenging due to the dual-culture requirement; many commercial kits use pre-inoculated substrate blocks with the companion species already established.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Tremella polysaccharides (TPS)**: primarily glucuronoxylomannan (GXM) and acidic heteropolysaccharides; MW ranging from 0.5–5 MDa; remarkable **water-holding capacity** — TPS can retain up to 500× its weight in water, surpassing hyaluronic acid (~1,000×) in some studies; β-1,3/1,6 glucan backbone with xylose, mannose, and glucuronic acid side chains
- **Immunomodulatory polysaccharides**: stimulate dendritic cells, macrophages, and NK cells via Dectin-1 and TLR pathways
- **Triterpenoids**: lanostane-type triterpenes; anti-inflammatory
- **Ergosterol**: vitamin D₂ precursor
- **Phenolic compounds**: antioxidant capacity

**Cosmetics industry application:** TPS is increasingly used as a cosmetic active ingredient, marketed as a natural competitor to hyaluronic acid. A 2019 study in *Carbohydrate Polymers* confirmed TPS film-forming ability and skin-penetration depth superior to standard HA preparations in ex vivo models.

**Neurological research:** A 2019 study (*Neural Regeneration Research*) found TPS enhanced neurogenesis and cognitive function in aging mouse models, with potential relevance to neurodegenerative conditions. The mechanism appears to involve BDNF upregulation.

**Hypoglycemic effects:** Multiple animal studies demonstrate TPS reduces fasting blood glucose and improves insulin sensitivity through AMPK-pathway modulation.

*See also: [[Beta-Glucans]], [[Terpenoids and Triterpenoids]], [[Polysaccharides and Immunity]]*

## Culinary

Snow fungus is a **culinary staple in Chinese cuisine**, valued for its texture and mild sweetness rather than a strong flavor — the gelatinous, slightly crunchy texture is the primary culinary attribute.

**Classic preparations:**
- **Tremella soup (银耳汤, Yín'ěr tāng):** The quintessential dessert preparation — snow fungus simmered with rock sugar, red dates (jujube), lotus seeds, goji berries, and sometimes lily bulb; consumed hot or chilled; a traditional beauty and longevity food
- **Eight Treasure Porridge (八宝粥):** Snow fungus is one of the eight "treasures" combined with various grains, beans, dates, and nuts in a sweet congee
- **Hot-and-sour soup:** The gelatinous texture works in savory applications too — snow fungus adds body and texture to soups
- **Cold salads (凉拌):** Rehydrated snow fungus dressed with sesame oil, vinegar, and chili

**Preparation:** Dried snow fungus must be soaked in cold water for 30–60 minutes until fully rehydrated and expanded (it expands 4–6×). Tough yellowish base pieces are trimmed before use. Over-cooking produces an unpleasant slimy texture; brief cooking (15–20 minutes) preserves the characteristic crunch.

**Nutritional note:** Low in calories; contains significant dietary fiber (primarily from the polysaccharides); protein approximately 6–10% of dry weight.
