# Antrodia camphorata (Niu-Chang-Chih / Stout Camphor Fungus)

## Identification

- **Fruiting body:** Highly variable depending on substrate orientation; sessile, flat resupinate to shelf-like brackets; **2–20 cm** in various dimensions; can be circular, irregular, or shelf-like
- **Color:** Variable — red, reddish-orange to yellowish-orange when young and fresh; fades to pale cream, yellow, or tan with age
- **Pore surface:** White to cream; pores 2–3 per mm; angular to irregular; pore surface easily distinguishes it from lookalikes
- **Texture:** Fresh specimens are moderately soft; dried material is extremely hard and woody
- **Odor:** Distinctive camphor-like aromatic smell — from the sesquiterpene compounds of both host and fungus; this is a key identification feature
- **Substrate:** EXCLUSIVELY inside the hollow heartwood of *Cinnamomum kanehirae* — never found on any other tree species

**Conservation status:** Both the fungus and its sole host are under extreme pressure. Wild *Antrodia camphorata* is legally protected in Taiwan; collection without permit is prohibited. Specimens that do appear in trade are either cultivated or illegally harvested.

## Biology

*Antrodia camphorata* is a **white-rot saprotrophic** fungus and one of the most extreme examples of host specificity in the fungal kingdom — it grows exclusively inside the hollowed heartwood cavity of *Cinnamomum kanehirae* (牛樟, Niu-chang), a tree endemic to Taiwan's mountain forests.

**Host tree:** *Cinnamomum kanehirae* (Hayata) Hayata is a large evergreen tree in the laurel family (Lauraceae) endemic to Taiwan; classified as Vulnerable by IUCN. The tree's heartwood contains highly toxic sesquiterpenes and monoterpenes that deter most organisms, making *A. camphorata*'s ability to colonize it remarkable.

**Etymology:** The scientific name *camphorata* refers to the camphor-like aroma of the fungus. *Antrodia* derives from Greek for "cavern-like," referring to the tunneling growth habit.

**Traditional use:** Known in Taiwan as 牛樟芝 (Niu-zhang-zhi) or 樟芝 (zhang-zhi). Traditional Taiwanese indigenous communities (particularly the Tsou and other mountain peoples) have used the fungus for generations as a liver tonic, for treating fatigue after alcohol consumption, and as a general strengthening agent. Modern Taiwanese pharmacological research on this species began in the 1990s.

**Conservation crisis:** Due to the extraordinary market value and rarity, illegal logging of protected *C. kanehirae* trees specifically to harvest *A. camphorata* has been a significant conservation problem. Taiwan's government has criminalized wild harvest without permits and invested heavily in cultivation research.

## Cultivation

Cultivation is the primary goal of research programs at National Taiwan University and other institutions, motivated by conservation and economic factors.

**Major cultivation challenges:**
- Extreme host specificity — wild specimens grow only on *C. kanehirae* heartwood
- Very slow growth rate
- High sensitivity to contamination

**Current cultivation methods:**
- **Liquid fermentation (mycelium):** Submerged culture in bioreactors with glucose/peptone media; produces mycelial biomass for extraction; commercially scaled; differs chemically from fruiting body
- **Log/wood cultivation:** *C. kanehirae* sawdust (or logs from controlled sources) inoculated with mycelium; partial success in producing fruiting bodies; requires 6–18+ months
- **Artificial substrate research:** Attempts to cultivate on other Cinnamomum species (C. zeylanicum, C. verum); limited success chemically
- **Mycelium-on-board (MOB) technology:** Solid-state fermentation on camphor wood boards; increasingly commercially viable

**Market pricing:** Wild-harvested *Antrodia camphorata* is the **most expensive medicinal mushroom in the world** by weight:
- Wild mature fruiting bodies: USD $15,000–$30,000+ per kilogram
- High-grade cultivated fruiting bodies: USD $2,000–$5,000/kg
- Mycelial biomass products: USD $100–$500/kg
- A single large wild specimen can be worth several thousand dollars

## Bioactive Compounds

*Antrodia camphorata* has one of the most complex and unique secondary metabolite profiles among medicinal fungi — significantly distinct from other Polyporales species:

- **Antroquinonol (antroquinonol, NBQO)**: a unique benzoquinone derivative; the most intensively studied compound; inhibits mTOR, EGFR, and AMPK pathways; antitumor activity in numerous cell lines; Phase I/II clinical trials in hepatocellular carcinoma (see below)
- **Antcins (A–K and beyond)**: a series of lanostane-type triterpenoids; anti-inflammatory, antitumor, hepatoprotective; antcin K is particularly studied for hepatoprotection
- **Maleic acid derivatives and succinic acid derivatives**: unusual small organic acids; contribute to antioxidant activity
- **Ergostane-type steroids**: ergosterol, ergosterol peroxide, antcin B, antcin C; anti-inflammatory
- **Polysaccharides**: β-glucans and galactan heteropolysaccharides; immunomodulatory
- **Sesquiterpene lactones**: contribute to distinctive camphor-like aroma and some bioactivity
- **Zhankuic acids**: a unique series of triterpenoids; anti-inflammatory via 5-LOX inhibition

### Species-Specific Health Research

**Antroquinonol clinical trials:**
- **Phase I trial (Taiwanese cancer patients):** Antroquinonol (NBQO) reached Phase I clinical trials in Taiwan for solid tumors; demonstrated acceptable safety profile and signals of antitumor activity
- **Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC):** Multiple preclinical studies show antroquinonol induces G1 phase arrest and apoptosis in HCC cell lines via mTOR/p70S6K inhibition; mechanism is distinct from conventional chemotherapy
- **Lung cancer:** Preclinical data show antroquinonol inhibits EGFR downstream signaling in NSCLC cell lines
- **Clinical data remain limited** — most evidence is preclinical; the high cost of material and limited supply constrain trials

**Hepatoprotection:**
- Traditional use as a liver tonic is supported by preclinical evidence
- Antcin K and related triterpenoids protect against acetaminophen-induced hepatotoxicity in mouse models (relevant to traditional use following alcohol consumption)
- Anti-fibrotic effects observed in TGF-β-activated hepatic stellate cell models

**Anti-inflammatory:**
- Zhankuic acids and antcins inhibit 5-LOX and COX-2; comparable to some pharmaceutical anti-inflammatories in vitro

## Culinary

Not consumed as food in any culinary tradition — the extremely bitter, astringent taste and tough woody texture make this impractical. Consumed exclusively as:
- Concentrated hot water or dual-extract capsules
- Tincture (ethanol extraction)
- Tea (decoction of dried slices in hot water — intensely bitter)
- Powdered mycelial biomass products

**Consumer caution:** The extreme value of authentic *A. camphorata* makes adulteration a significant commercial problem. Many products labeled as "Antrodia camphorata" or "Taiwan Antrodia" contain adulterants or related Antrodia species with different chemistry. Verified products with chemical authentication (antroquinonol HPLC quantification) should be required.
