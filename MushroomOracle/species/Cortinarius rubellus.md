# *Cortinarius rubellus* (Deadly Webcap)

> **DEADLY POISONOUS. Contains orellanine — a nephrotoxin that causes irreversible kidney failure. The most insidious dangerous mushroom in European mycology: symptoms may not appear until 2 to 14 DAYS after ingestion, by which time catastrophic, irreversible kidney damage has occurred. Dialysis or kidney transplant may be required. No antidote exists. Confusion with edible mushrooms has caused deaths and permanent kidney failure across Europe.**

## Identification

- **Cap:** 3–8 cm diameter; **reddish-brown to tawny-orange**, sometimes brick-red or fox-red; conical to convex when young, becoming broadly umbonate (with a central raised bump) to flat; surface dry, fibrous, silky; no viscid surface; often paler at margin
- **Gills:** Attached to stipe; **rust-brown to cinnamon-brown** with maturity; widely spaced; may appear paler when young
- **Spore print:** **Rust-brown** — characteristic of the genus *Cortinarius*
- **Spores:** Ellipsoid to almond-shaped; 9–12 × 5.5–7 μm; warted (verrucose) surface; rust-brown in mass
- **Cortina:** The defining character of genus *Cortinarius*: young specimens have a **cobweb-like, fibrous veil (cortina)** stretching between the cap margin and stipe; this veil is delicate and often visible only on fresh, young specimens; it disappears with age and rain, leaving rust-brown spore deposits on the stipe where the cortina attached — these spore deposits on the upper stipe are the residual evidence of the cortina in mature specimens
- **Stipe:** 5–10 cm × 0.5–1.5 cm; **reddish-brown to orange-brown**; fibrous; cylindrical or tapering toward the base; sometimes slightly swollen at base; no ring (the cortina is NOT a ring — it breaks down and disappears)
- **Flesh:** Orange-brown throughout; does not discolor when cut
- **Smell:** Faint, radish-like or slightly unpleasant; not distinctive
- **Taste:** Mild — provides absolutely no warning of toxicity

**Related species:** *Cortinarius orellanus* (Fool's Webcap) is equally toxic, contains the same orellanine, and has a broader habitat (not confined to conifers); orange to red-brown, brighter colored; occurs in southern and central Europe. Both are responsible for documented deaths and dialysis cases.

**Critical field note:** The cortina is the most important genus-level identifying feature. Look for it on young fresh specimens. On older specimens, look for the rust-brown spore smear on the upper stipe where the cortina collapsed and was coated with spores.

## Biology

*Cortinarius rubellus* is an obligate [[Mycorrhizal Relationships|ectomycorrhizal]] species — it cannot grow without a host tree. Associates primarily with conifers: pine (*Pinus*), spruce (*Picea*), and fir (*Abies*), often on acidic, peaty soils in boreal and montane forests. Fruits in summer through autumn (July–November).

The genus *Cortinarius* is the **largest genus of mushrooms**, with over 1,000 described species worldwide. Most are not well studied for toxicity. *C. rubellus*, *C. orellanus*, and a handful of related species contain orellanine and are deadly. Reliable identification to the species level within *Cortinarius* requires expert mycological knowledge — the genus is considered unsuitable for amateur foraging.

Fruiting bodies are relatively inconspicuous — medium-sized, brown-orange, forest floor mushrooms that could be overlooked or mistaken for several other common species.

## Toxicology

### Toxins

**Orellanine** (2,2'-bipyridine-N,N'-dioxide) is the primary nephrotoxin:
- A bipyridyl compound structurally unlike any other known mycotoxin
- **Mechanism:** Orellanine is a **nephrotoxin** that selectively destroys cells of the **renal proximal tubules** and distal tubules — the cells responsible for most active filtration and reabsorption in the kidney. The mechanism involves generation of free radicals (reactive oxygen species) and possibly inhibition of alkaline phosphatase. The result is progressive, irreversible renal interstitial fibrosis — scarring of the kidney tissue
- **Target organ:** Kidney (selectively nephrotoxic — liver and other organs are not the primary targets, distinguishing orellanine poisoning from amatoxin poisoning)
- **Stability:** Orellanine is remarkably heat-stable; **not destroyed by cooking, drying, or freezing**
- **Content in mushroom:** Varies; average concentration approximately 0.1% of dry weight; a moderate serving (100 g fresh) could easily deliver a toxic dose
- **Detection:** Orellanine fluoresces under UV light — used in forensic identification of suspected specimens; can also be detected in kidney biopsy or gastric contents by specialized toxicology labs

### The Critically Dangerous Delay

Orellanine poisoning has the **longest latent period of any mushroom toxin**: symptoms typically do not appear until **2 to 14 days** after ingestion, with a median around 11 days. Cases of delayed onset up to 21 days have been documented.

This extreme delay is the defining and most dangerous characteristic:
- By the time any symptom appears, significant and irreversible renal damage has already occurred
- Victims do not connect their symptoms to a meal eaten 1–2 weeks earlier
- Medical personnel may not identify mushroom poisoning as the cause for days
- The delay also means that hospital presentation is almost always too late for gastric decontamination

### Symptom Timeline

| Phase | Timing | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Latent phase** | **Days 0–3 to 14** | **Completely asymptomatic** — may be mild nausea/flu-like in some cases |
| **Early symptoms** | **Days 3–14** | Gradually increasing nausea, headache, fatigue, chills, mild abdominal discomfort; often mistaken for flu or unrelated illness |
| **Renal symptoms** | **Weeks 1–4** | Increased thirst (polydipsia), increased urination (polyuria), flank pain; laboratory tests reveal elevated creatinine and blood urea nitrogen (BUN) — acute kidney injury |
| **Progressive failure** | **Weeks 2–8** | Oliguria (decreased urine output); electrolyte imbalances; anemia; systemic symptoms of uremia; worsening laboratory values |
| **End-stage** | **Weeks to months** | Renal failure requiring dialysis; possible kidney transplant; recovery of some renal function possible in mild cases with aggressive treatment |

### Treatment

- **No specific antidote for orellanine**
- US Poison Control: **1-800-222-1222**
- Gastric decontamination is almost never possible (presentation is always days to weeks after ingestion)
- **Supportive renal care**: monitoring of creatinine, BUN, electrolytes, fluid balance
- **Hemodialysis**: required in severe cases; may need to be continued indefinitely or until kidney function stabilizes or transplant is performed
- **Kidney transplant**: required in the most severe cases where irreversible end-stage renal failure occurs
- **Corticosteroids**: have been used empirically to reduce inflammatory damage; limited evidence
- Prognosis depends on the amount consumed, time to diagnosis, and degree of renal damage at presentation; some patients recover partial renal function; others remain dialysis-dependent

### Mortality and Morbidity

- Direct fatality rate from orellanine poisoning is relatively low (perhaps 0–5% with modern care), but the **rate of permanent severe kidney damage is very high**
- Many survivors suffer from **chronic renal insufficiency** requiring long-term medical management
- Kidney transplant patients face the risks and lifetime immunosuppression of all organ transplant recipients
- This is one of the most devastating outcomes of any non-fatal mushroom poisoning

## Confusion Risk

*C. rubellus* and *C. orellanus* have caused the majority of their European poisonings through confusion with various edible autumn mushrooms. The rust-brown color and cortina are not always recognized by non-expert foragers.

### Chanterelles (*Cantharellus cibarius*)

Though structurally quite different, both are orange-brown autumn forest mushrooms, and misidentification by inexperienced foragers has caused documented orellanine poisonings.

| Character | *C. rubellus* (DEADLY) | *Cantharellus cibarius* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Hymenium | **True gills** — bladed, rust-brown | **False gills** — forking blunt ridges, pale yellow-orange |
| Spore print | **Rust-brown** | Pale cream to white |
| Veil | **Cortina present** on young specimens | **None** |
| Cap shape | Umbonate (raised center), conical | Funnel-shaped; margin wavy; no central umbo |
| Smell | Faint, unremarkable | **Fruity, apricot-like** |

### Brown Roll-Rim (*Paxillus involutus*)

An edible-appearing brown mushroom sometimes confused with webcaps; also toxic (causes immune-mediated hemolysis after repeated consumption). Both are brown forest mushrooms but *Paxillus* has a distinctive inrolled cap margin and decurrent gills.

### Edible *Cortinarius* Species

Some *Cortinarius* species are reported as edible in certain European traditions (e.g., *C. caerulescens*, *C. praestans*). **However, given the difficulty of species-level identification within this enormous genus and the catastrophic consequences of mistaking an orellanine-containing species for an edible one, consuming any *Cortinarius* species is strongly inadvisable for non-expert mycologists.** The rust-brown spore print is genus-level; it does not help distinguish between toxic and non-toxic species.

### Any Brown Autumn Forest Mushroom

The most important general rule: **any rust-brown-spored, brown-to-orange mushroom in forest habitat with a fibrous cortina (or cortina scar on the stipe) should be considered suspect for *Cortinarius* and not consumed without expert identification.**
