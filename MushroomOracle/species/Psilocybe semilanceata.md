## Identification

- **Cap:** 0.5–2.5 cm; characteristically **sharply conical to campanulate (bell-shaped)** with a distinctive **pointed central papilla** (nipple-like projection) — the "liberty cap" or "Phrygian cap" shape; surface smooth, striate; color chestnut-brown when moist, drying to pale tan/cream (hygrophanous); strongly **blueing when damaged**
- **Gills:** Adnate; brown to purplish-brown; relatively broad
- **Stipe:** 4–10 cm × 1–2 mm; slender; pale cream to brown; wavy; **fibrous and elastic** — can be bent without breaking; whitish veil remnants near base
- **Spore print:** Dark purplish-brown to black; spores 10–15 × 7–9 μm
- **Veil:** Cortinate (web-like); faint; may leave a faint ring zone

The conical cap with pointed papilla is the most distinctive field character — it has been compared to the Phrygian cap of French Revolutionary iconography, hence "liberty cap."

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (campanulate cap shape, hygrophanous surface, bruising), [[Spore Prints]] (dark purple-brown print), [[Dangerous Look-Alikes]] (Galerina marginata), [[Foraging Safety]], [[Habitat Guide]] (grassland habitats)*

**Nomenclatorial note:** Designated as the **type species** of the genus *Psilocybe* in 2010 during taxonomic revision that separated hallucinogenic from non-hallucinogenic species (moved non-hallucinogenic species to *Deconica*).

**[[Dangerous Look-Alikes]]:**
- *Galerina marginata*: **deadly poisonous** (amatoxins); grows on wood/dung; no blue bruising; rusty-brown spore print
- *Conocybe* and *Bolbitius* species: conical caps but rusty-brown or yellow-brown spore print; no blue bruising; fragile/shorter stipe
- *Panaeolus semiovatus*: on dung; no blue bruising; rounder cap

## Biology

Grows in **grass** (pastures, meadows, lawns, roadsides, dune grassland) — NOT on dung, unlike *[[Psilocybe cubensis]]*. Associated with areas grazed by livestock where soil is disturbed and enriched without being heavily fertilized. The mycelium grows in association with decaying grass roots and enriched soil.

First described by Fries; transferred to *Psilocybe* by Kummer in 1871. It is the most commonly encountered psilocybin mushroom in northwestern Europe and the Pacific Northwest.

## Cultivation

Can be cultivated but less commonly grown than *P. cubensis*. Challenging to grow indoors due to preference for outdoor grass-rich conditions. Outdoor cultivation in appropriate substrates (grass-inoculated beds) is possible. Primarily encountered as a wild-harvested species.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Psilocybin**: up to ~1.0% by dry weight (notably higher than *[[Psilocybe cubensis]]*; average ~0.37%)
- **Psilocin**: trace amounts in fresh specimens; psilocybin predominates

The higher psilocybin concentration relative to body size means potency per gram is substantially greater than *P. cubensis*. Small fruiting bodies (0.5–2.5 cm cap) belie their chemical potency.

See [[Psilocybin Research]] for context on therapeutic applications.

## Culinary

Not applicable as a culinary mushroom. Consumed for psychoactive effects. Most common psilocybin mushroom by geographic range in European and Pacific Northwest contexts. Associated with outdoor autumn foraging.
