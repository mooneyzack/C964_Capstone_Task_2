# *Galerina marginata* (Deadly Galerina)

> **DEADLY POISONOUS. Contains amatoxins (α-amanitin) in the same concentrations as the Death Cap (*Amanita phalloides*). Small, brown, unremarkable, and easily overlooked — grows in the same habitat as several prized edible species. Has killed foragers who harvested it accidentally alongside honey mushrooms. Do not consume any small brown mushroom from wood without an absolute positive identification.**

## Identification

- **Cap:** 1–5 cm diameter; **brown to tawny or honey-brown**; **hygrophanous** — dries to a paler buff color from the center outward, creating a two-toned appearance when partially dry (wet margin darker than dry center); convex when young, becoming broadly convex to nearly flat with age; surface smooth, slightly viscid when wet
- **Gills:** Pale brown when young; becoming **rust-brown to cinnamon** with age; adnate (attached directly to stipe) to slightly decurrent; moderately crowded; may have a slightly watery appearance
- **Spore print:** **Rusty brown** — this is the critical identifying character; must be confirmed for any wood-growing brown mushroom
- **Spores:** Ellipsoid; 8–10 × 5–6 μm; **dextrinoid** (react in Melzer's reagent); surface roughened or wrinkled (verrucose)
- **Stipe:** 2–9 cm × 3–8 mm; pale brown above, darkening downward; **often with a fragile, fibrous ring (annulus)** that may disappear with age, rain, or handling; below the ring zone the stipe is typically darker and fibrous; no volva
- **Flesh:** Pale brown; thin; no distinctive chemical reactions
- **Smell:** Faint, mealy or farinaceous (flour-like)
- **Taste:** Mild, slightly mealy — provides no warning of toxicity

**Critical note:** *G. marginata* is small, drab, and forgettable. There is nothing visually dramatic about it. It does not stand out in the field. It can and does grow intermixed with honey mushrooms on the same log.

## Biology

*Galerina marginata* (formerly separated into multiple species including *G. autumnalis*, *G. oregonensis*, *G. unicolor*; now consolidated as a single cosmopolitan species based on molecular data) is a **saprotrophic wood decay fungus**. It grows on the dead wood of both conifers and hardwoods — logs, stumps, buried roots, and woody debris. It causes brown rot.

Found fruiting from summer through late autumn; in mild climates can persist into winter or fruit in early spring. Fruits in clusters or scattered, often on the surface of mossy decaying logs. Very widespread and common across the Northern Hemisphere and in temperate regions of the Southern Hemisphere.

The species was formerly split into multiple species (*G. autumnalis* being the most commonly cited North American name) but is now considered one variable global species by modern mycologists.

## Toxicology

### Toxins

*Galerina marginata* contains **amatoxins** — specifically **α-amanitin and β-amanitin** — in concentrations comparable to *Amanita phalloides*:
- α-Amanitin content: approximately **100–200 μg per gram of dry weight** (comparable to or exceeding that of death cap per gram)
- Lethal dose: because the fruiting bodies are small, multiple caps are required to deliver a lethal dose in adults, but **as few as one or a few grams of dried material can be life-threatening**; children are at higher risk
- **Mechanism:** α-Amanitin selectively inhibits **RNA polymerase II (RNAP II)** — the enzyme that transcribes all protein-coding genes. This halts mRNA production and causes progressive cell death in high-turnover tissues, primarily **hepatocytes (liver cells)** and **renal proximal tubule cells**
- Amatoxins are **heat-stable** — not destroyed by cooking, drying, or freezing
- **Tasteless and odorless** — no sensory warning

The presence of amatoxins in a small, drab, wood-growing mushroom that grows alongside prized edibles makes *G. marginata* arguably the most practically dangerous mushroom for experienced foragers who may become complacent about small brown species in a honey mushroom harvest.

### Symptom Timeline

Identical to *Amanita phalloides* — the characteristic delayed-onset amatoxin syndrome:

| Phase | Timing | Symptoms |
|---|---|---|
| **Latent phase** | 0 to 6–24 hours | **No symptoms** — the victim is completely asymptomatic |
| **Gastrointestinal phase** | 6–24 hours onset | Severe watery diarrhea, vomiting, abdominal cramps, dehydration |
| **Apparent recovery** | ~48–72 hours | GI symptoms ease; false sense of improvement |
| **Hepatorenal failure** | 4–8 days | Jaundice, coagulopathy, liver failure, kidney injury, possible death |

### Treatment

- **No antidote**
- Emergency medical care immediately; do not wait for symptoms
- US Poison Control: **1-800-222-1222**
- Supportive care, IV fluids, organ function monitoring
- **Silibinin (IV)**: most evidence-based specific agent; blocks hepatocyte uptake of amatoxins
- **Liver transplantation** in fulminant hepatic failure
- Prognosis worsens significantly with delayed presentation

## Confusion Risk

*G. marginata* is particularly dangerous because it grows in the exact same microhabitat — on and around dead wood — as several frequently foraged edible species. Mixed collections are the primary cause of poisonings.

### Honey Mushroom (*Armillaria* spp.)

The most dangerous confusion scenario globally. *Armillaria* species are among the most widely foraged edibles in the world. *Galerina marginata* has killed multiple people who consumed it accidentally mixed into honey mushroom harvests.

| Character | *G. marginata* (DEADLY) | *Armillaria* spp. (edible with caution) |
|---|---|---|
| Cap size | **Small: 1–5 cm** | Larger: 3–15 cm |
| Spore print | **Rusty brown** | **White** |
| Gills | Brown, darkening to rust | White to cream; may develop rust-brown spots with age |
| Ring | **Fragile, fibrous, thin; often absent** | More substantial, cottony to membranous |
| Cap surface | Smooth, hygrophanous | Often fibrous, hairy, or scaly |
| Basal clustering | Less tightly clustered | Often tightly clustered with shared stipe base |

**The single most important test:** Spore print. Rusty brown = *Galerina*; white = *Armillaria*. This test is mandatory before consuming any cluster-growing wood mushroom. Never skip it because "it looks like honey mushroom."

### Velvet Shank / Enoki (*Flammulina velutipes*)

Both grow on dead wood. *Flammulina* is a popular edible; the cultivated form (enoki) is widely sold in grocery stores.

| Character | *G. marginata* (DEADLY) | *Flammulina velutipes* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Spore print | **Rusty brown** | **White** |
| Stipe surface | Fibrous; ring present (often faint) | **Distinctly velvety/fuzzy dark brown** toward base — very characteristic |
| Preferred season | Summer–autumn | **Cold-weather species**: late autumn, winter, early spring |
| Gills | Brown | Pale cream to white |

### Sheathed Woodtuft (*Kuehneromyces mutabilis*)

Edible and also grows in clusters on stumps; popular in Europe. One of the most hazardous species pairs in European mycology due to habitat overlap and similar appearance. Both have brown, hygrophanous caps and rust-colored spores.

| Character | *G. marginata* (DEADLY) | *Kuehneromyces mutabilis* (edible) |
|---|---|---|
| Spore print | **Rusty brown** | **Brown** (similar hue) |
| Stipe **below ring** | Smooth to fibrous | **Distinctly scaly** — pronounced dark, recurved scales below the ring zone |
| Ring | Thin, fragile, often absent | More persistent ring zone |
| Spore shape (microscope) | Roughened surface; 8–10 × 5–6 μm | Smooth surface; smaller |

**Critical note:** The spore prints of both species are brown; the color difference can be subtle. The stipe scaling below the ring is the best macroscopic differentiator, but it requires careful examination. When any doubt exists, do not consume.

### Psilocybe Mushrooms

*Galerina marginata* has been consumed fatally by individuals who misidentified it as a psychoactive *Psilocybe* species while foraging for "magic mushrooms." Both are small brown mushrooms that can grow near or on wood. The consequences of this confusion have been fatal.

| Character | *G. marginata* (DEADLY) | *Psilocybe* spp. (psychoactive) |
|---|---|---|
| Spore print | **Rusty brown** | **Dark purple-brown to purple-black** |
| Bluing reaction | **None** | Many *Psilocybe* bruise blue where damaged |
| Stipe ring | Usually present (fragile) | May or may not be present depending on species |

**Rule:** Any small brown mushroom on wood with a rust-brown (not purple-black) spore print and no blue bruising is suspect for *Galerina*. Never consume without definitive identification.
