## Identification

- **Cap (pileus):** 2–30 cm wide; fan- to oyster-shaped; broadly convex to nearly flat; smooth; color variable from pearl gray to dark gray-blue (young) to pale grayish-tan or cream with age; hygrophanous (color fades when dry)
- **Gills:** White to cream; crowded; decurrent (running down the stipe)
- **Stipe:** Very short (0.5–3 cm), eccentric to lateral or absent; white; tough
- **Spore print:** White to lilac-gray
- **Flesh:** White; firm when young; meaty texture
- **Smell and taste:** Mild, pleasant, slightly anise-like

## Biology

Primary saprotroph, **[[Wood Decay|white-rot]]** decomposer. Produces the full complement of lignocellulose-degrading enzymes (laccases, peroxidases, cellulases). Also **nematophagous** — produces toxocysts containing 3-octanone that paralyze and kill nematodes, which the mycelium then colonizes as a nitrogen source.

Originally described in 1775 by Jacquin; transferred to *Pleurotus* by Paul Kummer in 1871. The species complex contains at least **20 phylogenetic species** (phylogenomics of 40 nuclear orthologs; 2020), with at least 7 putatively new species.

Commercial cultivation began in Germany during World War I. Currently the **third most cultivated mushroom globally**, accounting for approximately 25% of total cultivated mushroom production.

## Cultivation

Grows on a wide range of [[Substrates|substrates]] including straw, hardwood sawdust, cottonseed hulls, and coffee grounds. One of the most beginner-friendly cultivated species due to aggressive colonization and wide temperature tolerance.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **[[Beta-Glucans]]**: immunomodulatory polysaccharides; **24.2 ± 1.6% DW** — among the highest beta-glucan content of common edible species
- **Pleuran** (β-1,3/1,6-glucan): studied specifically for respiratory health and allergy management
- **Lovastatin (mevinolin)**: HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor — the **exact same molecule** as the first FDA-approved statin drug (Mevacor)
- **[[Ergothioneine]]**: up to 4.0 mg/g DW — among the highest food sources (see [[Nutritional Profiles]])
- Various phenolic compounds

### Species-Specific Health Research

**Lovastatin content — unique among edible mushrooms:**
- Oyster mushrooms are the **only edible food source of naturally occurring lovastatin (mevinolin)**, an HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor
- Content varies: 50–5,991 mcg/g dry weight across *Pleurotus* species; up to **2.8% lovastatin on a dry weight basis** in some measurements (research conditions)
- *P. sapidus*, *P. sajor-caju*, and *P. ostreatus* all produce mevinolin; content is enhanced by certain growing conditions and substrate optimization

**Clinical cholesterol-lowering evidence:**
- Human study: 20 subjects consuming daily oyster mushroom soup (30g dried oyster mushroom per serving) showed significant reductions in triacylglycerol, oxidized-LDL rates, and total cholesterol
- HIV/antiretroviral patients study (PMC3163637): antihyperlipidemic effects of oyster mushroom consumption in HIV-infected individuals on antiretroviral therapy
- **Dual mechanism of action:** (1) lovastatin inhibits HMG-CoA reductase; (2) beta-glucans form intestinal gel reducing cholesterol absorption

**Ergothioneine:**
- Oyster mushrooms are among the top dietary ergothioneine sources (up to 4 mg/g DW), adding a separate antioxidant/neuroprotective dimension. See [[Ergothioneine]] for the full case for vitamin-like status.

**Pleuran (oyster beta-glucan):**
- In pediatric trials for respiratory health: **reduced peripheral blood eosinophilia** and **stabilized IgE levels** in respiratory illness patients
- Potential role in asthma/allergy management

## Culinary

One of the most versatile edible fungi. The flesh is meaty and mild with a slightly anise-like flavor. Excellent sautéed, roasted, or used in soups and stir-fries. Delicate texture best preserved with high-heat cooking. All color variants (gray, pink, yellow, white) are edible and similar in flavor.
