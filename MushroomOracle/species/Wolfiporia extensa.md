# Wolfiporia extensa (Poria / Fu Ling)

## Identification

**Note:** Unlike most medicinal fungi, *Wolfiporia extensa* is almost never identified by its fruiting body — the mushroom form is rarely produced and inconsequential. The medicinal product is the **sclerotium**: a dense, underground mass of compacted mycelium and storage tissue.

- **Sclerotium:** Irregular, tuber-like mass; typically **10–30 cm** (sometimes up to 50+ cm); weighing from hundreds of grams to several kilograms
- **External surface:** Rough, warty, dark brown to blackish-brown rind (the "Fu Pi" or poria rind, itself a separate TCM product)
- **Interior:** White to pale pinkish-white; dense, firm, and chalky; breaks with a granular texture
- **Attachment:** Connected to pine roots by mycelial cords (strands); found 20–60 cm below soil surface, often around decaying pine root systems
- **Fruiting body (when present):** Thin, flat, resupinate; white; pore surface with 4–6 pores per mm; typically forms only briefly during wet seasons and is not commercially relevant

**Field collection:** Locating *Wolfiporia* requires knowing where mature pines have stood (the sclerotia can persist for decades after tree death). Traditional harvesters use pointed iron rods to probe soil around pine stumps.

## Biology

*Wolfiporia extensa* produces its characteristic sclerotium as an adaptation for surviving harsh conditions and nutrient fluctuations. The sclerotium functions as a long-term energy and carbon reserve, analogous to a tuber in plants.

**Taxonomy:** Originally described as *Poria cocos* (Schw.) F.Wolf; reclassified as *Wolfiporia cocos* (F.Wolf) Ryvarden & Gilb.; most current literature uses *Wolfiporia extensa* (Peck) Ginns. Many sources still use *Poria cocos* or list it under *Pachyma hoelen* (an old synonym). The sclerotium in commerce is invariably labeled "Poria" or "Fu Ling" (茯苓).

**TCM tradition:** Fu Ling (茯苓) has been documented in Chinese medicine for over 2,000 years. It appears in the *Shennong Bencao Jing* (神農本草經), the oldest Chinese pharmacopoeia (~100 AD), classified as a Superior (upper class) herb — those taken long-term without toxicity. Traditional TCM properties: sweet, bland taste; neutral temperament; acts on Heart, Spleen, and Kidney meridians. Primary TCM actions: **tonifies Spleen Qi**, **calms Shen (spirit)**, **promotes urination and dampness**, **transforms Phlegm**.

**Different fractions recognized in TCM:**
- **Fu Ling (白茯苓):** White inner flesh — the main product
- **Chi Fu Ling:** Pink or reddish-tinged inner flesh — different clinical indication
- **Fu Shen (茯神):** Sclerotium tissue formed around a pine root — used specifically for calming the spirit (insomnia, anxiety)
- **Fu Pi (茯苓皮):** The dark outer rind — used primarily as a diuretic

## Cultivation

Cultivation has been practiced in China for several centuries, though it remains challenging. Modern cultivation is primarily conducted in Yunnan, Anhui, Hunan, and Fujian provinces.

**Traditional log cultivation:**
1. Freshly cut pine logs (or stumps) are inoculated with *Wolfiporia* spawn
2. Logs are partially buried in sloped hillside plots; draining soil essential (prevents waterlogging)
3. Incubation: 1–3 years for sclerotia to form and reach harvest size
4. Harvest: Soil is carefully excavated by hand; sclerotia removed; logs re-covered for subsequent harvests

**Modern methods:**
- **Sawdust bag cultivation**: Pine sawdust blocks inoculated and buried; faster than log cultivation (12–18 months) but smaller sclerotia
- **Wild simulation plots**: Hillside plantation sites with pine roots deliberately inoculated; mimics natural conditions

**Market scale:** China produces approximately **8,000–10,000 metric tonnes** annually; Fu Ling is one of the highest-volume TCM herbs by weight. Yunnan Province ("Yunnan Poria") is the most prestigious production region.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Pachymic acid** (pachymic acid A, B): lanostane-type triterpenoid; primary marker compound; anti-inflammatory via prostaglandin inhibition; inhibits 5-alpha-reductase; mild antiproliferative
- **Trametenolic acid (trametenolone)**: lanostane triterpenoid; structurally related to anti-inflammatory steroids; inhibits NF-κB pathway
- **Poricoic acids** (A–H): a series of lanostane triterpenoids unique to *Wolfiporia*; anti-inflammatory, antiproliferative, antifungal activity against some yeasts
- **Beta-pachyman / β-1,3-D-glucan (curdlan-type)**: the primary polysaccharide; immunostimulatory; forms the structural matrix of the sclerotium; water-insoluble but can be chemically modified (carboxymethylpachyman — water-soluble and more bioavailable); activates complement pathway and macrophages
- **Ergosterol, ergosterol peroxide**: anti-inflammatory; Ergosterol peroxide is apoptosis-inducing
- **Histidine, lysine**: free amino acids

### Species-Specific Health Research

**Immunomodulation:**
- Beta-pachyman (insoluble) and its soluble derivatives demonstrate complement activation, macrophage stimulation, and NK cell enhancement in animal studies
- Carboxymethylpachyman (CMP) has been studied in cancer adjunct applications

**CNS / anxiolytic effects:**
- Animal models show Fu Ling extract reduces anxiety-like behavior in the elevated plus maze
- Pachymic acid demonstrates GABA-A receptor modulation (positive allosteric modulation), providing a mechanism for the traditional "calming the spirit" use
- Triterpenes modulate serotonin 5-HT3 receptor — another plausible anxiolytic mechanism

**Anti-inflammatory:**
- Pachymic acid and trametenolic acid inhibit COX-2 and PGE2 production in macrophage cell lines; comparable activity to ibuprofen in some models
- Poricoic acids inhibit NF-κB nuclear translocation

**Metabolic/diuretic:**
- Traditional diuretic use supported by inhibition of sodium-potassium ATPase in renal tubule cells (animal models)
- Modest effects on blood glucose observed in streptozotocin diabetic mouse models

## Culinary

In Chinese food culture, Fu Ling occupies a unique position as both medicine and food ingredient. Unlike most medicinal fungi, the white interior of the sclerotium is milled and consumed in food preparations.

**Culinary forms:**
- **Fu Ling cake (茯苓饼, Fúlíng bǐng):** A famous Beijing specialty — thin, crispy wafer-like biscuits made with *Wolfiporia* powder and starch filling; sold throughout Beijing as a traditional snack and souvenir
- **Fu Ling congee (茯苓粥):** Cooked with rice, lotus seed, and Chinese date (jujube) for a nourishing breakfast porridge
- **Fu Ling noodles:** Sclerotium powder blended into noodle dough
- **Steamed dumplings and buns:** Added as a filling ingredient alongside sweetened paste
- **Decoction:** Simmered chunks of sclerotium in water for 30–60 minutes; drunk as a tonic tea

**Flavor:** Very mild, slightly bland, with a faint sweetness — easily incorporated into both sweet and savory preparations without dominating flavor.
