## Identification

**Wild Morphology:**
- **Cap:** 2–10 cm; orange-brown to honey-yellow; slimy/viscid when moist; convex to flat
- **Gills:** White to cream; adnate to slightly decurrent; moderately crowded
- **Stipe:** 3–8 cm; yellow-orange above; progressively darkening to **dark brown to black velvet at the base** — the characteristic feature giving the "velvet" name; stiff, fibrous
- **Spore print:** White; spores ellipsoid, 7–9 × 3–4 μm

**Cultivated Morphology (dramatic contrast to wild):**
- Long, thin, spaghetti-like stipes (8–13 cm)
- Tiny caps (0.5–2 cm); pale cream to white
- White throughout (etiolated — deprived of light)
- Grown in CO₂-enriched environments (800–1500 ppm) that promote stipe elongation

## Biology

**Key taxonomy note:** The common cultivated "enoki" sold in Asian markets was long classified as *F. velutipes* but DNA sequencing in 2018 confirmed it is a separate species native to East Asia: *Flammulina filiformis* Z.W.Ge, X.B.Liu & Zhu L.Yang, 2018. European/North American velvet shank (*F. velutipes*) is related but distinct.

Saprotrophic; **[[Wood Decay|brown-rot]]** causing on host wood — one of the few edible fungi with brown-rot ecology. One of the few edible fungi that fruits productively in cold conditions (autumn through winter into early spring; capable of fruiting under snow).

Described by Curtis; transferred to *Flammulina* by Singer in 1951.

## Cultivation

Cultivated on [[Substrates|hardwood sawdust]] blocks or straw. The distinctive pale cultivated form results from growing in the dark or low-light conditions in CO₂-enriched environments (800–1500 ppm CO₂ promotes stipe elongation and suppresses cap development). Optimal fruiting temperature: **5–12°C** — cold-tolerant and cold-preferring. [[Environmental Parameters]]: high humidity (85–95% RH), low light or darkness, high CO₂. Colonization at room temperature (~20°C); fruiting in cold conditions. High commercial production in Japan, Korea, China.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Flammulin (proflavin)**: ribosome-inactivating protein; antitumor properties in vitro
- **Enokitake polysaccharides**: immunomodulatory [[Beta-Glucans|beta-glucans]]
- **FVE (Flammulina velutipes extract)**: immunomodulatory lectin
- Various ergosterols and phenolic compounds

Enoki consumption has been epidemiologically associated with lower cancer rates in some Japanese population studies (Fujian/Nagano comparisons), though causality is not established.

## Culinary

The cultivated enoki (pale, elongated) and wild velvet shank have distinct culinary characters.

**Cultivated enoki:** Mild, slightly fruity flavor; crisp, crunchy texture. Excellent raw in salads, added at the end of cooking to soups (especially ramen and hot pot), or lightly sautéed. The elongated bundles make attractive garnishes. A staple in East Asian cuisine.

**Wild velvet shank:** More robust, honey-like flavor; firmer texture. Good sautéed in butter, used in soups, or cooked with game. The velvet stem is fibrous and best avoided.
