## Identification

- **Cap (pileus):** 5–30 cm (to 40 cm in exceptional specimens); convex to broadly umbonate; wine-red to burgundy when young, fading through chestnut-brown to tan or whitish with age and UV exposure; smooth, dry to slightly viscid when wet; margin with hanging white veil remnants when young
- **Gills:** White when young → gray → deep purple-black at maturity (from maturing spores); adnate to adnexed; crowded
- **Stipe:** 5–15 cm; 1–3 cm thick; white; firm; hollow when mature; bears a distinctive **skirt-like annulus** (ring) that is cogwheel-ridged on the upper surface — the "rugosoannulata" (rough-ringed) of the name
- **Spore print:** Deep purple-black to black
- **Flesh:** White; firm; mild pleasant odor
- **Smell and taste:** Mild, slightly nutty, pleasant

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (cogwheel annulus, purple-black spore print), [[Spore Prints]] (dark purple-black — critical for ID), [[Foraging Safety]] (dark spore print rules out deadly *Amanita* lookalikes), [[Habitat Guide]] (wood chip beds, disturbed ground)*

**Distinguishing features:** The cogwheel-textured ring combined with wine-red cap color in young specimens and deep purple-black spore print is diagnostic. The color progression (wine-red → tan) in strong light can confuse beginners. Always take a spore print — the dark purple-black is reliable and rules out dangerous white-spored lookalikes. See [[Foraging Safety]].

## Biology

Saprotrophic [[Wood Decay|white-rot]] decomposer of lignocellulosic material, especially wood chips, straw, and coarse woody debris. Distinguished from many gourmet species by its remarkable **outdoor garden cultivation suitability** — *S. rugosoannulata* thrives in cool, moist conditions and colonizes wood chip beds with the aggressive rhizomorphic mycelial growth characteristic of the Strophariaceae family.

Originally described from North America by Farlow in 1898, but subsequent phylogeographic analysis suggests the species may be Eurasian in origin, naturalized widely in North America and elsewhere through timber trade and horticulture. Molecular phylogenetics place it firmly in Strophariaceae, closely allied with *Pholiota* and *Hypholoma*.

Fruiting bodies are among the **largest of any wood-chip-associated species** — caps regularly exceed 20 cm and entire clusters can weigh several kilograms. The mycelium produces dense, rope-like rhizomorphs that can travel meters through wood chip beds to new resources.

**Seasonality:** Primarily a spring and fall fruiter in outdoor beds (optimum 10–20°C soil temperature). In mild climates it may flush year-round. Unlike many wood-decay fungi, it tolerates frost and can push through frozen surfaces.

## Cultivation

Wine cap is the easiest large-scale outdoor gourmet species for home gardeners and small farms — often called the "garden giant" precisely because it requires almost no infrastructure. Peter McCoy and many permaculture authors cite it as the flagship outdoor cultivation species.

**Method — wood chip beds:**
1. Lay fresh hardwood wood chips 10–20 cm deep over a garden bed or path; avoid cedar, walnut, or eucalyptus (allelopathic species)
2. Obtain grain or sawdust spawn and layer between chip strata (spawn-to-substrate ratio ~10–20% by weight)
3. Water thoroughly; cover with a thin layer of straw or additional chips to retain moisture
4. Mycelium colonizes within 4–8 weeks under optimal conditions (15–24°C)
5. First fruiting in 2–6 months depending on season; beds productive for 3–5 years before substrate exhaustion

**Substrate:** Wood chips (best: hardwoods — oak, alder, maple, fruit woods); straw; compost-wood chip mixes. Avoid nitrogen-rich materials that encourage competitive bacteria. See [[Substrates]].

**No sterilization required** — the species is an aggressive competitor that outcompetes most molds under outdoor conditions when fresh chips are used. This makes it uniquely accessible to small growers without pressure-cooking equipment.

**Companion planting:** Wine cap beds integrate beautifully with vegetable gardens — the mycelium improves soil structure, suppresses some plant pathogens, and the fruiting bodies emerge at the soil/chip interface without competing with vegetable roots. The decomposed chips become excellent garden soil amendment.

**Indoor cultivation:** Possible on supplemented straw or wood chip blocks but less efficient than outdoor production. Fruiting requires 15–20°C; higher temperatures produce misshapen specimens.

**Harvesting:** Harvest before the veil breaks (while cap is still convex and gills are gray-white) for best texture and shelf life. Post-veil-break specimens are still edible but shorter-lived and messier.

## Bioactive Compounds

*S. rugosoannulata* has received less pharmaceutical research attention than species like *[[Ganoderma lucidum]]* or *[[Lentinula edodes]]*, but preliminary work documents:

- **[[Beta-Glucans]]:** Polysaccharide fractions with immunomodulatory activity in vitro; composition and (1→3),(1→6)-β-glucan content comparable to other Agaricomycetes
- **Phenolic antioxidants:** Gallic acid, catechol, protocatechuic acid — antioxidant DPPH assays show meaningful radical scavenging capacity
- **Ergothioneine:** Present; concentration data limited but expected moderate given Agaricomycetes classification
- **Strophariaceae metabolites:** Family members (e.g., *Pholiota*, *Hypholoma*) produce a range of terpenoids and indole derivatives; *S. rugosoannulata* profiles under ongoing investigation

Research gap: the species has not been subject to the intensive pharmaceutical screening that lion's mane or reishi have received. Its accessibility and large biomass production make it a candidate for agricultural/functional food applications.

## Culinary

Wine cap delivers a pleasant, substantial eating experience that punches above its wild-harvested peers in terms of yield and accessibility. Flavor is mild to moderately earthy with a slightly meaty, potato-like quality that many find more approachable than stronger-flavored species.

**Texture:** Firm when young (pre-veil-break); stipes especially dense and fibrous — split lengthwise and cook thoroughly. Older post-veil-break specimens become softer and more porous.

**Flavor profile:** Mild umami (2/5), earthy, slightly nutty, mild potato-like quality. Drying or browning develops more complex savory notes. Less distinctive than shiitake or porcini; excels as a volume ingredient. See [[Flavor Profiles]].

**Key preparations:**
- Sliced and sautéed in butter with garlic and thyme — the classic approach that showcases its mild earthiness
- Stuffed caps: the large convex caps are excellent vessels (stuffed with ricotta, herbs, breadcrumbs; baked at 190°C)
- Grilled whole young caps over charcoal — develops excellent smoky char flavor
- Thinly sliced and dehydrated for mushroom "jerky" (marinate in tamari, smoked paprika, liquid smoke; dehydrate at 55°C)
- Incorporated into pasta sauces, risotto, and grain dishes as a bulk earthy component
- Pickled: firm texture holds up well in brine (wine vinegar, salt, dill, peppercorns)

**Stipes:** Fibrous but edible — slice thinly and add to long-cooked preparations (soups, braises) or use in stock. Discard only the very base where mycelium is attached.

**Pairings:** Garlic, thyme, parsley, lemon, butter, cream, white wine, bacon, eggs, Gruyère. The mild flavor integrates well with most herb-forward European cooking. See [[Mushroom Pairings]].

**Preservation:** Fresh specimens shelf-stable 3–5 days refrigerated. Sauté and freeze, or dehydrate. Pickling preserves well for 3–6 months. See [[Preservation Methods]].

**Safety:** Cook thoroughly. Young specimens with gray-white gills are the choicest eating. Very dark-gilled mature specimens are safe but less appealing in texture. The dark spore print can stain cutting boards and light-colored dishes. See [[Safety and Contraindications]].
