## Identification

- **Cap:** 2–8 cm; convex to broadly umbonate; **golden-brown to tan when moist**, fading to pale cream when dry (strongly hygrophanous); surface smooth, slightly viscid when fresh; margin striate when moist
- **Gills:** Adnate; close; **grayish to purplish-brown**, darkening with age; edges whitish
- **Stipe:** 4–15 cm × 0.4–1.5 cm; hollow; white, aging to pale yellow; **strongly blueing when handled** (distinctive diagnostic character)
- **Annulus:** Present; thin; white; often torn; staining dark purple from falling spores
- **Spore print:** Dark purplish-brown
- **Spores:** 11–17 × 7–12 μm; subellipsoid; thick-walled with germ pore; under microscopy have a pale yellowish tint
- **Bruising reaction:** All parts **immediately and distinctly turn blue** when damaged — caused by oxidation of psilocybin/psilocin through the action of enzymes (laccase-type)

The blue bruising reaction, dark purplish-brown spore print, and presence of an annulus distinguish *P. cubensis* from most look-alikes.

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (bruising reactions, spore print color), [[Spore Prints]] (purple-brown print), [[Dangerous Look-Alikes]] (Galerina marginata comparison), [[Foraging Safety]], [[Habitat Guide]] (dung substrate associations)*

**[[Dangerous Look-Alikes]]:**
- *Galerina marginata*: **deadly poisonous**; contains amatoxins; grows on wood (not dung); white/cream spore print maturing to rusty brown; NO blue bruising; ring present but more fragile
- *Pholiota* species: on wood; rusty-brown spore print; no blue bruising
- Various *Conocybe* and *Panaeolus* species: check spore print color and bruising carefully

## Biology

Saprotrophic on dung (coprophilous) and enriched organic matter. The most widely distributed and easily cultivated psilocybin mushroom. Described by Earle in 1906; transferred to *Psilocybe* by Singer in 1948.

Hundreds of named **strains** (Golden Teacher, B+, Penis Envy, Albino A+, etc.) are maintained in the cultivation community, varying in potency, morphology, and growth rate — though taxonomically all remain *P. cubensis*.

For comparative context on research into psilocybin's therapeutic applications, see [[Psilocybin Research]].

## Cultivation

The most widely cultivated psilocybin mushroom. [[Substrates]]: grain spawn (rye, wheat, brown rice flour), coco coir, vermiculite, pasteurized straw, and many other substrates. [[Environmental Parameters]]: colonization at 24–28°C; fruiting at 22–26°C, 80–90% RH, 12 hours light/dark. Relatively fast colonizer; multiple flushes possible. [[Fruiting Triggers]]: increased humidity, temperature drop, fresh air exchange, reduced CO₂.

Cultivation legality varies dramatically by jurisdiction. Legal status of spores (which contain no psilocybin) differs from mycelium and fruiting bodies in many jurisdictions.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Psilocybin**: 0.14–0.63% by dry weight (typical: ~0.37%)
- **Psilocin**: 0.03–0.6% by dry weight
- **Baeocystin**: trace amounts

Psilocybin is the O-phosphate prodrug of psilocin; dephosphorylated by alkaline phosphatase to psilocin (the active form) in the body. Psilocin acts as a **5-HT2A serotonin receptor agonist**, producing altered perception, synesthesia, mystical-type experiences, and profound changes in consciousness.

See [[Psilocybin Research]] for clinical trial data on therapeutic applications in depression, addiction, PTSD, and end-of-life anxiety.

## Culinary

Not applicable as a culinary mushroom. Consumed for psychoactive effects. Traditional ceremonial use in Mesoamerica (Mazatec, Zapotec, and other Indigenous traditions); contemporary therapeutic, spiritual, and recreational contexts.
