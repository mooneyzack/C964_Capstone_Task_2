## Identification

- **Fruiting body:** Large compound polypore; clusters typically 20–50 cm across, rarely up to 150 cm and 50 kg in weight
- **Individual caps:** 2–10 cm; spoon-shaped to fan-shaped; grayish-brown to dark gray; wavy margins; smooth surface
- **Pores:** White; tiny (3–5 per mm); extending down the branching stipes
- **Flesh:** White; firm; mild flavor
- **Spore print:** White

The name "maitake" (舞茸) means "dancing mushroom" in Japanese — said to describe the excitement of foragers upon finding it, or the overlapping frond-like caps resembling a dancing butterfly.

## Biology

Saprotrophic, causing **[[Wood Decay|white heart rot]]** in host trees. The sclerotium (underground tuber-like structure, potato-sized) is a perennial structure that may persist for years, giving rise to annual fruiting bodies in late summer to autumn. Described by Dicks; transferred to *Grifola* by Gray in 1821.

[[Mycorrhizal Relationships]]: While primarily saprotrophic on dead heartwood, *G. frondosa* frequently fruits at the base of living oaks, suggesting possible weak parasitic or endophytic interactions with living host tissue.

## Cultivation

Cultivated on [[Substrates|oak sawdust]] and woodchip blocks. More difficult to cultivate than oyster mushrooms or shiitake — requires specific humidity regimes and temperature cycling. Commercial production is substantial in Japan and increasingly in North America. Colonization period: 60–90 days at 20–25°C. Fruiting at 10–18°C with high humidity (90–95% RH) and good air flow.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Grifolan (GRN)**: a gel-forming (1→6)-branched (1→3)-β-D-glucan with antitumor activity
- **MZF fraction**: polysaccharide activating macrophage proliferation, elevating IL-12, IL-2, IFN-γ, TNF-α, and enhancing NK cells and cytotoxic T lymphocytes
- **Grifolin**: reported anticancer activity
- **D-fraction**: widely studied [[Beta-Glucans|beta-glucan]] extract; several clinical trials for cancer immunotherapy adjuvant use
- **SX-fraction**: studied for blood sugar regulation and insulin sensitivity

## Culinary

Highly prized in Japanese cuisine. The layered fronds have a rich, earthy, woodsy flavor and firm, meaty texture that holds up well to cooking. Excellent in hot pot, stir-fry, tempura, rice dishes, and soups. The base and thick stipe portions may be tough — trim and use for stock. One of the best mushrooms for roasting whole at high heat.
