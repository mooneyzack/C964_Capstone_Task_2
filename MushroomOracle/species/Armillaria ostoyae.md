# Armillaria ostoyae (Honey Mushroom — Largest Organism on Earth)

## Identification

- **Cap:** 5–15 cm; honey-colored to brown; convex becoming flattened with a broad umbo; surface covered with dark fibrous scales (especially at center), giving a distinctive shaggy appearance; margin often striate
- **Gills:** White, attached to slightly decurrent; may develop reddish-brown spots with age
- **Stipe:** 5–15 cm tall × 1–2 cm diameter; tough, fibrous; whitish to brown below; with a **prominent ring (annulus)** — membranous, white to yellowish, often with a scaly fringe; ring is a key identification feature
- **Spore print:** White; spores ellipsoid, smooth, 8–11 × 5–7 μm; inamyloid
- **Growth habit:** Almost always in **dense clusters** at tree bases, on stumps, or emerging from buried roots; single specimens are uncommon
- **Rhizomorphs:** Below-ground, *Armillaria* spreads via **rhizomorphs** — black, boot-lace-like mycelial cords resembling plant roots; these are a definitive diagnostic feature; visible when bark is peeled from dying trees at the base

**Critical look-alike warning:** Multiple toxic species resemble honey mushrooms:
- *[[Galerina marginata]]* (deadly — contains amatoxins): grows on wood; similar coloring; lacks the scaly cap; ring is present but more fragile; **spore print is rusty brown** (white in *Armillaria*)
- *Pholiota* species: have rusty-brown spore print; slimy or scaly cap; no white ring
- *Hypholoma* species: purple-brown spore print; no ring

## Biology

### The Largest Organism on Earth

The most extraordinary biological fact about *Armillaria ostoyae* is that a single genetically identical individual — a single "individual" in the sense of sharing one genome — can cover astonishing areas through its underground mycelium and rhizomorphs.

**The Malheur specimen (Oregon, USA):**
- **Location:** Malheur National Forest, eastern Oregon, USA
- **Area covered:** Approximately **2,385 acres (965 hectares)** — roughly 3.5 square miles
- **Estimated mass:** ~**35,000 U.S. tons (~31,750 metric tonnes)** of mycelium; sometimes cited as ~6,000 tonnes (denser estimates) or up to 35,000 tonnes (including substrate)
- **Age:** Estimated **2,400–8,650 years old** based on growth rate modeling and genetic analysis
- **Discovery:** Identified in 2000 by mycologists Cathy Parks and Greg Filip of the USDA Forest Service through tissue sampling and DNA fingerprinting across the outbreak zone
- **Previous record holder:** A *Armillaria gallica* specimen in Michigan, 37 acres — also enormous but dwarfed by the Oregon specimen
- **Status:** Recognized in *Nature* (2003, Ferguson et al.) as the world's largest known organism by area

**Why so large?** *Armillaria ostoyae* grows clonally through rhizomorphs — underground mycelial cords — at a slow but inexorable pace. In the nutrient-rich, moderately moist soils of the Blue Mountains of Oregon, with abundant conifer host trees, the organism has expanded for millennia without the physical barriers (rivers, ridges, gaps) that would fragment other large clones.

### Bioluminescence

*Armillaria* mycelium is **bioluminescent** — it glows faintly green in the dark, a phenomenon known as **foxfire** or **faerie fire**. This glow is produced by the mycelium (not the fruiting bodies in most species) via a luciferin-luciferase reaction. The biological purpose of bioluminescence in fungi remains debated:
- **Spore dispersal hypothesis:** Attracts nocturnal insects that carry spores
- **Metabolic byproduct:** A result of oxidative reactions in lignin decomposition
- A 2020 study in *Current Biology* identified the specific compounds involved: a hispidin-type compound (also found in bioluminescent *Mycena* and *Panellus* species)

Foxfire has been documented historically in folklore as mysterious lights in forests, attributed to fairies or spirits.

### Pathology

*Armillaria* root rot is one of the most economically damaging forest diseases in the world:
- Kills weakened trees by girdling roots and root crowns; the white mycelial fans (plaques) visible under bark at the tree base are diagnostic
- Spreads through root-to-root contact and rhizomorphs
- The Malheur specimen is actively killing trees across its territory; the forest service has documented significant timber losses
- Infected trees may stand dead for years, posing fire hazard
- Control is extremely difficult; fumigation, biological agents, and maintaining tree vigor are the primary management strategies

## Ecological Significance

- **Largest organism:** The Malheur *A. ostoyae* is the largest known single organism on Earth by area (challenged only by some clonal plant colonies like *Posidonia* seagrass meadows and *Pando* quaking aspen, which dispute "individual" definitions)
- **Forest dynamics:** *Armillaria* root rot creates canopy gaps that increase biodiversity by enabling light penetration and promoting understory species
- **Saprotrophic role:** After host death, *Armillaria* transitions to saprotrophic activity, breaking down woody lignin and cellulose, cycling nutrients
- **Bioluminescent ecosystem indicator:** Foxfire glowing wood in temperate forests indicates *Armillaria* activity and decomposition
- **Carbon cycling:** As one of the most widespread forest pathogens, *Armillaria* species play a significant role in forest carbon dynamics, influencing carbon sequestration rates at the landscape level

## Cultivation

*Armillaria ostoyae* is not commercially cultivated. Other *Armillaria* species (particularly *A. mellea*) are occasionally cultivated in Asia (Japan, China) for food. The species is experimentally cultivated on logs but not at commercial scale.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Armillarin, armillarins**: sesquiterpene aryl esters; unique to *Armillaria*; cytotoxic in cancer cell lines
- **Protoilludane sesquiterpenes**: the structural class responsible for most *Armillaria* bioactivity
- **Polysaccharides**: basic immunomodulatory beta-glucans; not specifically studied compared to other medicinal species
- Limited pharmacological research compared to other medicinal fungi; commercial interest is low

## Culinary

Edible when **thoroughly cooked**. Raw or undercooked specimens cause gastrointestinal distress (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea) in many people — the toxins are heat-labile and destroyed by cooking. **Boiling water must be discarded.**

Traditional preparation:
- Blanch in boiling water for 5–10 minutes; discard water
- Then sauté, stir-fry, or incorporate into soups and stews
- Young caps are preferred; old, tough specimens are unpalatable

Flavor is mildly pleasant when cooked — earthy, slightly sweet. Popular foraged mushroom in Eastern Europe, where honey mushrooms (various *Armillaria* species) are a beloved autumn food. In Russia and Ukraine, *Armillaria* (опеньки, opyonki) are widely collected and preserved by pickling, salting, and drying.

**Caution:** Individual sensitivity varies considerably; always start with a small quantity the first time.
