## Identification

- **Cap:** 8–30 cm diameter; **reddish-brown to tan**; convex when young, flattening with age; surface smooth, slightly viscid when moist; sometimes with paler margin
- **Pores/Tubes:** No gills — instead, densely packed tubes opening as **circular white pores** (when young) maturing to **greenish-yellow**; 2–3 pores per mm; **do not stain blue when bruised** (critical ID character)
- **Stipe:** 8–25 cm × 2–7 cm; white to pale tan; broadened toward base when young; surface covered with fine **white reticulation** (net-like raised pattern), most prominent on upper portion
- **Flesh:** White; thick; **firm when young, becoming soft-spongy with age**; **does not change color when cut** (critical — no blue, red, or other color changes)
- **Spore print:** Olive-brown; spores spindle-shaped, 12–17 × 5–7 μm

**[[Dangerous Look-Alikes]]:**
- *Boletus satanas* (Satan's bolete): **poisonous**; cap white to gray; pores red; stipe red; flesh **turns blue immediately when cut** — the blue staining is the key danger signal for boletes
- *Tylopilus felleus* (bitter bolete): resembles porcini but flesh is intensely bitter; pores pink at maturity; reticulation more prominent and darker
- Any bolete with **blue-staining flesh + red pores** should be avoided

## Biology

Ectomycorrhizal with >30 host tree species across 15+ genera. Associates with: *Pinus, Picea, Abies, Tsuga, Fagus, Quercus, Castanea, Betula*. Also associated with pioneer shrub *Cistus ladanifer* in Mediterranean degraded scrubland. See [[Mycorrhizal Relationships]] for ecology detail.

Obligate ectomycorrhizal — **cannot be easily cultivated**. Commercial supply is wild-harvested. Fruiting triggered by rainfall during warm periods followed by autumn temperature drops and increased moisture.

First described by Bulliard in 1782. The "king bolete" — one of the most prized edible fungi in Europe, Italy, and increasingly globally.

## Cultivation

Cannot be cultivated commercially due to obligate ectomycorrhizal requirements. Attempts at inoculating host seedlings with *B. edulis* spores have produced occasional fruiting but no reproducible commercial protocol exists. Annual global commercial harvest estimated at **20,000–100,000 tons**. Prices typically $20–80/kg fresh; can reach $200+/kg in scarcity years.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **[[Beta-Glucans]]**: immunomodulatory polysaccharides
- **Ergothioneine**: potent antioxidant amino acid; porcini contains among the highest concentrations of any food
- **Ergosterol**: 500 mg/100g dried — vitamin D₂ precursor upon UV exposure
- **Copper**: 87% DV per 100g dried — exceptional concentration
- **B vitamins**: niacin, riboflavin, pantothenic acid
- **Zinc**: significant concentrations
- **Mannitol** and other polyols

**Nutrition (fresh):** ~3% fat, 7% protein, 9% carbohydrates; rich in B vitamins.

## Culinary

One of the world's most highly prized edible mushrooms, rivaling truffles in culinary status in Italy and France. Rich, complex, deeply savory umami flavor. Excellent fresh, dried, or preserved in oil.

**Fresh:** Slice and sauté in butter or olive oil; add to risotto, pasta, polenta. The caps can be grilled whole.

**Dried:** The drying process dramatically intensifies the flavor. Reconstitute in warm water (save the soaking liquid for stock — full of flavor). Essential for authentic Italian *porcini* pasta dishes, risotto *ai porcini*, and French *cèpe* preparations.

**Market forms:** Fresh (seasonal, premium), dried (year-round, widely available), frozen (reasonable quality), preserved in oil (traditional Italian).
