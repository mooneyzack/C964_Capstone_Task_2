## Identification

- **Cap:** 2–10 cm (button stage) up to 15–20 cm (portobello); white, cream, or brown; convex to flat; smooth; dry
- **Gills:** Free (not attached to stipe); **pink when young** (unique character among common edible species), maturing through beige to **chocolate brown** as spores develop — never white, distinguishing it from deadly *Amanita* species
- **Stipe:** 2–7 cm × 1–3 cm; white; solid; prominent membranous annulus (ring) from partial veil
- **Spore print:** Chocolate brown; spores 5–7.5 × 4–5 μm
- **Flesh:** White; does not change color when cut (distinguishing from the toxic *A. xanthodermus*, which turns chrome-yellow at stipe base)

**Unique genetic character:** **Basidiospores mostly 2-spored basidia** (the *bisporus* epithet) unlike the typical 4-spored basidia of most Agaricales; this creates mostly diploid spores, simplifying genetics and enabling **secondary homothallism** (self-fertility).

**All three market forms are the same species:**
- Button mushroom = small, immature, white cultivar
- Crimini/cremini = mature brown cultivar (same size as button)
- Portobello = fully mature brown cultivar, cap fully opened (15–20+ cm)

**[[Dangerous Look-Alikes]]:**
- *Amanita phalloides* (death cap): white gills (never pink), white spore print, cup (volva) at base, no color change of cap flesh. Deadly.
- *Amanita bisporigera* (destroying angel): similar; must check for pink/brown gills and chocolate spore print in *A. bisporus*
- *Agaricus xanthodermus* (yellow-staining mushroom): turns bright chrome-yellow at stipe base when scratched; has unpleasant inky/phenolic smell; causes gastrointestinal illness

## Biology

Saprotrophic on composted organic matter. Cultivation developed in 17th century France (cave cultivation on horse manure compost). Currently the **most cultivated mushroom in the world** (~38% of global production); grown in >70 countries.

The wild form (*A. bisporus* var. *bisporus*) is brown; the cultivated white strain (*A. bisporus* var. *albidus*) is a depigmented mutant that arose in cultivation. Described by Lange; transferred to *Agaricus* by Imbach in 1946.

The 2-spored basidium means spores contain two nuclei from the same meiotic event — typically one of each compatible mating type — enabling **secondary homothallism** (self-fertility).

## Cultivation

Requires a **casing layer** of peat-chalk mixture to initiate fruiting — the mechanism involves water potential changes and volatile signal molecules from casing layer microbiome. [[Substrates|Substrate]] is horse manure compost (Phase I and Phase II composting processes). Grown in climate-controlled facilities. [[Environmental Parameters]]: 16–18°C for fruiting, 85–90% RH. Multiple flushes possible from a single substrate block (typically 3–5 flushes). The most intensively studied cultivated mushroom with extensive genetic resources.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **[[Beta-Glucans]]**: primarily β-1,3 and β-1,6 glucans
- **Conjugated linoleic acid (CLA)**: minor amounts
- **Ergothioneine**: antioxidant amino acid
- **Agaritine**: a hydrazine derivative unique to *Agaricus* species; heat-labile (destroyed by cooking); low-level carcinogenicity in rodent studies at very high doses; no established risk at dietary levels
- **Vitamins**: good source of B vitamins (B2, B3, B5), vitamin D (especially after UV exposure)

## Culinary

The most commercially consumed mushroom globally. Mild, earthy flavor that intensifies with age (portobello has strongest flavor). The button stage is mild and versatile; crimini has more developed earthy flavor; portobello is meaty and rich, making it an excellent vegetarian burger patty when grilled whole.

All forms are excellent raw, sautéed, roasted, grilled, stuffed, or used in soups and sauces. Pairs well with virtually any ingredient. The dark gills of portobello can bleed dark color into sauces — scrape them out if a lighter color is desired.
