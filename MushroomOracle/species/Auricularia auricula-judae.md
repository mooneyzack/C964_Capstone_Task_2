# Auricularia auricula-judae (Wood Ear / Jelly Ear)

## Identification

- **Fruiting body:** Ear-shaped to irregular cup; gelatinous; **3–15 cm across**; remarkably resembles a human ear, including the convoluted interior surface (the inner concave surface bears the hymenium)
- **Color:** Brown to reddish-brown above; gray-brown to purplish below; paler at the margin; lighter in very young specimens
- **Texture:** Rubbery and gelatinous when fresh; hard and cartilaginous when dry; revives to full gelatinous state when rehydrated
- **Surface:** The outer (convex) surface is finely hairy (velvety under lens); the inner (concave) surface is smooth and bears the basidia
- **Attachment:** Attached at one point by a narrowed base to the substrate; sessile or nearly so
- **Spore print:** White; spores cylindrical to allantoid (sausage-shaped), 12–16 × 4–6 μm; basidia are divided by transverse septa (cylindrical basidia in 3 cells)

**Substrate clue:** The strong preference for **elder wood** (*Sambucus nigra* and relatives) is a key field identification aid in Europe and North America. In Asia, found on a wider variety of hardwoods.

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (jelly fungi, ear-shaped), [[Habitat Guide]] (elder, hardwood deadwood)*

## Biology

*Auricularia auricula-judae* is a **white-rot saprotrophic** fungus and a member of the order Auriculariales (jelly fungi). Unlike the Tremellales (which have tremelloid basidia), Auriculariales possess **cylindrical, transversely septate basidia**.

**Etymology and cultural history:**
- The species name *auricula-judae* means "Judas's ear" — a reference to the medieval legend that Judas Iscariot hanged himself on an elder tree (the species' preferred host), and the ear-shaped fruiting bodies are his ears. This legend gave rise to the old English name "Jew's Ear" (now considered offensive and replaced by "Wood Ear" or "Jelly Ear")
- The Chinese names 木耳 (mù ěr, "wood ear") and 云耳 (yún ěr, "cloud ear") are purely descriptive and free from cultural baggage

**Cultivation history:** *A. auricula-judae* is one of the **oldest cultivated mushrooms in the world**. Historical accounts from China describe cultivation as early as **600 AD** during the Tang Dynasty, predating the cultivation of *[[Lentinula edodes]]* (shiitake, ~1100 AD). Ancient Chinese texts describe scoring elder logs and placing them near natural elder populations to encourage colonization — a form of proto-inoculation.

**Taxonomy:** Previously *Auricularia auricula*, split from *A. polytricha* (cloud ear), which is primarily tropical and differs in texture and size. Some sources still treat these as synonyms or regional varieties.

*See also: [[Wood Decay]], [[Fungal Life Cycle]], [[Cultivation History]]*

## Cultivation

One of the most cultivated mushrooms in Asia; annual global production exceeds **700,000 metric tonnes** (one of the top five by volume). Cultivation is commercially mature and technically straightforward.

**Substrate:** Hardwood sawdust (oak, beech, ash, or mixed) with wheat bran or rice bran supplement; occasionally agricultural byproducts (cottonseed hulls, corncobs). Straw is less suitable (too low in lignin).

**Parameters:**
- Sterilize substrate at 121°C for 2–2.5 hours (or pasteurize at 90–95°C for 2–3 hours for bulk bags)
- Inoculation with grain spawn or liquid culture
- Colonization: 25–30°C; 60–75% RH; 20–30 days
- Fruiting: 18–26°C; 85–95% RH; indirect light; fresh air exchange is important
- Harvest: Cut fruiting bodies at base; typically 4–6 flushes per bag

**Home cultivation:** Among the most accessible cultivated mushrooms. Ready-to-fruit kits and pre-pasteurized substrate bags are widely available. Cold shock can trigger fruiting.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Auricularia polysaccharides (APS)**: primarily **acidic heteropolysaccharides** and **glucomannans**; β-1,3/1,6-linked backbone; immunomodulatory; anticoagulant
- **Anticoagulant compounds**: water-soluble polysaccharides from wood ear have demonstrated **in vitro and animal model anticoagulant activity** — inhibiting platelet aggregation and extending clotting time; mechanism involves inhibition of thromboxane A2 synthesis and downregulation of platelet glycoprotein IIb/IIIa
- **Ergosterol**: vitamin D₂ precursor
- **Adenosine**: contributes to anticoagulant and vasodilatory effects
- **Melanin-like pigments**: responsible for color; antioxidant properties

### Species-Specific Health Research

**Anticoagulant / cardiovascular:**
- Multiple in vitro studies demonstrate inhibition of platelet aggregation by *A. auricula-judae* polysaccharides
- A landmark 1980s study by Dale and Brisson (*New England Journal of Medicine*, letters) found consumption of raw Chinese black mushroom dishes caused measurable platelet aggregation inhibition in the researcher himself — a colorful anecdote that initiated formal research
- Subsequent animal studies confirm anticoagulant activity; **clinical human data remain sparse**
- **Caution:** Patients on anticoagulant medications (warfarin, aspirin) should exercise caution with high-dose wood ear consumption

**Blood lipid regulation:**
- Animal model studies suggest wood ear polysaccharides reduce total cholesterol and LDL while raising HDL
- Mechanisms proposed: reduction of hepatic lipogenesis, enhancement of LDL receptor expression
- Human evidence: limited; primarily traditional medicine observation and small uncontrolled trials

**Immunomodulation:**
- APS activates macrophage phagocytosis and NK cell activity in vitro
- Anti-inflammatory effects observed via NF-κB pathway inhibition

## Culinary

Wood ear is a **major culinary ingredient** across East and Southeast Asia, with a multi-thousand-year culinary tradition. The texture is paramount — gelatinous, slightly crunchy, and slippery — while the flavor is very mild (earthy, neutral).

**Classic preparations:**
- **Hot-and-sour soup (酸辣汤, Suān là tāng):** Wood ear provides its characteristic slippery texture; a standard ingredient alongside tofu, bamboo shoots, and egg
- **Moo shu pork (木须肉):** Stir-fried with pork, eggs, and lily buds — a northern Chinese classic
- **Cold sesame noodles and salads:** Rehydrated wood ear sliced thin and dressed with sesame oil, chili, soy, and vinegar
- **Korean cuisine:** 목이버섯 (mogiebeoseot) used in japchae (glass noodle stir-fry) and various banchan (side dishes)
- **Vietnamese cuisine:** Nấm mèo in bún bò Huế and various soups
- **Japanese cuisine:** キクラゲ (kikurage, "tree jellyfish") in ramen, especially Hakata-style tonkotsu ramen

**Preparation:** Dried wood ear must be soaked in cold or lukewarm water for 20–30 minutes (hot water makes them mushy). They expand dramatically (4–6×). Trim the tough attachment point. They can be eaten raw in salads or briefly cooked; long cooking softens the texture desirably in soups.

**Flavor profile:** Neutral, very mild; functions primarily as a textural element. Absorbs surrounding flavors (soy, sesame, vinegar) readily.

**Nutritional:** High in fiber (primarily polysaccharides); low in calories; provides iron and other minerals.
