## Identification

- **Fungal stroma:** Dark brown to black; typically **4–10 cm long**; cylindrical, slightly enlarged at the fertile head; emerges from the head of the dead host larva, extending above the soil surface
- **Host component:** Mummified moth larva (~3–5 cm); tan to pale brown; intact but filled with fungal mycelium
- **Combined structure:** The two-part structure (larval "caterpillar" body + fungal stroma) constitutes the traditional medicine and trade product

The Tibetan name **yartsa gunbu** (གཡར་རྩྭ་དགུན་འབུ) means "summer grass, winter worm" — describing the appearance of the emergent stroma (summer, grass-like) from the mummified larva (winter, worm-like).

**IUCN Status:** Vulnerable — habitat loss, climate change at high elevation, and over-harvesting are serious threats.

## Biology

**Full scientific name:** *Ophiocordyceps sinensis* (Berk.) G.H.Sung, J.M.Sung, Hywel-Jones & Spatafora, 2007  
Basionym: *Sphaeria sinensis* Berk., 1843; intermediate: *Cordyceps sinensis*

**Host specificity:** Parasitizes larvae of **ghost moths** (family Hepialidae), with 57 recognized host taxa primarily from genera *Thitarodes*, *Hepialus*, and relatives.

**Life cycle:**
1. Late summer: caterpillar larvae (5th instar) shed skin, becoming vulnerable
2. Fungal spores (or hyphae from infected soil) contact and invade the larva
3. Mycelium colonizes larval body, preserving it in position (head up) underground through winter
4. Spring: stroma emerges from the larval head, pushing through soil surface
5. Late summer following spring emergence: mature perithecia release ascospores to complete cycle

**Asci:** Cylindrical; **filiform, multiseptate ascospores**, 5–12 μm wide, fragmenting into secondary spores.

**Cannot be cultivated** — the complex life cycle requiring specific high-altitude ghost moth hosts and precise alpine conditions makes commercial cultivation impossible.

## Cultivation

Currently not commercially cultivable. The obligate parasitism of high-altitude ghost moth larvae in specific alpine conditions precludes cultivation. Various mycelium preparations sold as "Cordyceps sinensis" are typically *[[Cordyceps militaris]]* or *O. sinensis* vegetative mycelium grown on grain — these are distinct from the wild product.

## Bioactive Compounds

- **Note on cordycepin controversy**: A definitive 2017 biosynthetic study found *O. sinensis* does **NOT** produce cordycepin — earlier reports used unverified commercial material; cordycepin is produced by *[[Cordyceps militaris]]*
- Genuine compounds: polysaccharides, nucleosides (adenosine), amino acids, trace amounts of various metabolites
- Biological properties claimed in traditional medicine: immunomodulation, aphrodisiac, performance enhancement; clinical evidence remains inadequate

**Consumer warning:** Most commercial "Cordyceps sinensis" products do not contain authentic wild *O. sinensis*. Authentic wild material is extraordinarily expensive and in limited supply.

## Culinary

Used in traditional Tibetan and Chinese medicine, typically prepared by:
- Simmering in soups or broth (duck, chicken, or pork) — classical Chinese preparation
- Decoction in hot water
- Steeped in baijiu (Chinese spirits)
- Traditional Tibetan preparation varies by region

The food/medicinal preparation integrates the whole two-part fungus-insect structure into rich soups and stocks as a tonic preparation.

**Economic significance:**
- 1980s: 1,800 CNY/kg
- 2004: 36,000 CNY/kg
- 2013: 125,000–500,000 CNY/kg
- 2012 retail: up to $50,000/pound (top quality)
- Contributes ~40% of annual household cash income in rural Tibetan communities; 8.5% of regional GDP (2004 data)
- Collection rights disputes have led to violence, including 19 Nepalese villager convictions for murder (2011)
