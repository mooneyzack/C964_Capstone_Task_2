# *Rhizopus oligosporus* (Tempeh Mold)

## Identification

*R. oligosporus* is a zygomycete mold — not a mushroom. It produces no macroscopic fruiting body. In culture:

- **Colony morphology**: Rapidly growing, cottony white mycelium that darkens with age as sporangia mature to grey-black
- **Microscopy**: Sporangiophores arise from stolons opposite rhizoids; sporangia globose (100–200 µm diameter), dark brown to black; columella hemispherical; sporangiospores angular to subglobose, striate
- **Growth**: Very fast — visible colonization of substrate within 12–15 hours at optimal temperature

## Biology

*R. oligosporus* belongs to Mucoromycota (formerly part of the paraphyletic "Zygomycota" — see [[Minor Phyla]]). It reproduces primarily via asexual sporangiospores. Sexual reproduction via zygospores is rare in culture.

**Key biological features:**
- Produces a dense network of white, non-septate (coenocytic) hyphae
- Optimal growth at **30–35°C**, 85–90% RH
- Generates significant metabolic heat during active growth — temperature management required
- Produces **phytase**, which degrades phytic acid and increases mineral bioavailability
- Secretes proteases that degrade storage proteins to free amino acids and low-MW peptides
- Does **not** produce B12 on its own (see Cultivation notes)

## Cultivation

*R. oligosporus* is cultivated exclusively via solid-state fermentation on cooked legume or grain substrates.

**Standard tempeh process:**
1. Soybeans soaked, dehulled, and cooked
2. Cooled substrate inoculated with *R. oligosporus* spore powder
3. Packed into perforated containers (banana leaves traditionally; plastic bags with holes commercially)
4. Incubated at **30–35°C, 85–90% RH** for **24–48 hours**
5. Mycelium fully penetrates and binds substrate into a sliceable cake
6. Heat-killed by cooking for consumption

**Binding mechanism**: Mechanical — hyphae physically penetrate and envelop substrate particles, creating a continuous mycelial mat. Enzymatic softening of the substrate (proteolysis) further enhances structural integration.

See [[Tempeh and Rhizopus]] for detailed fermentation science and nutritional transformation data.

## Bioactive Compounds

*R. oligosporus* is valued for the nutritional transformation it drives during fermentation:

| Transformation | Magnitude |
|---------------|-----------|
| Trypsin inhibitor reduction | 64–67% |
| Phytate reduction | 29–33% |
| GABA production | ~21.4 mg/100g per 24h |
| Mineral bioavailability | Increased (Fe, Zn, Ca) |

**B12 co-fermentation**: When co-cultivated with *Propionibacterium freudenreichii*, B12 production reaches **8.26 ± 0.13 µg/100g wet weight** — significant for plant-based nutrition. Synergistic effects increased B12 to **1,230 ng/g dry weight** in lupin tempeh.

## Culinary

*R. oligosporus* is consumed as part of the tempeh matrix (mycelium + fermented substrate), not independently. Tempeh is:

- Sliced and pan-fried, deep-fried, grilled, or steamed
- A staple protein source in Indonesian cuisine
- Increasingly adopted globally as a whole-food plant-based protein
- Distinct from tofu (which is a precipitated protein curd, not a fermented product)

## Novel Substrates

Beyond soybean, research has validated 25+ substrates:

| Substrate | Notable Property |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Lupin (*Lupinus* spp.) | 32–53% protein; B12 co-fermentation study substrate |
| Mung bean | Exceptionally high polyphenols (137.53 mg GAE/g DM) |
| Cowpea | 32.6% phytate reduction |
| Cereals, algae, seeds | Several superior to soybean in specific nutritional metrics |
