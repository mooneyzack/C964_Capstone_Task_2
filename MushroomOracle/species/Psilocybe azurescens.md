## Identification

- **Cap:** 3–10 cm; convex to flat with a **pronounced, persistent broad umbo** (central mound); surface smooth, viscid with a separable gelatinous layer; **chestnut to ochraceous-brown to caramel when moist**, fading to light straw when dry; strongly hygrophanous; **bruises intensely blue-black** when damaged
- **Gills:** Sinuate to adnate; brown; close arrangement in two tiers; whitish edges
- **Stipe:** 9–20 cm × 3–6 mm; **notably long** relative to cap; silky white to dingy brown; hollow at maturity; twisted cartilaginous tissue; base thickens downward with coarse white aerial tufts and azure-colored base; rhizomorphs densely binding wood chips
- **Spore print:** Dark purplish-brown to purplish-black; spores 11–15 × 7–9 μm
- **Flavor:** Extremely bitter; odorless to starchy
- **Blueing reaction:** Extremely intense — immediately and distinctly blue-black throughout all tissues upon damage

*See also: [[Morphological Identification]] (bruising reactions, umbonate cap), [[Spore Prints]] (dark purple-brown print), [[Dangerous Look-Alikes]] (Galerina marginata), [[Foraging Safety]], [[Habitat Guide]] (coastal wood chip habitats)*

## Biology

Formally described by Stamets & Gartz in 1995. Saprotrophic; causes **[[Wood Decay|white rot]]** (wood whitening observed around mycelial mats). The dense rhizomorphic mycelium binds wood-chip substrates tenaciously.

Native range is strictly limited to the coastal Pacific Northwest. However, it has been cultivated internationally (Germany, Netherlands, UK, New Zealand, and multiple US states), expanding its range through intentional outdoor cultivation on wood chips in gardens and parks.

Cold and frost tolerant — one of the few psilocybin species that fruits during frost conditions (late September through January in the Pacific Northwest).

## Cultivation

Can be cultivated outdoors on [[Substrates|wood chip]] beds in appropriate climates. Harder to cultivate indoors than *[[Psilocybe cubensis]]*. Prefers slightly acidic, sandy, wood-chip-rich substrates. Outdoor beds in temperate climates can produce reliable autumn/winter crops. The dense rhizomorphic mycelium colonizes wood chips aggressively.

## Bioactive Compounds

**The most potent psilocybin mushroom widely documented.**

| Alkaloid | Range | Average |
|---|---|---|
| Psilocybin | up to 1.8% | ~1.1% |
| Psilocin | up to 0.5% | ~0.15% |
| Baeocystin | ~0.4% | ~0.4% |
| **Total** | **up to ~2.7%** | **~1.8%** |

Concentration approximately **2.5–3× that of *[[Psilocybe cubensis]]*** (0.37% psilocybin average). Psilocybin concentration decreases in older specimens.

Psilocybin is dephosphorylated to psilocin in the body; psilocin acts as a **5-HT2A serotonin receptor agonist**. See [[Psilocybin Research]] for clinical research context.

**"Wood lover's paralysis":** Anecdotal reports from consumers of temporary lower body paralysis or weakness, distinct from standard psilocybin effects; not fully explained; may relate to unidentified additional metabolites or combination effects.

## Culinary

Not applicable as a culinary mushroom. The flesh is extremely bitter. Consumed for psychoactive effects. Small cap size relative to potency requires careful dose estimation — the much higher alkaloid content versus *P. cubensis* has led to inadvertent overdoses by consumers unfamiliar with the potency difference.
