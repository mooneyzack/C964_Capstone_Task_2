# Mycelium Structure

## Overview

Mycelium is the vegetative body of fungi, composed of a network of thread-like filaments called hyphae. Its microscopic architecture — including septa, dolipore structures, and clamp connections — defines how fungi grow, transport nutrients, and maintain genetic integrity during reproduction.

## Details

### Hyphae

Hyphae (singular: hypha) are the thread-like tubular filaments that constitute the vegetative body of fungi. Characteristics:
- Diameter: typically 2–10 μm
- Growth mode: **apical extension** — new cell wall material is incorporated at the growing tip
- Branching: frequent, creating the three-dimensional mycelial network
- Density: a single cubic inch of substrate may contain **up to 8 miles of mycelial threads**
- Function: absorption, secretion of digestive enzymes, structural support, information integration

### Septa (Cross-walls)

Most hyphae are divided into cells by internal cross-walls called **septa** (singular: septum). Key types:

**Simple septa** (Ascomycota): thin cross-walls with a central pore (Woronin body-plugged in some) allowing cytoplasmic streaming and organelle movement between cells.

**Dolipore septa** (Basidiomycota): characterized by a barrel-shaped swelling around the central pore, flanked by membranous parenthesomes (curved sacs of endoplasmic reticulum). The dolipore allows nuclear migration in monokaryons and is diagnostic for Basidiomycota. Visible at 600× magnification.

**Aseptate hyphae** (coenocytic): present in Mucoromycota — septa are absent except where reproductive structures are isolated; the hypha is one long multinucleate tube.

### Clamp Connections

Clamp connections are **curved bridge-like outgrowths** at the septa between cells of dikaryotic hyphae in Basidiomycota. They function as a **cellular mechanism to ensure each new cell receives one nucleus from each parent** during cell division.

**Mechanism:**
1. Both nuclei at the septum divide simultaneously (conjugate division)
2. One daughter nucleus migrates into the developing clamp
3. The new septum forms, creating two cells
4. The clamp fuses with the cell below, delivering the nucleus that migrated into it
5. Result: each new cell contains exactly one nucleus of each mating type

Clamp connections are visible by light microscopy and their presence is a reliable indicator of a viable dikaryotic culture in cultivation. Their absence in a culture that should be dikaryotic may signal loss of fertility or contamination.
