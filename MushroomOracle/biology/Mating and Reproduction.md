# Mating and Reproduction

## Overview

Basidiomycete fungi have complex mating systems controlled by genetic loci that determine compatibility between individuals. Most species are self-incompatible and require genetically distinct partners for sexual reproduction.

## Details

### Heterothallism

The majority of basidiomycetes are **heterothallic** — they are self-incompatible and require genetically distinct partners for sexual reproduction. Most basidiomycetes exhibit **tetrapolar (bifactorial) heterothallism**, controlled by two unlinked mating-type loci:

- **MAT-A locus**: encodes homeodomain transcription factors that regulate nuclear recognition and dikaryotic development
- **MAT-B locus**: encodes pheromones and pheromone receptors controlling plasmogamy (cytoplasmic fusion)

For mating to succeed, the two mycelia must differ at **both** loci. With multiple alleles at each locus (often dozens), the number of mating types can be enormous: *Coprinellus disseminatus* has been estimated to have over **20,000 mating types**. In a typical species with 3 alleles at each locus, there are 9 mating types, each compatible with 4 others (44% compatibility rate). Tetrapolar systems maximize outbreeding.

**Bipolar (unifactorial) heterothallism** is less common — only a single MAT locus controls compatibility, yielding ~50% compatibility rates.

### Homothallism

**Homothallic** fungi are self-fertile — a single spore or mycelium can complete the sexual cycle without a partner. Mechanisms include:
- **Primary homothallism**: a single genome expresses both MAT1-1 and MAT1-2 idiomorphs
- **Secondary homothallism** (pseudohomothallism): two compatible nuclei are packaged into a single spore during meiosis

Evolutionary analysis shows that the transition from heterothallism to homothallism has occurred at least **31 times** independently in fungi, vs. only ~3 reverse transitions. Homothallism benefits colonization of new substrates (no partner required) but reduces genetic diversity.
