Wood is composed of three interlocked structural polymers: cellulose (40–50%), hemicellulose (20–35%), and lignin (15–35%). Lignin — an aromatic polymer of phenylpropanoid units — is the most recalcitrant component and serves as structural reinforcement throughout the plant cell wall. White-rot fungi are uniquely equipped to completely mineralize all three components, including lignin, through a specialized enzymatic arsenal: lignin peroxidases (LiP), manganese peroxidases (MnP), versatile peroxidases (VP), and laccases (copper-containing oxidases). This combination of high-redox-potential peroxidases and oxidases allows white-rot fungi to cleave the aromatic bonds in lignin that no other organism can efficiently attack.

White-rot fungi evolved during the late Permian period, approximately 250 million years ago — a timing that coincides strikingly with the end of the Carboniferous coal-forming era. Before white-rot lignin degradation evolved, dead plant matter accumulated for millions of years because nothing could break it down efficiently. The great coal deposits of Pennsylvania, the UK, and elsewhere represent organic matter that accumulated during this pre-white-rot era. After the evolution of white-rot enzymatic systems, carbon began cycling through ecosystems rather than accumulating as geological deposits — a transformation of global biogeochemical significance. (Floudas et al., 2012, Science, "The Paleozoic origin of enzymatic lignin decomposition reconstructed from 31 fungal genomes.")

Well-known white-rot species include Trametes versicolor (turkey tail), Pleurotus ostreatus (oyster mushroom), Ganoderma spp. (reishi relatives), and Hericium erinaceus (lion's mane). By contrast, brown-rot fungi degrade cellulose and hemicellulose but cannot efficiently degrade lignin — they evolved independently from white-rot ancestors through convergent gene loss, and their modified lignin residues contribute substantially to stable humic soil organic matter in boreal forests.

**Key evidence:**
- White-rot fungi: only organisms capable of efficiently degrading lignin via LiP, MnP, VP, and laccase enzymes
- Evolved in late Permian (~250 million years ago) — end of Carboniferous coal-forming era
- Floudas et al. (2012, Science): Paleozoic origin of enzymatic lignin decomposition from 31 fungal genomes
- Without white-rot fungi, lignin accumulates indefinitely — the mechanism that created coal deposits
- Brown-rot fungi evolved multiple times independently from white-rot ancestors via gene loss; cannot degrade lignin efficiently
- White-rot examples: Trametes versicolor, Pleurotus ostreatus, Ganoderma spp., Hericium erinaceus

**Context for content creators:**
This is one of the most genuinely awe-inspiring facts in mycology — fungi literally changed the carbon cycle of the entire planet. The Carboniferous coal connection is scientifically supported and highly communicable. Key nuances: the "coal gap" hypothesis (that Carboniferous coal formed because nothing could decompose lignin) is well-supported but not universally unchallenged — some researchers point to climate and burial conditions as co-factors. The timing correlation is real and documented in the Floudas et al. Science paper. This fact works well as a "scale of impact" hook: "fungi may have ended the age of coal formation." Avoid stating this as absolute certainty — say "evidence suggests" or "is associated with."
