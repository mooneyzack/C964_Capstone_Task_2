R. Gordon Wasson and his Russian-born wife Valentina Pavlovna Wasson developed the mycophobia/mycophilia framework from a personal observation: on their honeymoon in the Catskills in 1927, she enthusiastically gathered wild mushrooms from the forest; he hung back in alarm. This cultural difference between a Russian and an Anglo-American response to wild mushrooms led to decades of cross-cultural research and their 1957 book *Mushrooms, Russia and History*.

Wasson proposed that mycophilic cultures — Russia, Poland, Czech Republic, France, Italy, Scandinavia — have deep foraging traditions, affectionate names for mushrooms, and integrate fungi into cuisine, art, and folklore. Mycophobic cultures — primarily England and the Anglo-Saxon sphere — treat wild mushrooms with deep suspicion, use the pejorative "toadstool" for any unfamiliar wild fungus, and historically associated fungi with death, witchcraft, and the devil. Wasson attributed the Anglo-Saxon mycophobia partly to the suppression of pre-Christian mushroom traditions by the Church.

The framework has been critiqued as an oversimplification: attitudes vary within cultures by class, region, era, and species. Even in "mycophobic" England, working-class communities preserved some foraging knowledge; even in "mycophilic" Russia, urban populations rely increasingly on markets. A spectrum model is now preferred. Nevertheless, the cross-cultural contrast in mushroom attitudes is genuinely pronounced and the basic dichotomy remains useful as a conceptual anchor.

**Key evidence:**
- Wasson coined "ethnomycology" and "mycophobia/mycophilia" in *Mushrooms, Russia and History* (1957) with Valentina Pavlovna Wasson
- Russia: *gribniki* (mushroom gatherers) is a recognized social identity; Soviet-era public health posters promoted safe identification; autumn foraging is near-universal cultural practice
- England: "toadstool" as pejorative; historically associated wild mushrooms with poverty, witchcraft, fairies; very limited culinary tradition for wild species until recent decades
- Modern shifts: gourmet cuisine, health food movement, psychedelic renaissance, and the 2019 documentary *Fantastic Fungi* have significantly shifted Anglo-American attitudes toward fungi
- Critique: Wasson's own methodology and the binary framework are contested; the correlation between prehistoric religious use and modern cultural attitudes has little direct evidence

**Context for content creators:**
The mycophobia/mycophilia framework is excellent contextual content for explaining why mushroom curiosity is experiencing a cultural renaissance in previously mycophobic cultures. Caveat: present Wasson's hypothesis about prehistoric cult suppression as a "theory" or "speculation" — there is no direct archaeological evidence. The cultural attitudes themselves are real and well-documented; the historical explanation is more speculative.
