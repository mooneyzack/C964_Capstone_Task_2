María Sabina (c. 1894–1985) was a Mazatec curandera (healer) from Huautla de Jiménez, Oaxaca, Mexico — one of the most celebrated practitioners of the traditional Mazatec velada, an all-night healing ceremony in which the healer consumes sacred psilocybin mushrooms (teonanácatl, "flesh of the gods") to enter a dialogue with the spirit world on behalf of a patient. The mushrooms are sacred intermediaries in Mazatec cosmology, not recreational substances.

In 1955, R. Gordon Wasson, a J.P. Morgan vice president and amateur mycologist, became (reportedly) the first outsider to attend a velada ceremony with María Sabina's permission. His 1957 Life magazine article, "Seeking the Magic Mushroom" (May 13, 1957), brought this experience to a mainstream American audience for the first time. The article triggered a wave of Western seekers who descended on Huautla de Jiménez — thousands came over the following years, disrupting the community, transforming a sacred practice into a tourist commodity, and exposing Sabina to severe local backlash. Her home was burned down. She was ostracized by community members who blamed her for the disruption.

María Sabina's own account of the aftermath, recorded in interviews, included the words: "From the moment the foreigners arrived, the saint children lost their purity. They lost their force; the strangers spoiled them." This case became a defining example of ethnomycological ethics — the collision between Western scientific curiosity and indigenous sovereignty over sacred knowledge.

**Key evidence:**
- Wasson attended the velada in June 1955; the Life article was published May 13, 1957
- Wasson coined the term "ethnomycology" and went on to write *Soma: Divine Mushroom of Immortality* (1968)
- María Sabina's home was burned down; she lived in poverty and relative ostracism in her final years despite her international fame
- The velada tradition was disrupted by tourism; the sacred context was profoundly compromised
- Roger Heim, French mycologist who accompanied Wasson, formally described *Psilocybe mexicana* from specimens collected during these visits

This story is important for the psychedelic renaissance context — it connects modern psilocybin research to indigenous cultural roots and raises ongoing questions about who benefits from indigenous knowledge. Caveat: present with sensitivity; this is not a triumphant story of discovery but one of extraction and harm.
