*Amanita muscaria* (fly agaric) is the archetypal "poisonous mushroom" of Western folklore — red cap, white spots, deadly appearance. In reality, it is rarely fatal to adults when consumed accidentally in typical quantities; the primary active compounds are ibotenic acid and muscimol (not amatoxins). However, it causes an unpleasant and potentially dangerous intoxication syndrome: nausea, vomiting, dizziness, disorientation, visual disturbances, and in severe cases, seizures and coma.

Crucially, ibotenic acid and muscimol are highly water-soluble. Parboiling — placing sliced or chopped fly agaric in boiling water for 10–15 minutes, then discarding the water and reboiling — removes the majority of these compounds into the cooking liquid. Traditional Siberian food preparation documented by European explorers included this detoxification step. In Japan (particularly in parts of Nagano prefecture), *A. muscaria* is consumed after thorough salting and repeated washing — a process that similarly leaches water-soluble toxins.

The result is a mushroom with a mild, slightly fruity flavor when properly prepared. It is important to note that this preparation does not eliminate all toxicity; muscarine (a different toxin, cholinergic in mechanism) is also water-soluble but present in much lower concentrations than in genuinely dangerous muscarine-bearing species like *Inocybe* or *Clitocybe*. The detoxification is real but not absolute, and individual sensitivity varies.

**Key evidence:**
- Ibotenic acid and muscimol: water-soluble; substantially removed by boiling and discarding cooking water
- Traditional practice: documented in Siberian ethnographic records and in Japanese regional cuisine (Nagano prefecture)
- Parboiling protocol: typically 10–15 min boil, discard water, repeat 1–2 times; salt-washing also used in Japanese preparation
- Remaining toxins: muscarine present but at low levels; individual response varies
- Not considered safe for home preparation without prior experience; the safety margin is narrow
- *A. muscaria* is classified as toxic and not recommended for foraging in Western food safety guidance

**Context for content creators:**
This challenges the "always deadly" narrative around fly agaric but requires careful framing. Caveat: "detoxification by parboiling" is real and practiced in specific cultural contexts, but it is NOT a universal recommendation. Do not encourage people to try preparing fly agaric at home — the margin between reduced toxicity and dangerous intoxication is variable and unpredictable. Present as cultural/historical context, not as practical cooking advice.
