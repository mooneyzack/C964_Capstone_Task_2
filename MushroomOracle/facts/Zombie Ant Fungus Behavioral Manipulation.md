*Ophiocordyceps unilateralis* (the zombie ant fungus) is an entomopathogenic ascomycete that parasitizes *Camponotus* carpenter ants in tropical and subtropical forests. The infection follows a precise sequence: conidia (spores) contact the ant's cuticle, germinate, and penetrate. The fungus proliferates through the ant's body, consuming lipids and proteins. As the infection progresses, the ant undergoes a dramatic behavioral change: it abandons its nest and colony territory, climbs nearby vegetation to a height of approximately 25 cm above ground (specifically optimal for humidity levels that support spore germination and for airflow patterns that maximize spore dispersal distance), then bites down on a leaf vein with maximum mandibular force and dies — locked in position by the fungus inducing tetanus-like muscular contractions.

Remarkably, histological analysis (de Bekker et al., 2014, *BMC Evolutionary Biology*) shows that the ant's brain is largely free of fungal invasion — the fungus concentrates in muscles, hemolymph, and connective tissue. The behavioral manipulation appears to be achieved pharmacologically: fungal metabolites (including specific alkaloids and sphingolipids) act on the musculature and peripheral nervous system, not through direct brain colonization. The ant is being remote-controlled through chemistry, not overwritten.

The precise height specification varies slightly by geographic locality and forest layer — different populations of the fungus have tuned their manipulation to local microclimatic optima. Multiple *Ophiocordyceps* species infect different ant hosts and host body parts, each with distinct behavioral manipulation outcomes.

**Key evidence:**
- Behavior: infected ants climb to ~25 cm height, bite leaf veins, die in position — all within hours of each other, creating "graveyards" of zombie ants
- Brain largely uninvaded by fungal hyphae — manipulation is pharmacological/peripheral
- Specific alkaloids and sphingolipids identified in metabolomics studies as candidate manipulation compounds
- The biting height is not random — experiments moving infected ants to different heights result in failed sporulation
- Ancient fossil evidence: amber-preserved leaf tissue showing the characteristic bite marks has been dated to ~48 million years ago (*Proceedings of the Royal Society B*, 2010)

**Context for content creators:**
This is one of the most compelling animal behavior stories in nature — widely known from documentaries. Caveat: "zombie" is a metaphor; the ant is not reanimated after death, but manipulated before it. The mechanism is still being characterized — "largely leaving the brain intact" is accurate as of current research but the full picture remains an active area of investigation.
