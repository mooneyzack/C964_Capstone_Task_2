Fresh mushrooms are 85–95% water by weight. When placed in a cold or crowded pan, they release this water as steam, dropping the pan temperature below the 140–150°C threshold required for the Maillard reaction. The result is watery, pale, bland mushrooms with a boiled texture. The dry sauté method solves this by driving off surface and cellular moisture before fat is introduced.

The technique: heat a wide, heavy-bottomed pan (stainless steel or cast iron — nonstick cannot reach the necessary temperature) until a drop of water evaporates immediately. Add mushrooms in a single layer without crowding. Do not stir for the first 1–2 minutes. Once one side has browned and visible moisture has escaped, stir or flip. Only when the moisture has substantially evaporated should butter or oil be added — at which point fat contact with hot, dry mushroom surfaces instantly triggers Maillard browning and caramelization. Salt should be added after browning, not before, because salt draws additional moisture out and delays the browning process.

The principle applies to all methods: roasting requires a single layer on a bare metal sheet (no parchment, which traps steam) at 200°C+; grilling requires the highest heat zone; even in soups, pre-browning mushrooms before adding liquid pays enormous dividends in flavor depth.

**Key evidence:**
- Mushrooms are 85–95% water by fresh weight — managing this moisture is the central challenge of all mushroom cookery
- Maillard reaction requires surface temperatures above ~140°C; steam prevents surfaces from exceeding 100°C
- Crowding drops pan temperature, causing steaming; single-layer cooking maintains high surface contact temperature
- Salt timing: early salt draws cellular moisture out via osmosis, further impeding browning
- This principle holds across species from oyster to portobello — the timing varies, not the principle
