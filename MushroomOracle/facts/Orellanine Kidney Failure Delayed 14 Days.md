Orellanine is a bipyridyl N-oxide nephrotoxin found in *Cortinarius rubellus* (deadly webcap) and *C. orellanus* (fool's webcap), two brown, forest-floor mushrooms widespread in European and North American coniferous and mixed forests. Its defining danger is the extreme latency of symptoms: most victims experience no symptoms for 2–14 days after ingestion, during which time the toxin is silently destroying renal proximal tubule cells through free radical-mediated oxidative damage, leading to progressive, irreversible renal interstitial fibrosis.

When symptoms eventually appear, they are non-specific: fatigue, headache, nausea, chills — often mistaken for influenza or a stomach bug. By this time, significant irreversible renal damage has already occurred. As damage progresses: intense thirst, polyuria (excessive urination), flank pain, then oliguria (decreased urine output), rising creatinine and BUN, and uremia. Dialysis is required in severe cases; kidney transplant is a common outcome in cases with severe exposure. Orellanine is heat-stable — cooking does not destroy it.

The species are not visually distinctive or easy to identify without expertise. *Cortinarius rubellus* can resemble chanterelles at a glance (both are orange-brown autumn forest mushrooms) and young specimens may still have the characteristic cobweb-like cortina on the stipe. The rust-brown spore print is diagnostic for all *Cortinarius* species — distinguishing them from edible chanterelles, which print white to pale cream.

**Key evidence:**
- Latency: symptoms appear 2–14 days post-ingestion — longest latency of any mushroom toxin
- Mechanism: orellanine → free radical generation → oxidative destruction of proximal tubule cells → irreversible fibrosis
- Orellanine is heat-stable: boiling/cooking does not reduce toxicity
- Outcome: dialysis required in many cases; kidney transplant in severe cases; low mortality with modern renal care but high permanent morbidity
- *Cortinarius* genus: >1,000 species; rust-brown spore print (from cortina) is genus-level diagnostic character
- Primary look-alike danger: chanterelles (*Cantharellus cibarius*) — orange-brown autumn forest mushrooms sharing habitat; separated by false gills vs. true gills and rust-brown vs. pale spore print

**Context for content creators:**
The delayed onset is the most important safety communication point — victims feel fine for days and may not connect their illness to mushroom consumption. This makes standard "watch and wait" advice dangerously inadequate. Caveat: deaths from orellanine poisoning are relatively rare with modern dialysis access; the consequence is more often permanent kidney damage and transplant than fatality. Do not understate severity in attempting to avoid alarming the public — this is a serious, consequence-bearing toxin.
