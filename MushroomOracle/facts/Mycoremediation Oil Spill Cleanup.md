White-rot fungi evolved enzymes (lignin peroxidase, manganese peroxidase, laccase) to attack lignin — an irregular aromatic polymer with no repeating unit. Because these enzymes cannot rely on substrate specificity, they attack any molecule with similar aromatic ring chemistry. Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) — the toxic, carcinogenic components of petroleum and diesel — have exactly this chemistry, making them susceptible to the same enzymatic machinery.

In a landmark field demonstration by Paul Stamets and colleagues at Fungi Perfecti (Bellingham, Washington, 2005), four large piles of diesel-contaminated soil were treated with: oyster mushroom mycelium (*Pleurotus ostreatus*), indigenous bacteria, standard bioremediation bacteria, and left untreated as control. After eight weeks, the oyster mushroom pile had reduced PAH concentrations by approximately 95%, transforming dark, tarry, near-sterile soil into rich, dark substrate colonized by mushrooms. Insects, birds, and other invertebrates returned to the mushroom pile — absent from the others — suggesting complete ecological restoration. The bacterial piles showed moderate reduction; the control pile showed negligible change.

The mechanism is oxidative ring-opening: peroxidases and laccases attack aromatic rings, generating unstable radicals that initiate chain reactions ultimately mineralizing PAHs to CO₂ and water. White-rot fungi are the only organisms with the enzymatic power to mineralize high-molecular-weight PAHs efficiently.

**Key evidence:**
- The Stamets diesel field experiment (2005) showed ~95% PAH reduction in 8 weeks for the oyster mushroom pile
- Mechanism: same lignin peroxidase/manganese peroxidase/laccase system that evolved for wood decay
- *Pleurotus ostreatus* is most deployable: grows rapidly on agricultural waste substrates; can be self-sustaining in field conditions
- *Phanerochaete chrysosporium* produces highest lignin peroxidase activity of any known organism but does not fruit in field conditions — primarily a lab model
- Limitation: the Stamets experiment was not a peer-reviewed controlled study; subsequent formal research has confirmed the mechanism but field efficacy varies with PAH type, concentration, soil matrix, and climate

**Context for content creators:**
The image of oyster mushrooms growing from diesel-soaked soil is powerful. The 95% figure is from a well-documented but non-peer-reviewed field demonstration — should be described as a "field demonstration" not a "clinical trial." Peer-reviewed laboratory and mesocosm studies broadly support the mechanism and general efficacy; the specific field conditions matter greatly.
