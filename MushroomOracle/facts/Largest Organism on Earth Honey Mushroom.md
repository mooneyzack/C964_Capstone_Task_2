In 2000, researchers from the US Forest Service published a study in *Nature* documenting a single genetic individual of *Armillaria ostoyae* (honey mushroom) in Oregon's Malheur National Forest, identified by DNA fingerprinting of mycelial samples across a 9.65 km² area. All samples matched a single genotype — one organism. The individual is estimated to be between 2,400 and 8,650 years old (with 8,000 years as a frequently cited median estimate) based on growth rate extrapolations.

*Armillaria* species are both saprotrophic and parasitic — they kill living trees by colonizing roots and spreading through the soil as dark "rhizomorphs" (mycelial cords protected by a melanized outer layer). The Malheur individual has been killing conifers in this forest throughout its multi-millennial lifespan. It is not a dormant or dead entity — the mycelium remains actively growing, and fresh honey mushroom fruiting bodies emerge from the area each autumn.

Before the Oregon discovery, the largest known organism was a *Armillaria bulbosa* individual in the Upper Peninsula of Michigan (discovered 1992), covering ~15 hectares with a 1,500-year age estimate. The Oregon individual vastly exceeded this. By mass, the largest organism remains arguably the Pando aspen clone in Utah (~6,000 tonnes of interconnected trees), though the Armillaria's underground mycelial mass per unit area is significant.

**Key evidence:**
- Documented in *Nature* (Ferguson et al., 2003; Anderson et al., 2000) via DNA genotyping of 112 *Armillaria* mycelial samples across the forest
- Area: ~9.65 km² (2,385 acres); equivalent to ~1,665 football fields
- Age estimate: 2,400–8,650 years based on growth rate studies in controlled conditions
- Organism remains alive and actively growing — annual honey mushroom fruiting
- *Armillaria* spreads via rhizomorphs (melanized mycelial cords) through soil, colonizing roots of living and dead trees

**Context for content creators:**
"Largest living organism" is a perennially engaging hook. Caveat: "largest" depends on the metric — by area, the Malheur *Armillaria*; by mass, the Pando aspen clone is competitive. The age estimate is an extrapolation from growth rates, not a direct measurement, and carries significant uncertainty. The organism is not a forest steward — it is actively killing trees in its territory.
