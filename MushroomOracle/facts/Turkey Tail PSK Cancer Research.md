PSK (Polysaccharide-K, also marketed as Krestin) is a protein-bound polysaccharide extract derived from a specific strain of Trametes versicolor (turkey tail) — historically referred to as Coriolus versicolor CM-101 in the clinical literature. It consists of approximately 25–38% protein with the remainder composed of beta-1,3/1,6-glucans. PSK has been approved as a prescription drug adjunct to cancer chemotherapy in Japan since 1977, making it one of the longest-standing government-approved mushroom-derived pharmaceuticals.

Multiple large-scale randomized controlled trials, predominantly conducted in Japan, have demonstrated improved survival outcomes when PSK is added to chemotherapy regimens for Stage I–III colorectal and gastric cancer (typically at 3g/day oral dose). Meta-analyses of these trials consistently show survival benefit in treated populations. PSK stimulates NK cell activity and promotes IL-2 and IFN-γ production, activating immune cells via Dectin-1, TLR2/4, and CR3 pathways.

PSP (Polysaccharopeptide), a related but distinct protein-polysaccharide developed in China from the same species, has shown comparable immunomodulatory effects in Chinese clinical trials and has been used in colorectal cancer treatment. In the United States, a Phase 1 dose-finding trial (NCT01857895) was funded at UCSF — directly resulting from a 2012 TEDx talk by Paul Stamets — examining turkey tail as an immune supplement following conventional breast cancer treatment, with findings showing faster immune reconstitution in the turkey tail group.

**Key evidence:**
- PSK approved as prescription cancer adjuvant in Japan since 1977 (oral, 3g/day)
- Multiple Japanese RCTs: improved 5-year survival in Stage I–III colorectal and gastric cancer
- Mechanism: Dectin-1, TLR2/4, CR3 activation; enhanced NK cell activity; IL-2 and IFN-γ induction
- PSP: prebiotic activity confirmed in 2014 RCT (Pallav et al., Gut Microbes) — increased Lactobacillus and Bifidobacterium
- US FDA-funded Phase 1 trial (NCT01857895, UCSF): improved immune reconstitution in breast cancer patients

**Context for content creators:**
This is one of the strongest evidence stories in medicinal mycology — government approval and RCT data are compelling anchors. Key nuances: PSK is approved in Japan, not by the FDA in the US (it is not a prescription drug in the US). The clinical trials are predominantly Japanese, conducted on Japanese populations, using chemotherapy protocols that may differ from Western practice. Always clarify "approved in Japan" rather than "approved" without qualifier. PSK is not simply "turkey tail" — it is a specific extract from a specific strain; quality of commercial turkey tail products varies widely.
