*Auricularia auricula-judae* (wood ear, black fungus, cloud ear) is one of the most widely consumed mushrooms globally — a staple of Chinese, Japanese, Korean, and Southeast Asian cuisines. It is particularly prominent in dishes like hot-and-sour soup, moo shu pork, and various stir-fries. In Traditional Chinese Medicine, it has been used since at least the Tang Dynasty as a blood tonic and cardiovascular herb, with the gelatinous texture associated with "nourishing and lubricating the blood."

Modern research has documented antiplatelet activity — the mushroom contains compounds that inhibit platelet aggregation (the clumping process that initiates blood clot formation). An adenosine component appears to play a role, as adenosine is a well-characterized endogenous platelet inhibitor. The polysaccharides may also contribute through as-yet-incompletely-characterized mechanisms. This activity has been observed in both in vitro platelet aggregation assays and in some human dietary studies.

The clinical significance is that excessive consumption of wood ear (particularly in extract or supplement form, or very large dietary quantities) can potentiate the effects of anticoagulant drugs (warfarin, aspirin, heparin, clopidogrel) and increase bleeding risk. This is particularly relevant before surgery. The Taiwan Food and Drug Administration has documented the anticoagulant properties in consumer safety communications.

**Key evidence:**
- Antiplatelet activity: documented in human platelet aggregation studies and dietary intervention studies
- Mechanism: adenosine component + polysaccharide fractions; inhibits ADP- and collagen-induced platelet aggregation
- Clinical relevance: patients on warfarin or other anticoagulants should be aware; pre-surgical caution
- Culinary consumption at normal dietary quantities is not generally considered dangerous for healthy individuals
- The same TCM "blood-thinning" traditional indication has been validated mechanistically — a relatively unusual case of TCM claim aligning with modern pharmacology

**Context for content creators:**
The convergence of ancient TCM blood-thinning tradition and modern pharmacological validation is compelling. Caveat: the antiplatelet effect is clinically relevant primarily for people on anticoagulant medications or pre-surgery — dietary consumption in typical Asian cooking quantities is not a safety concern for healthy adults. Do not alarm general consumers about eating hot-and-sour soup.
