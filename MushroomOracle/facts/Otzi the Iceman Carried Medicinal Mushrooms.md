Ötzi the Iceman, found preserved in a glacier on the Austro-Italian border in 1991, is dated to approximately 3300 BCE — making his possession of medicinal fungi among the oldest documented examples of intentional human use of fungi as medicine.

Analysis of Ötzi's possessions and tissues revealed two fungal species. *Fomes fomentarius* (tinder polypore, horse hoof fungus) was found in a leather pouch. This bracket fungus produces a spongy inner layer (amadou) historically used as tinder, wound dressing, and styptic (bleeding stopper) across Eurasia. Amadou's absorbent, slightly antiseptic properties make it mechanistically plausible as a wound dressing — a use documented in European folk medicine from antiquity through the twentieth century.

*Piptoporus betulinus* (birch polypore) was found threaded on a leather cord. Analysis of Ötzi's gut contents revealed active infection with intestinal parasites (*Trichuris trichiura*, whipworm). Birch polypore contains agaric acid (a laxative) and other compounds with documented antiparasitic properties in laboratory studies. Several researchers have proposed that Ötzi was intentionally self-medicating with the birch polypore — which would make this potentially the world's oldest documented case of rational medicinal mushroom use: a Neolithic person carrying what appear to be pharmaceutical tools to treat a known disease.

**Key evidence:**
- *Fomes fomentarius*: found in leather pouch; documented wound dressing and styptic use across Eurasia; amadou has antibacterial properties (in vitro)
- *Piptoporus betulinus*: found threaded on cord; agaric acid (laxative); antiparasitic compounds identified in laboratory studies
- Gut parasite: *Trichuris trichiura* eggs confirmed in intestinal contents — active infection at time of death
- Alternative interpretation: the fungi may have been carried for fire-starting (tinder) and their proximity to the parasite may be coincidental — the self-medication interpretation is plausible but not proven
- Ötzi also carried other plant materials suggesting intentional pharmacopoeia

**Context for content creators:**
"5,000-year-old medicine kit" is a compelling framing that grounds modern interest in medicinal mushrooms in very deep human history. Caveat: the self-medication interpretation is the most parsimonious explanation but is circumstantial — the birch polypore could have had other uses.
