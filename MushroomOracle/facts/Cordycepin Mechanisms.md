Cordycepin (3'-deoxyadenosine) is the signature bioactive nucleoside of Cordyceps militaris, first isolated in 1950. Its pharmacological identity stems from a single structural difference from adenosine: the absence of the 3'-hydroxyl group. This gap allows cordycepin to be incorporated into RNA as a pseudosubstrate for adenosine, but because no subsequent nucleotide can be attached at the 3' position, RNA chain elongation terminates. This chain-termination mechanism underlies several of cordycepin's pharmacological activities simultaneously: inhibition of mRNA polyadenylation (blocking protein synthesis in target cells), direct antitumor activity via apoptosis induction in cancer cell lines, and antiviral activity through inhibition of viral RNA replication.

On the anti-inflammatory side, cordycepin inhibits LPS-induced NF-κB activation and suppresses pro-inflammatory cytokines (TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6). It also suppresses T-cell activation, with potential relevance to COPD therapy, and has shown bronchodilatory and anti-inflammatory effects in airway tissue in animal studies. The cardiovascular and athletic performance effects are proposed to occur through adenosine-like vasodilation and potential enhancement of cellular ATP production via effects on nucleotide pools.

In human clinical evidence, a double-blind RCT (Hirsch et al., 2017, PMC5236007) with 28 participants found that 4g/day of a C. militaris mushroom blend for 3 weeks improved VO₂max by +4.8 ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ (p=0.042) versus +0.9 ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ in placebo — a statistically significant improvement in maximal oxygen consumption. An important correction for the supplement market: wild Ophiocordyceps sinensis (caterpillar fungus) does NOT contain significant cordycepin — confirmed by a 2017 biosynthetic study. Nearly all modern supplement research and production uses cultivated C. militaris.

**Key evidence:**
- Missing 3'-OH group → RNA chain termination → apoptosis induction, mRNA polyadenylation inhibition
- Anti-inflammatory: inhibits NF-κB activation; suppresses TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6
- VO₂max RCT (Hirsch et al., 2017): +4.8 ml·kg⁻¹·min⁻¹ vs. +0.9 placebo after 3 weeks (N=28, p=0.042)
- Antiviral: same chain-termination mechanism inhibits viral RNA replication
- Wild O. sinensis does NOT contain significant cordycepin (confirmed 2017)
- Most cordycepin-specific research is preclinical; athletic performance data is the strongest human evidence

**Context for content creators:**
Cordycepin's mechanism is elegant and scientifically interesting — the "missing hydroxyl" angle is a concrete molecular hook. The VO₂max RCT is real and statistically significant, though the study is small (N=28) and used a 3-week protocol; results should be contextualized as promising, not definitive. Most cancer and antiviral cordycepin research is preclinical. The O. sinensis vs. C. militaris distinction is critical for supplement education — many premium-priced "cordyceps" products use O. sinensis mycelium, which lacks the key bioactive. Content creators should clarify: look for C. militaris or verified cordycepin content.
