*Chlorophyllum molybdites* (green-spored parasol, also called the "vomiter" or false parasol) causes more cases of mushroom poisoning in North America than any other species. It is a large, attractive, parasol-shaped mushroom that grows on lawns, parks, and disturbed ground — exactly the environments where people feel safe picking mushrooms. Its toxin (not fully characterized) causes rapid-onset severe gastrointestinal illness within 1–3 hours: explosive vomiting, profuse diarrhea, and abdominal cramping. While rarely fatal in healthy adults, it regularly causes hospitalizations.

The diagnostic character is its green spore print — completely unique among large lawn mushrooms in North America. No common edible or deadly look-alike produces a green print. When young, the gills are white and the cap can be clean and pale, making it look completely benign. As the mushroom matures, the gills gradually turn green as spores develop, but this color change can be subtle in fresh specimens.

The edible parasol (*Macrolepiota procera*) has a distinctive snakeskin pattern of brown zigzag scales on its very tall stipe, a moveable double ring, and a white spore print. *C. molybdites* has a smoother stipe without the snakeskin pattern and always prints green.

**Key evidence:**
- Leading cause of North American mushroom poisonings by annual case count (not fatality)
- Habitat (lawns, parks, golf courses) creates false sense of safety
- Green spore print is absolutely diagnostic — no other common look-alike produces it
- Gills turn greenish with age but can be white when young, making young specimens especially dangerous
- More common in warm climates: southeastern US, southern California, tropical/subtropical regions worldwide
- The stipe of the edible *Macrolepiota procera* has a distinctive "snakeskin" zigzag brown pattern absent in *C. molybdites*

**Context for content creators:**
The "most common poisoning" statistic is attention-grabbing and accurate. Emphasize the lawn habitat — the public associates danger with dark forests, not their backyard. The green spore print is visually compelling content. Caveat: not deadly in healthy adults but hospitalizations are real; children and immunocompromised individuals face greater risk.
