Following the 1986 Chernobyl nuclear disaster, robotic cameras surveying the interior of the damaged reactor documented black, melanin-rich fungi colonizing the walls — appearing to move toward the radiation source rather than away from it. Species recovered included *Cladosporium sphaerospermum*, *Cryptococcus neoformans*, *Wangiella dermatitidis*, and *Aureobasidium pullulans*.

A landmark 2007 study by Dadachova et al. published in *PLOS ONE* demonstrated that these melanized fungi grew significantly faster in the presence of ionizing radiation (gamma rays) than in its absence. Ionizing radiation caused measurable changes in the electronic structure of melanin, analogous to how chlorophyll's electronic structure is altered by photons. The energy appeared to be channeled into metabolic processes — specifically, measurably increased NADH production.

The proposed mechanism, termed "radiosynthesis," would be mechanistically distinct from photosynthesis and chemosynthesis — the only previously known biological energy capture strategies. This does not mean the fungi are immune to radiation damage; rather, their melanin-based pigmentation appears to provide both protection against and potential metabolic exploitation of ionizing radiation.

These fungi are now being investigated for potential applications in radioactive waste biosorption and as inspiration for radiation-shielding materials. NASA has explored radiotrophic fungi for radiation protection in deep space habitats.

**Key evidence:**
- Dadachova et al. (2007, *PLOS ONE*): melanized fungi grew faster under gamma radiation; melanin electronic structure changed analogously to chlorophyll response to photons
- NADH increase under radiation measured in laboratory experiments with *Cladosporium sphaerospermum*
- Chernobyl reactor interior: robotic camera documentation of black fungal growth on walls near the radiation source
- Melanin's exceptional metal-binding capacity also makes these fungi candidates for biosorption of radioactive metals (Cs-137, Sr-90)
- NASA funding: Innovative Advanced Concepts (NIAC) program investigated radiotrophic fungi for space habitat shielding

**Context for content creators:**
One of the most genuinely astonishing findings in mycology. Caveat: "radiosynthesis" is a proposed mechanism, not yet fully validated to the level of photosynthesis — the 2007 Dadachova study is compelling but needs replication and mechanistic confirmation. The fungi are not immortal or unharmed by radiation — they appear to tolerate and possibly benefit from radiation levels that would kill most organisms, not to be impervious. Do not present as proven equivalent to photosynthesis without qualification.
