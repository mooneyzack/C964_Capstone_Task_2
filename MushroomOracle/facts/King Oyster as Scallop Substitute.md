*Pleurotus eryngii* (king oyster mushroom, also called trumpet royale or eryngii) has a dramatically different flavor and texture profile from common oyster mushrooms. The thick, dense white stipe — which comprises most of the mushroom's mass — remains remarkably firm after cooking, unlike most mushrooms that soften significantly with heat. When the stipe is sliced crosswise into rounds 2–3 cm thick, scored with a crosshatch pattern on the cut faces, and pressed into a very hot pan with butter, the flat surfaces caramelize deeply while the interior heats through slowly, creating a texture that is firm, succulent, and slightly springy — properties that closely parallel sea scallops.

The visual result is striking: golden-brown caramelized discs that can be plated identically to seared scallops. The mild, slightly sweet, savory flavor of king oyster is compatible with classic scallop preparations (lemon-butter sauce, pea puree, cauliflower cream) without requiring strong seasoning to mask a mushroom-specific flavor.

The key technique is high heat and patience: start in a cold dry pan, bring to high heat, add butter or neutral oil, then place scored rounds scored-side down without moving for 2–3 minutes until a deep crust forms. Flip once; repeat on the other side. Season only after caramelization is achieved.

**Key evidence:**
- The stipe's unusually dense, firm texture (due to a tighter hyphal packing than most mushrooms) survives high-heat cooking without becoming mushy
- Crosshatch scoring increases surface area for browning and helps the flat face caramelize evenly
- Plated next to scallops, the visual similarity is striking — widely used in plant-based restaurant menus as an upscale seafood alternative
- Flavor profile: mild, slightly sweet, savory — does not have a strong "mushroom" flavor that would clash with classic scallop preparations
- Works with dairy and non-dairy sauces equally well, making it vegan-compatible
