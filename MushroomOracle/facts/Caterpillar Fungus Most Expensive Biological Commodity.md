*Ophiocordyceps sinensis* (Chinese: dong chong xia cao, "winter worm, summer grass") is an entomopathogenic fungus that parasitizes the ghost moth larvae (*Thitarodes* spp.) living in alpine soils above 3,800 meters elevation on the Tibetan Plateau and adjacent regions of Qinghai, Yunnan, and Nepal. The fungus kills and mummifies the larva underground over winter, then produces a finger-like fruiting body from the larva's head in spring/summer — the "summer grass" of its Chinese name.

Wild harvest requires hand-searching alpine meadows at extreme altitude during a narrow window when the fruiting body emerges above ground but before it deteriorates. A kilogram of finished product requires thousands of individual specimens, each found by eye in remote terrain. Peak retail prices in Chinese and Hong Kong luxury medicine markets have reached $50,000–$63,000 USD per kilogram for high-grade product, surpassing gold by weight and making it one of the most expensive biological commodities on Earth. The harvest economy supports tens of thousands of Tibetan and Himalayan families — it constitutes a majority of cash income in some communities.

Wild *O. sinensis* is critically endangered from overharvesting and climate change (alpine snow patterns are shifting, affecting both the fungus and its moth host). Almost all commercial supplements labeled "cordyceps" now use cultivated *Cordyceps militaris*, which produces similar compounds (cordycepin, adenosine, beta-glucans) but is a distinct species grown on grain in controlled conditions.

**Key evidence:**
- Price history: peaked at ~$50,000–$63,000 USD/kg for top-grade wild product (mid-2010s); typical wholesale prices lower but still exceeding $20,000/kg for premium grades
- Harvest: hand-collected at >3,800m elevation; each specimen weighs ~0.4–1g; thousands per kg
- Documentation in Chinese medicine since *Ben Cao Shi Yi* (657 CE, Tang Dynasty)
- 1993 Chinese women's athletics world records at Stuttgart: coach Ma Junren attributed performance partly to cordyceps supplementation, triggering global interest
- Critically endangered in wild; CITES consideration has been discussed
- Nearly all commercial products use *Cordyceps militaris* (cultivated), not wild *O. sinensis*

**Context for content creators:**
The price point and the "winter worm, summer grass" transformation narrative are extremely engaging. Caveat: the 1993 sports performance claims were never substantiated by controlled research — cordyceps human clinical evidence for athletic performance remains weak. Wild vs. cultivated distinction is critical — most products are not the same species as the traditional medicine. Price data should be presented as peak market prices, not current universal prices.
