*Amanita phalloides* (death cap) is the single most lethal mushroom species on Earth, accounting for roughly 90% of mushroom-related fatalities globally despite being only one of thousands of toxic species. Its dominance in fatality statistics stems from the combination of a potent amatoxin payload, an unremarkable appearance (no warning color, no bitter taste, no immediate symptoms), and a dangerous resemblance to several edible species.

A single cap weighing approximately 50 g fresh weight contains enough alpha-amanitin to kill an adult. Amatoxins inhibit RNA polymerase II, blocking protein synthesis in liver and kidney cells — a mechanism with no antidote. The characteristic 6–24 hour latent phase (no symptoms) means victims often eat multiple meals before realizing anything is wrong. By the time fulminant hepatic failure develops (4–8 days post-ingestion), irreversible damage has already occurred.

The death cap was originally European but has been introduced to North America, Australia, New Zealand, South America, and South Africa via imported tree saplings, and its range is expanding. It is native to oak and beech woodland but has established in coastal California under live oak, in the Pacific Northwest, and on the US East Coast.

**Key evidence:**
- Alpha-amanitin dose of ~0.1 mg/kg body weight is lethal; a single cap delivers several times this dose to an adult
- No taste or smell to warn the consumer — folk detection methods (silver spoon, garlic, turning onions black) are myths
- Cooking, drying, boiling, or freezing does NOT destroy amatoxins
- Mortality rate with modern critical care is ~5–10%; without intervention historically 10–30%
- Liver transplant may be required in severe cases

**Context for content creators:**
This statistic is frequently cited and is well-supported by poison control and toxicology literature. Caveat: the 90% figure applies to fatalities, not to total mushroom poisoning incidents (where GI-only toxic species far outnumber fatalities). Never present this as a general "most common" poisoning — it is the most lethal, not the most frequent. Use as an anchor for safety messaging without overstating general mushroom danger.
