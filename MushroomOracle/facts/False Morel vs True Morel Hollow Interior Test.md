True morels (*Morchella* spp.) and false morels (*Gyromitra esculenta* and related species) fruit at the same time in spring and can occupy the same habitats, creating a dangerous confusion risk. Visual inspection from the outside is insufficient because both can have brown, irregular-looking caps. The definitive test is a longitudinal slice from top to bottom through the center: a true morel is completely hollow — cap and stipe form a single continuous chamber empty like a hot-air balloon. A false morel shows chambered internal partitions, cotton-like stuffing, or partial walls.

*Gyromitra esculenta* contains gyromitrin, which metabolizes to monomethylhydrazine (MMH) — a volatile hepatotoxic and hemolytic compound structurally related to rocket fuel. Symptoms develop 6–12 hours after ingestion: nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, followed by jaundice, hemolysis, and in severe cases liver failure. Partial detoxification by parboiling is sometimes practiced in parts of Europe, but the mushroom is considered unsafe to eat and is not recommended for foraging.

Beyond the hollow test, true morels have a regular honeycomb pattern of pits and vertical ridges, while false morels have an irregular, brain-like, lobed, or saddle-shaped cap that does not form regular geometric pits. True morel caps are attached to the stipe at the base margin; false morel caps hang or drape loosely.

**Key evidence:**
- True morel: fully hollow interior, regular honeycomb surface, cap attached to stipe margin
- False morel: chambered/cotton-filled interior, irregular brain-like surface, cap hangs loosely
- Gyromitrin toxicity: historcially up to 30% fatality rate in untreated severe European poisonings
- Gyromitrin is partially volatile — parboiling reduces but does not eliminate toxicity; raw consumption is seriously dangerous
- The mushroom is legally sold dried in some European countries but is considered toxic by most food safety authorities

**Context for content creators:**
The hollow test is a visually compelling, easily communicated safety technique. Caveat: the hollow test is a necessary but not sufficient test — beginners should not forage morels without expert guidance. Also note that while "parboiling" is mentioned in historical European practice, modern safety guidance discourages eating false morels entirely.
