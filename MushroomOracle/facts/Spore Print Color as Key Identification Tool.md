A spore print is made by placing a mature mushroom cap gill-side down on paper (half white, half black) for 4–12 hours. The accumulated spore deposit reveals the mass spore color — a character that is consistent across a species regardless of individual variation in cap color, habitat, or specimen age. It requires no equipment beyond paper and time, yet it separates species that visual inspection cannot.

Several life-or-death identifications depend on spore color. The green-spored parasol (*Chlorophyllum molybdites*, the most common cause of North American mushroom poisoning by case count) produces a diagnostic green print — no other common look-alike does. The death cap and destroying angels (*Amanita* spp.) produce white prints; cultivated button mushrooms (*Agaricus bisporus*) produce chocolate-brown prints. A brown print in what looks like a death cap egg rules in *Agaricus*. The deadly galerina (*Galerina marginata*) produces rusty-brown prints that separate it from the white-printing honey mushrooms it resembles.

The technique requires a mature cap (buttons will not print) and at least 4 hours of undisturbed contact. Results should be read against both white and dark backgrounds, as some colors (white, cream) are only visible against dark paper.

**Key evidence:**
- White print + white gills + growing from a volva = treat as deadly *Amanita* until proven otherwise
- Green print = *Chlorophyllum molybdites* — no other common North American species prints green
- Rusty-brown print on a small wood-growing mushroom = *Galerina* danger; white print = *Armillaria* (honey mushroom)
- Chocolate-brown print on a white-gilled button = *Agaricus* not *Amanita*
- Pink/salmon print = *Entoloma* or *Volvariella*, not white-spored deadlies

**Context for content creators:**
Spore printing is genuinely accessible as a teaching tool and a visually engaging process for social media. Caveat clearly: spore color alone is not a complete identification — it must be combined with cap shape, gill attachment, veil presence, habitat, and smell. Never advise someone to eat a mushroom based on spore color alone.
