*Antrodia cinnamomea* (also called *Antrodia camphorata*; Chinese: niu zhang zhi) is a polypore bracket fungus known to grow almost exclusively on the inner heartwood of *Cinnamomum kanehirae* (Taiwan red cypress or bull camphor tree), a tree that is itself endemic to Taiwan and now relatively rare due to logging. The strict host specificity — combined with the protected status of the tree and the difficulty of wild harvest — makes wild *A. cinnamomea* extraordinarily scarce and expensive, with prices reaching $20,000–$30,000 USD per kilogram for authenticated wild product.

*A. cinnamomea* is classified in Taiwanese traditional medicine as a potent liver-protective and immune-modulating agent. Modern research has identified a rich array of bioactive compounds: antcins (steroids), zhankuic acids, antrodins, and other unusual triterpenoids not found in other medicinal fungi. These compounds show significant hepatoprotective, anti-inflammatory, and anti-tumor activity in in vitro and animal studies. Several Taiwan government-funded research programs have investigated the species.

Because wild harvest is near-impossible at scale, cultivated *A. cinnamomea* (grown on artificial media) is now available, but traditional Taiwanese practitioners distinguish wild from cultivated and price them accordingly. Wild product requires verification due to extensive adulteration in the market.

**Key evidence:**
- Host specificity: wild growth essentially restricted to *Cinnamomum kanehirae* heartwood — no other reliably productive substrate known for wild specimens
- Price range: $20,000–$30,000 USD/kg for authenticated wild product (Taiwanese market reports)
- Bioactive compounds: antcins, zhankuic acids, antrodins, beta-glucans — many compounds unique to this species
- Hepatoprotective activity: significant in vitro and animal model evidence; human clinical evidence limited
- Market adulteration: high price drives substitution with other *Antrodia* species or *Ganoderma* — authentication difficult without chemical analysis
- *C. kanehirae* is now listed as a protected species in Taiwan, further limiting wild harvest

**Context for content creators:**
This is a compelling story of extreme rarity and price, with a legitimate scientific basis in its unique chemistry. Caveat: the "most expensive" ranking is Taiwan-specific and should not be generalized globally without noting that *Ophiocordyceps sinensis* is the world record holder. Human clinical evidence for the impressive in vitro activity is still limited — avoid overclaiming therapeutic effects.
