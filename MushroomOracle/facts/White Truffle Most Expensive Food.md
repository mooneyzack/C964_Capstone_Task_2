The white Alba truffle (*Tuber magnatum*), found in the Langhe and Monferrato regions of Piedmont, Italy (and less abundantly in Istria and parts of central Italy), is the world's most expensive food by weight. Auction prices at the annual Alba Truffle Fair have regularly exceeded €3,000/kg for good specimens, with extraordinary individual truffles — particularly large, unblemished ones sold to Asian markets or celebrity bidders — reaching €8,000–€10,000/kg or more.

Unlike the black Périgord truffle (*Tuber melanosporum*), which can be cultivated in inoculated oak orchards with some reliability, white truffles have resisted all cultivation attempts. They form an obligate ectomycorrhizal association with specific trees (oak, poplar, willow, linden) in specific soil conditions (calcareous, clay-rich, well-drained) that cannot yet be reproduced artificially at commercial scale. Supply is therefore entirely dependent on wild harvest, which is limited, seasonal (October–December), and subject to weather variability.

The flavor comes from an extraordinarily complex cocktail of volatile sulfur compounds — primarily dimethyl sulfide, 2,4-dithiapentane, and bis(methylthio)methane — that evaporate rapidly at room temperature. White truffles must be consumed within days of harvest and are always served raw (shaved directly onto warm food), never cooked, because heat drives off the aromatics in seconds.

**Key evidence:**
- Cannot be cultivated — entirely wild-harvest; supply constrained by ecology and weather
- Peak price at Alba Truffle Fair (documented): individual specimens sold at charity auction for $100,000+ USD (publicity/charity events); commercial market prices €3,000–€10,000/kg
- Piedmont's calcareous soils and specific tree associations are the primary production zone
- Flavor volatility: primary aromatics evaporate within days of harvest; consumption must be immediate
- Black truffle (*T. melanosporum*) can be cultivated and is significantly cheaper; white truffle cannot yet be reliably farmed
- Trained dogs (and historically pigs) required to locate them underground — harvest is artisanal by necessity

**Context for content creators:**
The price is inherently engaging content but context is important: most "truffle" products (truffle oil, truffle chips) use synthetic 2,4-dithiapentane (one compound from the white truffle's complex profile), not actual truffle. This synthetic version is detectable as "fake" by experts because it lacks the full aromatic complexity. Caveat: auction prices are peak/publicity prices; wholesale market prices fluctuate widely with harvest season.
