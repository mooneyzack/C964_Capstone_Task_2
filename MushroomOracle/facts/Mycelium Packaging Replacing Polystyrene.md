Mycelium packaging is a composite material made by inoculating agricultural waste (corn stalks, hemp hurds, wood chips) with fungal spawn, loading the mixture into product-shaped molds, and allowing mycelium to colonize for 5–7 days at ambient temperature. The growing hyphae bind the substrate into a solid, form-stable composite. The material is then heat-killed at ~60°C to halt growth and stabilize the product. The finished material is fire-resistant, moderately water-resistant, and structurally comparable to expanded polystyrene (EPS/Styrofoam) for protective packaging applications.

The critical environmental advantage is end-of-life: mycelium packaging home-composts in 30–45 days, whereas EPS is essentially permanent in landfill and takes decades to centuries to fragment (and never fully mineralizes). EPS production generates approximately 70 kg of CO₂ equivalent per kilogram of material; mycelium packaging is net carbon-sequestering during growth (the mycelium fixes carbon from agricultural waste into fungal biomass).

IKEA announced in 2016 plans to use mycelium packaging for large fragile items (mirrors, flat-pack furniture). Dell tested it for server and hardware shipment. Multiple wine and luxury goods brands adopted it for sustainable packaging positioning. Ecovative Design, founded in 2007 by Eben Bayer and Gavin McIntyre while students at Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute, licensed its technology to manufacturers globally.

**Key evidence:**
- Compostability: 30–45 days in home compost vs. centuries for EPS
- Manufacturing: 5–7 days growth at ambient temperature — no high-energy heating or chemical inputs
- Performance: comparable compressive strength to EPS for protective packaging; slightly inferior thermal insulation
- Limitations: moisture resistance lower than EPS; surface coatings can improve this at some environmental cost
- Commercial adoption: IKEA (2016 announcement), Dell (pilot testing), luxury goods brands

**Context for content creators:**
This is highly shareable sustainability content with a compelling before/after contrast (ancient Styrofoam vs. 45-day compost). Caveat: cost remains higher than EPS at current scales; moisture resistance requires coatings; thermal insulation for cold-chain applications is inferior. The technology is real and commercially deployed — avoid overpromising imminent replacement of all EPS.
