Quorn is a meat substitute made from mycoprotein — the dried, textured biomass of *Fusarium venenatum*, an ascomycete fungus originally isolated from Buckinghamshire soil in 1967 during a Rank Hovis McDougall (RHM) protein alternatives screening program. Production uses a continuous airlift fermentation process: glucose and mineral salts are fed continuously into the fermenter while culture is continuously harvested, with the fermenter running for weeks without stopping. This is fundamentally different from batch fermentation — the fungus grows, divides, and is harvested in a continuous steady state.

Harvested mycelium is heat-treated to reduce RNA content (a regulatory requirement; high nucleotide consumption can elevate uric acid and contribute to gout) and then textured, flavored, and formed into meat-like products. As of the mid-2020s, Quorn produces over 15,000 tonnes of mycoprotein per year from its facility in Billingham, UK, and sells products in 17+ countries.

The nutritional profile is compelling: approximately 45% protein by dry weight, all essential amino acids, a protein digestibility-corrected amino acid score (PDCAAS) above 0.9 (comparable to meat and milk), high dietary fiber from chitin and beta-glucans, and substantially lower saturated fat than meat. The carbon footprint per kilogram of protein is lower than chicken (already the most carbon-efficient conventional animal protein) and dramatically lower than beef.

**Key evidence:**
- Production scale: >15,000 tonnes/year of mycoprotein (mid-2020s) from Billingham, UK facility
- *Fusarium venenatum* — isolated 1967; commercial production since 1985 following extensive safety testing required by UK regulatory authorities
- Nutritional PDCAAS: >0.9 (comparable to animal protein) — verified by independent assay
- Lower carbon and water footprint than chicken per gram of protein
- RNA heat-reduction treatment is a regulatory requirement, not optional — distinguishes mycoprotein from raw mycelium

**Context for content creators:**
Quorn is the most commercially proven mycoprotein product — 40+ years of safe consumption at scale. Caveat: not vegan (most products contain egg white as binder — newer vegan formulations use potato protein); the RNA reduction treatment is invisible to consumers but important context. Not all "mycoprotein" products are equivalent to Quorn's safety record.
