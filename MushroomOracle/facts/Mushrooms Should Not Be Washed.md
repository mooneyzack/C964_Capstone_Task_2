Mushrooms are not plants with a waterproof cuticle — they are mostly chitin and water-filled cells with a highly porous surface architecture. When exposed to running water, they absorb moisture quickly. This extra water must then be driven off during cooking, extending the steaming phase and further delaying the Maillard browning that creates deep, savory flavor. For delicate species like chanterelles (*Cantharellus cibarius*), water also leaches out fat-soluble flavor compounds, reducing aroma intensity.

The professional kitchen standard is dry cleaning: use a soft brush (a dedicated mushroom brush, a pastry brush, or a soft toothbrush) to remove soil, debris, and forest material. For firm species like portobello or crimini, a slightly dampened paper towel or cloth can wipe surfaces clean without saturating the flesh. Only for very dirty, heavily soil-encrusted wild mushrooms is a quick cold rinse followed by immediate thorough drying acceptable — and even then, the mushrooms should be spread on towels and blotted completely before cooking.

The exception to the no-wash rule: mushrooms going directly into soups, stocks, or braising liquids where moisture is a desired component of the cooking medium. In these applications, the absorbed water simply becomes part of the liquid.

**Key evidence:**
- Mushrooms are 85–95% water already; additional absorbed water extends steaming time in the pan
- Maillard browning (the key flavor reaction) requires surface temperatures above ~140°C, which cannot occur while steam is being produced
- Chanterelles are especially sensitive: rinse → flavor loss in fat-soluble aroma compounds
- Professional kitchen standard: dry brush for most species; damp cloth for firm, very dirty specimens
- Exception: recipes where mushrooms go directly into liquid (soups, stocks) — washing here is inconsequential

**Context for content creators:**
This directly contradicts standard vegetable washing advice and is therefore counterintuitive and shareable. Caveat: food safety note — wild mushrooms should have obvious soil and debris removed; the no-wash guidance is about technique, not sanitation. If there is genuine concern about contamination (e.g., pesticides on cultivated mushrooms), a very brief rinse followed by thorough drying is acceptable.
