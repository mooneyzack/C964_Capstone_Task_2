*Tremella fuciformis* (snow fungus, silver ear, white jelly mushroom) has been used in Chinese beauty traditions for centuries — historically associated with imperial court beauty regimens, particularly in accounts connecting it to the legendary beauty of Yang Guifei (719–756 CE), a consort of Emperor Xuanzong of the Tang Dynasty. Modern cosmetic science has begun to characterize the mechanism behind these traditional claims.

Tremella polysaccharides are highly branched, water-soluble glucuronoxylomannan polymers with an extraordinarily high capacity for water binding. Their molecular weight (approximately 1.6–2 × 10⁶ Da for high-molecular-weight fractions) and branched structure create a three-dimensional matrix that holds water molecules through multiple hydrogen bond sites. Several in vitro studies and manufacturer-commissioned testing have compared Tremella polysaccharide to hyaluronic acid — the gold standard skin-hydration ingredient — and found equivalent or superior moisture retention at comparable concentrations.

A critical advantage is particle size: Tremella polysaccharide naturally forms nanoparticles (50–300 nm) that can penetrate the outer stratum corneum layers more effectively than most high-molecular-weight hyaluronic acid preparations. The polysaccharide is also stable across a wider pH range, making it compatible with a broader range of formulation chemistries.

**Key evidence:**
- Tremella polysaccharide water-retention: in vitro studies show moisture binding comparable to or exceeding sodium hyaluronate at equivalent concentrations
- Particle size: ~50–300 nm nanoparticles formed naturally; penetration advantage over large HA molecules
- Traditional use: documented in Tang Dynasty records; associated with imperial beauty regimens in historical Chinese literature
- Antioxidant activity: Tremella polysaccharides also show free radical scavenging activity in vitro, adding a secondary skin benefit
- Regulatory: widely used in Chinese, Korean, and Japanese cosmetic formulations; no safety concerns at cosmetic-use concentrations

**Context for content creators:**
"Better than hyaluronic acid" is a strong claim that should be qualified: the comparison depends heavily on concentration, molecular weight of the HA being compared, and whether the test is in vitro or in vivo. In vitro water retention studies are not the same as clinical skincare outcomes. Present as "comparable to or superior to hyaluronic acid in water-retention benchmarks" with appropriate hedging. The traditional history angle is genuine and adds cultural depth.
