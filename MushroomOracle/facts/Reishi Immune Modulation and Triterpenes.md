Ganoderma lucidum (reishi, lingzhi) produces two major classes of bioactive compounds with distinct mechanisms and extraction requirements. The first class — ganoderic acids — is a family of lanostane-type triterpenoids of which over 300 individual variants have been identified (designated by letters A, B, C, DM, T, X, etc.). These are fat-soluble compounds requiring alcohol extraction and are responsible for reishi's characteristic bitter taste. The second class — polysaccharides including Ganoderans A, B, and C, and beta-1,3/1,6-glucans — are water-soluble, requiring hot water extraction, and drive the primary immune effects via Dectin-1 receptor activation.

Ganoderic acids demonstrate a broad spectrum of in vitro and preclinical activities: direct cytotoxicity against hepatocellular, lung, colon, and breast cancer cell lines via caspase-mediated apoptosis; anti-angiogenic effects through VEGF suppression; HMG-CoA reductase inhibition (the same enzyme targeted by statin drugs); ACE inhibition; antihistamine effects; and anti-inflammatory activity via NF-κB and MAPK signaling inhibition. Some ganoderic acids also inhibit 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase, affecting cortisol metabolism — one proposed mechanism for adaptogenic effects.

A 2016 Cochrane review of reishi supplementation in cancer patients (N=373) documented modest but measurable immunomodulatory effects: CD3 T-cells +3.91%, CD4 T-cells +3.05%, CD8 T-cells +2.02%. The review also noted that evidence was insufficient to recommend reishi as a primary cancer treatment, but potentially useful as an adjunct to support immune function alongside conventional therapy. Triterpene content in commercial reishi products ranges from undetectable to 7.8% — standardization is critical for medicinal use.

**Key evidence:**
- 300+ ganoderic acid triterpenes identified; responsible for bitter taste; require alcohol extraction
- Ganoderic acids A, B, C, DM, T: cytotoxic activity against multiple cancer cell lines in vitro
- HMG-CoA reductase inhibition: same target as statin drugs
- Cochrane review (2016, N=373): CD3 T-cells +3.91%, CD4 +3.05%, CD8 +2.02% in cancer patients
- Commercial products: triterpene content ranges from undetectable to 7.8% — standardization matters
- Dual extraction required to capture both polysaccharides and triterpenes

**Context for content creators:**
Reishi has the longest traditional use history of any medicinal mushroom and the widest array of studied compounds, but human clinical trial evidence is more limited than the preclinical literature. Most ganoderic acid research is in vitro or animal-based — avoid implying proven cancer treatment in humans. The Cochrane review is a legitimate human evidence anchor for immune effects in cancer patients. Emphasize the importance of dual extraction (hot water + alcohol) for any product claiming full-spectrum activity — and note that triterpene content varies enormously between products, making standardized extracts essential for medicinal applications.
