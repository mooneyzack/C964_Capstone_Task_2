Lovastatin (also known as mevinolin) is an HMG-CoA reductase inhibitor — the enzyme responsible for the rate-limiting step in cholesterol biosynthesis. Inhibiting this enzyme reduces endogenous cholesterol production, which is the mechanism of action for the entire statin drug class. Lovastatin was the first FDA-approved statin drug (Mevacor, Merck, 1987), and Pleurotus ostreatus (oyster mushroom) produces this exact molecule naturally. This makes oyster mushroom the only edible food source of naturally occurring lovastatin.

Lovastatin content in Pleurotus species varies substantially by strain and growing conditions, ranging from 50 to 5,991 mcg/g dry weight across studies, with up to 2.8% lovastatin on a dry-weight basis documented in research settings. P. sapidus, P. sajor-caju, and P. ostreatus all produce mevinolin; content can be enhanced through substrate optimization. Human clinical evidence supports a cholesterol-lowering effect: a study of 20 subjects consuming daily oyster mushroom soup (30g dried oyster mushroom per serving) showed significant reductions in triacylglycerol, oxidized-LDL, and total cholesterol. A study in HIV/antiretroviral patients also documented antihyperlipidemic effects (PMC3163637).

Oyster mushrooms operate through a dual cholesterol-lowering mechanism: (1) lovastatin directly inhibits HMG-CoA reductase, reducing endogenous cholesterol synthesis; and (2) beta-glucans (24.2% DW in oyster mushrooms) form a viscous intestinal gel that traps dietary cholesterol and bile acids, reducing absorption and increasing fecal cholesterol excretion. This dual mechanism makes oyster mushroom a genuinely interesting subject for cardiovascular nutrition research.

**Key evidence:**
- Oyster mushroom: only edible food source of naturally occurring lovastatin (mevinolin)
- Lovastatin content: 50–5,991 mcg/g DW; up to 2.8% DW in research conditions
- P. sapidus, P. sajor-caju, and P. ostreatus all produce mevinolin
- Human study (N=20): daily oyster mushroom soup (30g dried) → reduced triacylglycerol, oxidized-LDL, total cholesterol
- Dual mechanism: (1) HMG-CoA reductase inhibition (lovastatin); (2) beta-glucan intestinal cholesterol trapping
- Oyster mushroom also ranks high for beta-glucan content: 24.2 ± 1.6% DW

**Context for content creators:**
The "same molecule as the statin drug" angle is compelling and scientifically accurate. Key nuances: the amount of lovastatin in a typical serving of cooked oyster mushrooms is far below the therapeutic dose used in pharmaceutical lovastatin (10–80 mg/day prescribed dose vs. typical food amounts). Content should frame this as "interesting research" or "a cardiovascular-supporting food" rather than "natural statin alternative." For anyone already taking statin drugs, adding large amounts of lovastatin-containing foods could theoretically compound effects — worth noting. The dual mechanism (lovastatin + beta-glucans) is a genuinely interesting story about how whole foods can work through multiple pathways simultaneously.
