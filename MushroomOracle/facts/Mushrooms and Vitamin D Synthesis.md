Mushrooms are unique among foods in their ability to synthesize vitamin D through UV exposure — a property shared with human skin. Ergosterol, concentrated in the fungal cell membrane, serves as the provitamin precursor. UV-B radiation (280–315 nm) cleaves the B-ring of the ergosterol steroid backbone through an electrocyclic ring-opening reaction, producing pre-vitamin D2, which then undergoes a thermal [1,7]-sigmatropic hydrogen shift to form vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol). Prolonged UV exposure produces photoisomers tachysterol and lumisterol — a natural ceiling that prevents over-production.

The practical yield varies dramatically by UV exposure. Dark-grown commercial mushrooms deliver fewer than 40 IU per 100g. Sun-exposed wild mushrooms (chanterelles, morels) can reach up to 1,200 IU per 100g. Button mushrooms exposed gill-side-up for 30–60 minutes in midday sunlight generate approximately 400 IU per 100g — sufficient to meet the adult daily requirement. Dried sun-exposed mushrooms retain approximately 600 IU per 100g, with D2 remaining stable in dried form.

Mushroom-derived vitamin D is D2, not D3 (cholecalciferol, which human skin produces). Studies indicate D3 is approximately 87% more effective than D2 at raising and maintaining serum 25(OH)D levels. However, D2 from mushrooms is bioavailable and clinically meaningful — confirmed to raise serum vitamin D levels in deficient populations.

**Key evidence:**
- UV-B (280–315 nm) converts ergosterol → pre-vitamin D2 → vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol)
- Commercial dark-grown mushrooms: <40 IU/100g; sun-exposed (30–60 min midday): ~400 IU/100g
- Wild sun-exposed chanterelles and morels: up to 1,200 IU/100g (Harvard Nutrition Source)
- Vitamin D2 from mushrooms confirmed to raise serum 25(OH)D in deficient populations
- D3 is ~87% more effective than D2 at maintaining serum vitamin D levels

**Context for content creators:**
The "gill-side-up in sunlight" tip is accurate, well-supported, and highly practical — a genuine content opportunity. Key nuance: mushroom D is D2, not D3; both are bioavailable but D3 is more potent. Avoid claiming mushrooms are equivalent to supplements without noting the D2/D3 distinction. The claim is strongest for UV-exposed mushrooms specifically — commercial indoor-grown mushrooms are not a meaningful vitamin D source without UV treatment.
