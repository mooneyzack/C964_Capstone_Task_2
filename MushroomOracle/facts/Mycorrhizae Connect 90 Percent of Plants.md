Mycorrhizal symbiosis — from the Greek *mykes* (fungus) and *rhiza* (root) — is the dominant nutritional strategy for land plants. Fossil evidence from the Rhynie Chert (Scotland, ~407 million years ago) shows arbuscular mycorrhizal structures in some of the earliest land plants; molecular clock estimates push the origin of mycorrhizal symbiosis to approximately 450–460 million years ago, coinciding with the colonization of land by plants. The symbiosis may have been essential for enabling terrestrial plant life in the nutrient-poor, desiccating conditions of early land environments.

Mycorrhizal fungi extend beyond the root surface into surrounding soil, exploring volumes and pore sizes inaccessible to root hairs alone. In exchange for photosynthate (sugars) from the plant — typically 10–35% of the plant's fixed carbon — the fungus delivers phosphorus, nitrogen, zinc, copper, and water to the plant root. Plants with arbuscular mycorrhizal (AM) fungi can access phosphorus at concentrations 4–10 times lower than non-mycorrhizal plants can detect. This is not a marginal benefit: in most terrestrial soils, phosphorus is the primary limiting nutrient for plant growth.

The ~90% figure encompasses multiple mycorrhizal types: arbuscular mycorrhizae (AM, Glomeromycota) form with approximately 72% of plant species including most crops and grasses; ectomycorrhizae (ECM, primarily Basidiomycota and some Ascomycota) form with about 2% of plant species but dominate temperate and boreal forests (oaks, beeches, pines, spruce). The remaining plant families with mycorrhizae include ericoid, orchid, and arbutoid types.

**Key evidence:**
- ~90% of all terrestrial plant species form at least one type of mycorrhizal association
- Origin: ~450–460 million years ago based on fossil and molecular clock evidence
- AM fungi can deliver P to plants from concentrations below plant root detection threshold
- 10–35% of plant fixed carbon is allocated to mycorrhizal fungi — a major carbon cost that the plant "pays" for the symbiosis
- Exceptions (non-mycorrhizal families): Brassicaceae (mustard family), Chenopodiaceae (now Amaranthaceae), Proteaceae (some), Caryophyllaceae

**Context for content creators:**
The "90% of plants" statistic is accurate and well-established. The popular framing of the "Wood Wide Web" draws on this symbiosis but goes further to claim inter-plant communication and resource sharing — which is more contested (see [[Common Mycorrhizal Networks]]). Distinguish the established symbiosis (well-proven) from the more speculative claims about intentional resource transfer. The symbiosis itself is one of the most important biological relationships on Earth.
