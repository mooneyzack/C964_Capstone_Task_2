During the Carboniferous period (358–299 million years ago), vast forests of tree-sized lycopsids, horsetails, and early seed plants dominated tropical lowland swamps. When these trees died, their lignin-rich wood did not decompose efficiently because no organism yet possessed the enzymatic machinery to break down lignin — the complex aromatic polymer reinforcing plant cell walls. Dead wood accumulated in oxygen-poor swampy conditions, was buried, compressed, and over geological time became the coal deposits that powered the Industrial Revolution.

A landmark 2012 genomic study (Floudas et al., *Science*) reconstructed the evolutionary history of wood-decay fungi from 31 fungal genomes and found that the evolution of the class II peroxidase enzymes capable of lignin oxidation (lignin peroxidase, manganese peroxidase) occurred predominantly in the Agaricomycetes (the main group of Basidiomycota) approximately 290–300 million years ago — coinciding with a sharp decline in coal-bed formation in the geological record. Before this evolutionary event, wood accumulated; after it, decomposition kept pace with production.

The only organisms capable of efficiently mineralizing lignin today remain white-rot Basidiomycota such as *Trametes versicolor*, *Phanerochaete chrysosporium*, and *Pleurotus ostreatus*. Brown-rot fungi and soft-rot fungi can degrade cellulose and hemicellulose but leave modified lignin behind as the dark, crumbly residue characteristic of brown-rot decay.

**Key evidence:**
- Floudas et al. (2012, *Science*): genomic reconstruction places lignin peroxidase evolution at ~290–300 million years ago
- Coal formation: predominantly Carboniferous (358–299 Ma); sharp decline aligns with white-rot peroxidase evolution
- Only white-rot Basidiomycota efficiently mineralize lignin; all other decomposers leave it partially intact
- Without white-rot fungi, lignin would accumulate indefinitely — this is not speculative; modern white-rot-free environments (certain arctic peats, waterlogged anoxic conditions) do show lignin accumulation
- Alternative hypothesis: climate change (drying and cooling at end of Carboniferous) reduced swamp forests, diminishing coal formation regardless of fungi — the fungal/coal hypothesis is supported but not universally accepted as the sole explanation

**Context for content creators:**
This is one of the most profound "small organism, vast consequence" stories in biology. Caveat: the coal hypothesis is the dominant interpretation of the genomic data but is not the only explanation for the Carboniferous-Permian transition in carbon burial. The confidence is "medium" because the causal attribution (fungi caused the end of coal formation) remains debated, even though the timeline correlation and mechanistic capability are strong.
