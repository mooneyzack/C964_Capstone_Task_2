Umami — the fifth basic taste — is produced primarily by free glutamate and free ribonucleotides, particularly 5'-IMP (inosine monophosphate) from meat and 5'-GMP (guanosine monophosphate) from shiitake and other fungi. The critical discovery in umami science is that glutamate and 5'-nucleotides do not simply add together — they synergize exponentially. A mixture of glutamate and 5'-GMP at concentrations that produce minimal umami individually creates an umami intensity 7–8 times greater than glutamate alone.

This is the scientific basis for Japanese dashi, the foundational broth of Japanese cuisine. Combining dried shiitake (highest 5'-GMP source: ~150 mg/100g dry weight) with kombu seaweed (extraordinarily rich in glutamate, ~1,600 mg/100g dry weight) exploits the full synergy. The resulting broth has depth of flavor that neither ingredient produces alone — this is why removing kombu and adding a synthetic glutamate (MSG) source cannot perfectly replicate the broth made from the natural combination.

Beyond dashi, dried porcini (*Boletus edulis*) is the richest food source of free glutamate, making it the premier umami-amplifier in Italian and French cooking. The soaking liquid from dried mushrooms concentrates all soluble flavor compounds and should always be incorporated into the dish.

**Key evidence:**
- Dried shiitake: ~150 mg/100g 5'-GMP (dry weight) — highest among common foods
- Synergy: glutamate + 5'-GMP produces 7–8× the umami intensity of glutamate alone at equivalent concentrations
- Kombu seaweed: ~1,600 mg/100g glutamate — the complementary partner in dashi
- Lenthionine (the cyclic polysulfide giving dried shiitake its distinctive sulfurous-forest aroma) forms from lentinic acid during cell damage and is separate from umami nucleotides
- Fresh shiitake has milder flavor than dried; drying concentrates 5'-GMP and converts flavor precursors

**Context for content creators:**
The synergy concept is inherently engaging — it explains why the whole is greater than the sum of its parts in a way that has direct kitchen application. Caveat: the 7–8× figure is derived from sensory science panel studies at specific concentration ranges and should not be overgeneralized. The mechanism is well-established; the exact multiplier varies with concentration and matrix.
