On May 13, 1957, *Life* magazine published "Seeking the Magic Mushroom" by R. Gordon Wasson — an account of his participation in a Mazatec velada ceremony guided by curandera María Sabina in Huautla de Jiménez, Oaxaca in 1955. The article reached an estimated 5 million readers, representing the first time a mainstream American publication described the ingestion of psilocybin mushrooms and their psychedelic effects to a broad public audience.

The article's influence was immediate and profound. Among its readers was Timothy Leary, then a Harvard clinical psychologist, who would go on to conduct (initially legitimate) psilocybin research at Harvard before becoming the defining public advocate of psychedelic culture in the 1960s. Leary later described reading the article as one of the catalysts for his interest in psychedelics. Albert Hofmann — the Swiss chemist who had already synthesized LSD in 1943 — also learned of Wasson's work and subsequently isolated psilocybin and psilocin from specimens sent by French mycologist Roger Heim, publishing the chemical structures in 1958.

The article also triggered a flood of Western travelers to Huautla de Jiménez seeking the mushrooms — a cultural collision that deeply harmed the Mazatec community (see [[Maria Sabina and the Mazatec Velada]]). The wave of interest directly fed into the Harvard Psilocybin Project (1960–1962), the subsequent psychedelic counterculture, and — through decades of backlash and criminalization — the eventual psychedelic renaissance in clinical research that began in the 1990s.

**Key evidence:**
- Published: *Life* magazine, May 13, 1957, with photograph by Allan Richardson
- Estimated readership: ~5 million (standard *Life* circulation at the time)
- Hofmann isolated psilocybin and psilocin from *Psilocybe mexicana* specimens in 1958, publishing structures
- Timothy Leary cited Wasson's article among his formative influences; first psilocybin experience: 1960 in Cuernavaca, Mexico
- Harvard Psilocybin Project (1960–1962): Leary, Richard Alpert (Ram Dass), and colleagues conducted research that ended in controversy and their dismissal from Harvard
- The article was the primary trigger for Western awareness of psilocybin mushrooms as distinct psychedelic agents

**Context for content creators:**
This is a pivotal historical moment connecting ancient indigenous tradition to modern psychedelic science.
