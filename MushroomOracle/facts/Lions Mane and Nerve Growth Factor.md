Hericium erinaceus (lion's mane) produces two distinct families of neuroactive compounds: erinacines (from the mycelium) and hericenones (from the fruiting body). Erinacines are cyathin diterpenoids — 15 variants identified (A–K, P–S) — and are the more pharmacologically significant class. Erinacine A, the most potent, induces approximately 250 pg/mL NGF synthesis at 1 mM in vitro and has reduced amyloid-β plaques while increasing insulin-degrading enzyme expression by 130–303% in Alzheimer's disease models.

Nerve Growth Factor (NGF) promotes the survival, maintenance, and regeneration of neurons, and supports the differentiation of cholinergic neurons — the neuronal subtype most affected in Alzheimer's pathology. Critically, erinacines cross the blood-brain barrier, allowing them to act centrally rather than being restricted to peripheral tissues. In a landmark 2009 RCT (Mori et al.), participants taking 3g/day of dried lion's mane fruiting body for 16 weeks showed significant improvement on Hasegawa's Dementia Scale in mild cognitive impairment (N=30); effects reversed during a 4-week washout period.

The fruiting body vs. mycelium distinction matters enormously for product evaluation: hericenones are found exclusively in the fruiting body, while erinacines are found exclusively in the mycelium. A product sourced only from fruiting body contains no erinacines.

**Key evidence:**
- Erinacine A induces ~250 pg/mL NGF synthesis in vitro at 1 mM concentration
- In vivo: 44% reduction in cerebral infarct volume (stroke model); reversal of MPTP-induced motor deficits (Parkinson's model)
- 2009 Mori et al. RCT: significant cognitive improvement in mild cognitive impairment over 16 weeks (N=30)
- All in vivo neuroprotection data is preclinical (cell culture and animal models) as of 2024

**Context for content creators:**
The NGF-stimulating activity of erinacines is well-supported in preclinical research, but human clinical trials specifically testing erinacines (rather than whole fruiting body extracts) remain limited. State claims carefully: "may support NGF production" or "preclinical research shows NGF stimulation" is more accurate than "increases NGF in humans." The 2009 RCT supports cognitive benefits but used fruiting body powder, not isolated erinacines. Avoid conflating preclinical animal data with proven human benefits.
