When mycorrhizal fungi colonize multiple plants simultaneously, their hyphae can physically interconnect root systems of neighboring plants, forming Common Mycorrhizal Networks (CMNs) — colloquially known as the "Wood Wide Web." This concept entered widespread public awareness following a landmark 1997 paper in *Nature* by Suzanne Simard and colleagues, who used radiocarbon tracers to demonstrate bidirectional carbon transfer between paper birch (*Betula papyrifera*) and Douglas fir (*Pseudotsuga menziesii*) via a shared ectomycorrhizal network.

Controlled studies have verified resource transfer of carbon (using ¹³C and ¹⁴C radiolabels), nitrogen (¹⁵N-labeled), and phosphorus (³²P radiolabeled) through mycorrhizal networks. Transfer is bidirectional and seasonal — direction follows concentration gradients and source-sink relationships between connected plants, varying by season, species, and soil nutrient availability. Defensive chemical signals (salicylic acid, volatile terpenoids, jasmonates) have also been detected moving through some ectomycorrhizal networks.

However, the popular "Wood Wide Web" narrative has been substantially qualified by the scientific community. A 2023 paper in *Nature Ecology & Evolution* (Karst et al.) documented positive citation bias in the CMN literature and overinterpretation of results, noting that most resource transfer studies were conducted in controlled laboratory settings with simplified seedling systems — and that field verification in complex natural forests remains extremely difficult. The mechanism of transfer (direct fungal transport through cytoplasm vs. indirect release into soil solution) remains unresolved. The "mother tree" hypothesis — that large old trees selectively route resources to their own offspring — has very limited experimental support.

**Key evidence:**
- Simard et al. (1997, Nature): bidirectional carbon transfer between birch and Douglas fir via ECM network (landmark study)
- Verified transfers: carbon (¹³C/¹⁴C), nitrogen (¹⁵N), phosphorus (³²P), strontium, rubidium, and defensive signals
- Transfer is bidirectional and follows concentration gradients, not directional "sharing"
- Karst et al. (2023, Nature Ecology & Evolution): positive citation bias; CMN literature overstated in popular accounts
- Most studies in controlled seedling systems; field ecological significance in complex forests remains uncertain
- CMNs are real; the extent of their ecological significance is what's contested

**Context for content creators:**
The "Wood Wide Web" concept is extraordinarily compelling for content — and the underlying science is real. The caveat is that popular accounts (including some best-selling books) have significantly overstated the certainty of the ecological significance. A responsible framing: "Mycorrhizal fungi physically connect trees and experimental studies show resource transfer — but how significant this is in natural forest systems is still being actively studied, and some earlier claims have been challenged." The 2023 Karst et al. critique in Nature Ecology & Evolution is worth acknowledging if you want to be credibility-forward. Avoid "trees talk to each other" framing unless heavily caveated — fungi are not intentional agents and resource flow follows thermodynamics, not intent.
