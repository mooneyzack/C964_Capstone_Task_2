## Overview

Fungi appear in human art and religion from the prehistoric through the contemporary. Their appearances range from contested prehistoric cave paintings to Aztec stone carvings, medieval European frescoes, and modern psychedelic renaissance culture. This note traces those appearances while distinguishing well-documented history from speculative interpretation.

Cross-links: [[Ethnomycology]], [[Traditional Medicine]], [[Mycophobia and Mycophilia]]

## Prehistoric Art

### Tassili n'Ajjer, Algeria (~7,000–9,000 BP)

The **Tassili n'Ajjer** rock art complex in the Algerian Sahara contains thousands of images spanning roughly 12,000 years. Among them is a composite figure sometimes called the **"Bee Man"** or "Mushroom Shaman" — a human figure with a bee-like or masked head, from whose hands appear to grow mushroom-shaped objects. The image has been interpreted by some researchers (notably Giorgio Samorini and others working in the ethnomycological tradition) as depicting a shaman holding or emerging from mushrooms, suggesting ritual mushroom use predating any written record.

**Caution:** The identification of the objects as mushrooms is interpretive. The images are abstract and scholars disagree about their meaning. The "psychedelic interpretation" of prehistoric rock art is frequently critiqued as projecting modern frameworks onto ancient imagery. The Tassili figures are genuine and significant; the mushroom interpretation is plausible but not established.

### Selva Pascuala, Spain (~6,000 BP)

A mural at **Selva Pascuala** (Villar del Humo, Cuenca Province, Spain) depicts what appear to be mushroom-shaped objects arranged in a row near a bull figure. Some researchers have proposed these represent *Psilocybe hispanica*, a psilocybin-containing species native to the Iberian Peninsula.

Again: the interpretation is contested. The images could represent mushrooms; they could represent other objects. The mural is real and significant regardless of interpretation.

## Pre-Columbian Mesoamerica

### Mushroom Stones of Guatemala and Mexico

**Mushroom stones** are carved stone objects, predominantly anthropomorphic (human figure emerging from or surmounted by a mushroom cap), produced across Guatemala and southern Mexico from approximately **1000 BCE to 900 CE**. Over 200 have been identified, mostly from highland Mayan sites. Many are clearly phallic; many combine the mushroom form with human or divine figures.

Their function is not definitively established, but their distribution — concentrated in regions where *Psilocybe* species grow, and in association with other ritual objects — strongly suggests they were connected to religious or ceremonial mushroom use.

### Teonanácatl in Aztec Culture

**Teonanácatl** — often translated as "flesh of the gods" or "divine flesh" in Nahuatl — was the term used by Spanish colonizers (often pejoratively) to describe ritual mushroom use among the Aztec and neighboring peoples. The Aztec *tonalpohualli* calendar and religious system incorporated multiple psychoactive plants and fungi as gateways to divine communication.

Spanish missionaries documented and attempted to suppress teonanácatl ceremonies, comparing them to Christian communion (which disturbed them further). Friar Bernardino de Sahagún's *Historia General de las Cosas de Nueva España* (*Florentine Codex*, c. 1540–1585) contains some of the earliest written descriptions of ritual mushroom use, including descriptions of effects and social context.

The **Aztec codices** — illustrated manuscripts surviving from before and immediately after the conquest — contain depictions of deities associated with mushrooms, particularly Xochipilli, the "Prince of Flowers," associated with ecstatic states and psychoactive plants.

## Medieval and Renaissance Europe

### Illuminated Manuscripts and Botanical Illustration

Fungi appear in European manuscripts and herbals from the medieval period onward, primarily in botanical and medical contexts. The **Dioscorides** tradition — transmitted through Byzantine and Islamic scholarship and then into Latin Europe — includes fungal descriptions, mostly focused on edibility and toxicity.

By the Renaissance, scientific illustration of fungi became increasingly precise. **Pietro Andrea Mattioli's** *Commentarii* (1554) contains early detailed fungal illustrations. The tradition culminated in the great mycological atlases of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries — most importantly **Elias Magnus Fries's** *Systema Mycologicum* (1821–1832), which established the first systematic classification of fungi.

### The Plaincourault Fresco (1291)

The **Plaincourault Chapel** near Mérigny in France contains a Romanesque fresco depicting Adam and Eve at the Tree of Knowledge. The tree in the fresco has a distinctive form — a branching structure topped with rounded forms that some observers (most prominently John Marco Allegro, and later Wasson) interpreted as *Amanita muscaria* rather than a conventional tree.

This interpretation is disputed by art historians, who argue the image follows a conventional Byzantine stylized tree form (*arbor vitae* motif) seen across medieval European art. The "mushroom tree" reading is not accepted by mainstream art history, though it continues to circulate in popular mycological and psychedelic literature.

## Religious and Spiritual Claims

This section requires particular care: several popular claims about mushrooms and religion are speculative or rejected by scholars. They are documented here because they are culturally influential, with their scholarly status clearly noted.

### Soma and Amanita muscaria — Wasson's Hypothesis (1968)

In *Soma: Divine Mushroom of Immortality* (1968), R. Gordon Wasson proposed that the Vedic ritual drink **Soma** — whose identity has been unknown for millennia — was prepared from [[Amanita muscaria]].

**Scholarly status:** The hypothesis was influential but has not achieved acceptance among Vedic scholars or ethnobotanists. *A. muscaria* does not grow in many of the regions where the Rig Vedic hymns are set; the botanical and linguistic evidence does not clearly support the identification. Alternative candidates proposed include *Ephedra*, *Peganum harmala*, *Sarcostemma*, and *Psilocybe* species. Wasson's soma hypothesis is best understood as a brilliant and generative speculation that opened a field, not as established history.

### The Sacred Mushroom and the Cross — John Marco Allegro (1970)

Philologist John Marco Allegro published *The Sacred Mushroom and the Cross* (1970), arguing that early Christianity was fundamentally a mystery religion based on *Amanita muscaria* use, and that Jesus was a mythologized representation of the mushroom itself.

**Scholarly status:** The book was almost universally rejected by biblical scholars, linguists, and Allegro's own Dead Sea Scrolls colleagues. Several signatories to a public letter of rejection included prominent scholars who had previously supported Allegro's work. The book damaged his academic reputation irreparably. It remains in print and influential in popular counterculture, but its core claims are not accepted by scholarship.

### The Stoned Ape Hypothesis — Terence McKenna

Ethnobotanist and counterculture figure **Terence McKenna** (1946–2000) popularized the "Stoned Ape Hypothesis" in *Food of the Gods* (1992): that the consumption of psilocybin mushrooms by early *Homo sapiens* played a role in the rapid development of human consciousness, language, and symbolic thought.

**Scholarly status:** The hypothesis is not accepted by evolutionary biologists or archaeologists. No evidence directly links psilocybin mushroom consumption to human cognitive evolution. McKenna's proposed mechanism (that psilocybin enhanced visual acuity and thus hunting success at low doses, and promoted social bonding and creativity at higher doses) is speculative and not supported by the fossil or archaeological record.

The hypothesis is documented here because it has been enormously culturally influential — particularly in psychedelic communities — and because its popularization by Stamets and others has continued after McKenna's death. Its influence should be understood separately from its scientific merit.

## María Sabina: Sacred Knowledge, Exploitation, and Regret

**María Sabina** (c. 1894–1985) was a Mazatec *curandera* (healer) from Huautla de Jiménez, Oaxaca, Mexico, who became arguably the most important figure in the global history of psychedelic mushrooms — largely against her own wishes.

Her tradition held the *velada* — an all-night healing ceremony in which she consumed sacred *Psilocybe* mushrooms, entered communion with spiritual forces, and offered healing and guidance to those who came to her. The ceremony was embedded in a specific Mazatec Catholic-syncretic religious framework; the mushrooms were understood as saints or divine intermediaries, not as drugs.

**The Wasson encounter (1955):** Wasson and photographer Allan Richardson attended a velada with Sabina's permission in 1955. The publication of the 1957 Life article named Sabina — by her village and general identity if not her full name initially — and the subsequent waves of Western seekers changed her life catastrophically. The sacred knowledge was, in her words, "profaned."

**Sabina's later statements:**
- "From the moment the foreigners arrived, the saint children lost their purity."
- "Before Wasson, I felt the mushrooms were mine alone... [he] ruined my life."
- She expressed ambivalence rather than simple regret — she had genuinely sought to heal; some visitors were sincere; but the scale of intrusion was beyond anything she had consented to

**Her legacy:** Despite this, she became a global counterculture icon. Poets and musicians composed tributes. She was visited by Allen Ginsberg, Bob Dylan's road manager, John Lennon (reportedly), and thousands of seekers. Her *chants* (*cantos*) were recorded and published — documents of extraordinary beauty that are also ethically contested. She died in poverty.

Sabina's story is central to contemporary discussions of **indigenous data sovereignty**, informed consent in ethnographic research, and the ethics of sharing sacred knowledge with outside audiences.

## Fungi in Literature

### Beatrix Potter (1866–1943)

Before she became famous for *The Tale of Peter Rabbit*, Beatrix Potter was a serious amateur mycologist. She produced hundreds of detailed watercolor illustrations of fungi and developed a theory about the germination of fungal spores that was ahead of its time. When she attempted to present her paper "On the Germination of the Spores of Agaricineae" to the Linnean Society of London in 1897, she was not permitted to attend (as a woman) and her work was dismissed. Her theory was later vindicated.

Potter's mycological illustrations — held by the Armitt Museum in Ambleside — are recognized as scientifically significant as well as aesthetically remarkable.

### Lewis Carroll: Alice in Wonderland (1865)

In *Alice's Adventures in Wonderland*, the Caterpillar instructs Alice to eat from a mushroom to change her size. Carroll's mushroom-as-transformation agent has been read by some (particularly in the counterculture 1960s) as an oblique reference to psychedelic experience. Most Carroll scholars consider this anachronistic projection. The mushroom serves Carroll's narrative as a convenient magical object, probably drawing on Victorian fairy tale conventions.

Regardless of intent, the image lodged itself in Western cultural consciousness and became one of the most enduring visual associations between mushrooms and altered perception.

### J.R.R. Tolkien: Mushroom-Loving Hobbits

In Tolkien's fiction, **hobbits** are specifically and enthusiastically fungus-loving. In *The Fellowship of the Ring*, Frodo, Sam, Merry, and Pippin's detour through Farmer Maggot's fields to steal mushrooms is an early plot point and an index of hobbit character — earthy, domestic, deeply attached to the good things of the Shire.

Tolkien scholars generally interpret the hobbits' mycophilia as part of the broader English rural nostalgia that animates the Shire — an idealized pre-industrial English countryside. Given Wasson's thesis about English mycophobia, the hobbits' extraordinary love of mushrooms can be read as Tolkien's deliberate inversion — the ideal English rural type would be a mushroom-lover.

## The Modern Psychedelic Renaissance in Culture

From roughly 2010 onward, a new wave of cultural production has engaged with fungi:

- **Michael Pollan:** *How to Change Your Mind* (2018) brought the clinical psilocybin research (see [[Psilocybin Research]]) to a general audience of millions. Pollan's mainstream credibility legitimized discussion of psychedelics in contexts where it had been impossible since the 1970s.
- **Paul Stamets:** Mycologist, author (*Mycelium Running*, 2005), and entrepreneur; his advocacy for fungi as ecological, medical, and spiritual resources has reached enormous popular audiences. His claim of a personal transformational experience with psilocybin (detailed in *Fantastic Fungi*, 2019) positioned him as a bridge between science and spirituality.
- ***Fantastic Fungi* (2019):** Documentary directed by Louie Schwartzberg, featuring Stamets and others; became one of the most widely watched mycology documentaries globally.
- **Art and visual culture:** Fungi have become significant subjects in contemporary fine art, illustration, tattoo culture, and graphic design — often combining scientific accuracy with spiritual or ecological themes.
