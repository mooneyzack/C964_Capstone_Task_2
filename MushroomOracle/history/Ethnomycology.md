## Overview

Ethnomycology is the study of the relationships between human cultures and fungi — encompassing food, medicine, religion, art, and folklore. The field examines how different societies have understood, used, revered, and feared fungi across millennia.

Cross-links: [[Traditional Medicine]], [[Mycophobia and Mycophilia]], [[Mushrooms in Art and Religion]]

## Origins of the Field

### R. Gordon Wasson

R. Gordon Wasson (1898–1986) was a J.P. Morgan vice president and amateur mycologist who effectively founded ethnomycology as a formal discipline. Together with his wife Valentina Pavlovna Wasson — a Russian-born pediatrician — he developed a systematic cross-cultural framework for examining human relationships with fungi.

Their foundational observation: some cultures are deeply fungus-loving (mycophilic) and others profoundly fungus-fearing (mycophobic). This framework, first articulated in their 1957 book *Mushrooms, Russia and History*, remains influential despite its oversimplifications (see [[Mycophobia and Mycophilia]]).

**The 1957 Life magazine article** ("Seeking the Magic Mushroom," May 13, 1957) brought Wasson's 1955 participation in a Mazatec *velada* ceremony to a mainstream American audience for the first time. The article described his experience under the guidance of the curandera María Sabina in Huautla de Jiménez, Oaxaca. It triggered a wave of Western curiosity that would prove deeply disruptive to the Mazatec community.

Wasson coined the term **"ethnomycology"** and went on to write *Soma: Divine Mushroom of Immortality* (1968), in which he argued that the Vedic ritual drink soma was prepared from [[Amanita muscaria]] — a hypothesis that remains contested.

## Regional Traditions

### Mesoamerica: The Mazatec Tradition

The Mazatec people of Oaxaca, Mexico, have one of the world's most extensively documented sacred mushroom traditions. The **teonanácatl** ("flesh of the gods") — a Nahuatl term applied by Spanish colonizers in a derogatory sense — encompassed several *Psilocybe* species including [[Psilocybe mexicana]], [[Psilocybe cubensis]], and *Psilocybe caerulescens*.

**The velada** is the all-night healing ceremony in which a *curandera* or *curandero* (healer) consumes sacred mushrooms and enters dialogue with the spirit world on behalf of a patient. The mushrooms are considered sacred intermediaries, not mere drugs.

**María Sabina** (c. 1894–1985) was the most celebrated Mazatec curandera of the twentieth century. She allowed Wasson and his photographer to attend and document a velada in 1955 — a decision she later deeply regretted. The publication of the Life article brought thousands of Western seekers to Huautla de Jiménez, disrupting the community, commodifying a sacred practice, and exposing Sabina to local backlash. Her home was burned down. She described the intrusion as a desecration: "From the moment the foreigners arrived, the saint children lost their purity. They lost their force; the strangers spoiled them."

The Mazatec tradition illustrates a recurring tension in ethnomycology: the collision between Western scientific curiosity and indigenous sovereignty over sacred knowledge.

### Siberia: Amanita muscaria Among Tungusic and Paleo-Siberian Peoples

[[Amanita muscaria]] (fly agaric) has a well-documented history of ritual use among several Siberian peoples, including the **Koryak**, **Yakut (Sakha)**, **Evenki**, and **Chukchi**. Use appears to have been concentrated in regions where other psychoactive plants were unavailable due to climate.

**Key documented practices:**
- Shamans (*kams*) consumed mushrooms to enter trance states for divination, healing, and spirit communication
- A remarkable urine-recycling practice was documented by multiple eighteenth- and nineteenth-century European explorers: the active compound **muscimol** is excreted largely unchanged in urine, and participants would consume the urine of someone who had ingested the mushroom — extending the intoxicating effect to more people from a limited supply. This unusual pharmacology distinguishes *A. muscaria* from most other psychoactive substances
- Reindeer show a striking attraction to *A. muscaria* and will seek out both the mushroom itself and urine of people who have consumed it. The relationship between Siberian reindeer-herding cultures and fly agaric has been proposed (by some scholars) as a possible origin of certain Christmas mythology — the flying reindeer, the red-and-white-clad shaman entering through the smoke hole — though this remains speculative

**Note on toxicity:** *A. muscaria* contains ibotenic acid (which converts to muscimol) and muscarine. Preparatory techniques including drying and boiling reduce toxicity. Raw consumption is unpredictable and can cause dangerous symptoms. See [[Amanita muscaria]] for full safety profile.

### China: The Oldest Mycological Tradition

China has the world's longest documented mycological tradition, anchored in both culinary and medicinal practice.

**Shen Nong Ben Cao Jing** (*Divine Farmer's Classic of Materia Medica*), compiled roughly 100–200 CE but attributed to the mythological Emperor Shen Nong, is among the oldest surviving herbal texts. It describes **lingzhi** ([[Ganoderma lucidum]], reishi) as the supreme medicinal substance — the "mushroom of immortality." Lingzhi occupies a central place in Taoist spiritual practice, symbolizing longevity, divine blessing, and cosmic harmony. Its image appears in classical Chinese art, embroidery, and architecture as a symbol of auspiciousness.

**Shiitake cultivation** (*Lentinula edodes*) is attributed to **Wu Sang Kwuang**, a Chinese farmer who developed a technique of scoring logs and inoculating them with spore-laden wood during the Song Dynasty (c. 1209 CE). The technique — still recognizable in modern log cultivation — spread to Japan and became the basis for global shiitake production.

**Cordyceps** (*Ophiocordyceps sinensis*, dong chong xia cao, "winter worm, summer grass") has been documented in Chinese medicine since at least 620 CE (*Ben Cao Shi Yi*). Harvested at extreme altitude in Tibet and Qinghai, it commands extraordinary prices and has historically been a trade good of significant economic and diplomatic weight. See [[Traditional Medicine]] for medicinal detail.

### Japan: Shiitake, Matsutake, and Mountain Monks

Japan built on Chinese mycological knowledge while developing distinctive traditions of its own.

**Shiitake** (*Lentinula edodes*) was adopted from China and became central to Japanese cuisine. Dried shiitake (hoshi-shiitake) became a key component of *dashi* broth and is documented in Japanese culinary records from the Edo period onward.

**Matsutake** (*Tricholoma matsutake*) occupies an almost unparalleled cultural status in Japan — a seasonal luxury comparable to European truffles, given as gifts, celebrated in poetry, and mourned as its habitat (mature red pine forests) has declined dramatically since the mid-twentieth century due to pine forest changes. Matsutake gifting is a practiced social ritual tied to autumn and gratitude.

**Yamabushitake** (*Hericium erinaceus*, lion's mane) takes its Japanese name from the **yamabushi** — mountain ascetic monks of the Shugendo tradition who trained in remote forested mountains. The mushroom's cascading white tendrils were said to resemble the straw vestments worn by these monks. See [[Hericium erinaceus]].

### Europe: A Divided Continent

European mycological attitudes are deeply regional, reflecting ecology, history, and cultural inheritance. See [[Mycophobia and Mycophilia]] for the full framework.

**Mycophilic regions** — Russia, Poland, Czech Republic, Slovakia, Romania, Italy, France, Spain, Catalonia, Scandinavia — have deep foraging traditions. In Russia, mushroom foraging (*gribniki*) is a near-universal autumn activity woven into national identity; the poet Pushkin wrote about it, and Soviet-era public health posters promoted safe identification. In Italy, *porcini* (*Boletus edulis*) and *funghi* are embedded in regional cuisines.

**Mycophobic regions** — England, Ireland, and much of the Anglo-Saxon cultural sphere — historically viewed wild mushrooms with deep suspicion. "Toadstool" became a pejorative applied to any non-cultivated mushroom. The Wasson hypothesis attributes this partly to Celtic pre-Christian religion being suppressed by Christianity, which associated mushrooms with witchcraft and the devil.

### Australian Indigenous Traditions

Indigenous Australian knowledge of fungi is less extensively documented in the Western ethnomycological literature than traditions from other regions — a gap that reflects the limitations of colonial-era documentation rather than an absence of knowledge. Some Aboriginal groups in southeastern Australia and Queensland recognized edible species including *Laccocephalum mylittae* (blackfellow's bread, a large underground sclerotium), which served as a food source.

The relative scarcity of documentation underscores the importance of community-controlled research and indigenous data sovereignty in contemporary ethnomycology.

### Africa: Termite Mushrooms and Regional Traditions

Sub-Saharan African culinary traditions feature **Termitomyces** species — mushrooms that grow in symbiosis with termite colonies — as highly prized foods. *Termitomyces titanicus*, found across central and West Africa, is one of the world's largest mushrooms and a significant seasonal food source. In many communities, Termitomyces harvesting rights are socially regulated and economically important.

Psychoactive mushroom traditions are limited in the documented African ethnomycological record, though this may again reflect documentation gaps rather than their absence.

## Ethical Dimensions

Ethnomycology raises persistent ethical questions:

- **Intellectual property and bioprospecting:** Who benefits when indigenous knowledge leads to commercial products?
- **Sacred knowledge and tourism:** The Mazatec case is a cautionary example of how documentation can disrupt communities
- **Community-controlled research:** Contemporary ethnomycologists increasingly work with, rather than on, indigenous communities, seeking free, prior, and informed consent
