# Koji Fermentation

**Koji** refers to the culture of *Aspergillus oryzae* grown on cooked grain or legume substrates — and, by extension, to the inoculated substrate itself. It is the enzymatic engine behind soy sauce, miso, sake, mirin, amazake, and rice vinegar, and has been adopted by Western chefs for applications ranging from aged meats to fermented butter. In 2006, the Brewing Society of Japan designated *A. oryzae* the **national fungus** (*kokkin*) of Japan.

*A. oryzae* evolved from *Aspergillus flavus* through human selection of atoxigenic (non-aflatoxin-producing) lineages over thousands of years, gradually becoming a domesticated "cell factory" for enzymes and metabolites. Its genome (37 Mb, 12,074 predicted protein-coding genes, sequenced via whole-genome shotgun of strain RIB40) encodes an extraordinary secretory capacity: 65 endopeptidases, 69 exopeptidases, plus amylases, lipases, and glutaminase. It holds FDA GRAS status and WHO endorsement.

## The Enzyme Arsenal

| Enzyme Class | Key Enzymes | Function | Activity Measured |
|-------------|-------------|----------|-------------------|
| Amylases | α-amylase, glucoamylase | Starch → glucose (saccharification) | Up to 200 U/g dry weight in soybean koji |
| Proteases | Neutral protease, alkaline protease, metallopeptidase, leucine aminopeptidase | Protein → peptides → free amino acids | Up to 84.38 U/g dry weight |
| Lipases | Triacylglycerol lipase | Fat hydrolysis; first commercial lipase (1988, laundry detergent) | Variable by strain |
| Glutaminase | Cell wall-bound glutaminase | L-glutamine → L-glutamate (umami) | Strain-dependent; correlates directly with soy sauce umami intensity |

## The Umami Mechanism

The pathway that produces umami in koji-fermented foods operates through coordinated enzyme activity:

1. **Proteolysis**: Koji proteases break proteins into peptides and free amino acids
2. **Glutamate liberation**: Leucine aminopeptidases and acid carboxypeptidases specifically release glutamic acid from peptide chains
3. **Glutaminase conversion**: Cell wall-bound glutaminase converts L-glutamine to **L-glutamate** — the primary umami compound

This glutaminase step is critical: without it, L-glutamine non-enzymatically converts to flavorless pyroglutamate, losing umami potential. The enzyme "locks in" umami by producing the more stable L-glutamate before peptides fully break down. L-glutamate concentration in soy sauce correlates directly with the glutaminase activity of the *A. oryzae* strain used.

See also [[Umami Science]] for the receptor-level mechanism and synergistic effects.

## Traditional Applications

- **Soy sauce** (*shoyu*): Koji grown on roasted wheat and steamed soybeans; the koji mold's enzymes drive months-long moromi (mash) fermentation with *Zygosaccharomyces rouxii* yeast and *Tetragenococcus halophilus* bacteria
- **Miso**: Koji (rice, barley, or soybean-based) mixed with cooked soybeans, salt, and starter; fermented weeks to years
- **Sake**: Rice koji provides amylase for parallel saccharification and fermentation — starch-to-glucose conversion occurs simultaneously with yeast alcohol production
- **Mirin**: Sweet rice wine made from glutinous rice, rice koji, and shochu
- **Amazake**: Low/no-alcohol sweet rice drink produced by rice koji's amylase activity alone

## Western Culinary Applications

The "koji revolution" in Western gastronomy has expanded applications dramatically:

**Koji-aged meats**: Powdered dried koji is rubbed onto steaks and left uncovered in the refrigerator for ~48 hours. The proteases tenderize meat and generate amino acids that impart umami and the characteristic "funk" of dry aging — in 2 days instead of weeks. The technique is documented by Umansky and Shih in *Koji Alchemy*.

**Koji charcuterie**: Koji grown directly on meats or applied as *shio koji* (salt koji paste) accelerates curing of bresaola, coppa, and other charcuterie, completing in a fraction of the usual time.

**Koji butter, koji vegetables, koji dairy**: Amino pastes, koji-treated cheeses, and fermented vegetables are all documented applications.
