## Overview

The method by which bioactive compounds are extracted from mushrooms fundamentally determines what is present in the final product, at what concentration, and in what bioavailable form. There is no single "best" extraction method — the correct approach depends on which compounds are the target. Understanding extraction is essential for evaluating supplement quality.

See also [[Safety and Contraindications]] for quality control issues in the supplement industry.

## Hot Water Extraction

### What It Extracts
- [[Beta-Glucans]] and other polysaccharides (primary target)
- [[Ergothioneine]] (water-soluble)
- Most B vitamins
- Amino acids and peptides
- Some phenolic acids (caffeic, protocatechuic)
- PSK and PSP (Turkey Tail — *[[Trametes versicolor]]*)

### Why It's Essential

Raw mushroom cell walls contain **chitin**, which encases bioactive compounds and renders them largely inaccessible. Hot water (typically 80–100°C for 30–120 minutes) both ruptures cell walls and dissolves water-soluble compounds. Without this step, the majority of beta-glucans remain locked in the cell wall matrix.

Studies confirm that chitin-rich fractions show only 38.4% solubilized protein and 41.2% degree of hydrolysis versus 84.0% and 61.1% for isolated protein fractions. **Raw mushroom powder without hot water extraction delivers substantially less bioavailable polysaccharides.**

### Process
Simmer/decoct mushroom material in water at near-boiling temperature; filter, concentrate, and spray-dry to powder. Standardized extracts specify beta-glucan content (commonly 20–40% beta-glucan by HPGPC assay).

### Limitation
Does not extract fat-soluble terpenoids (ganoderic acids, triterpenoids from reishi or chaga). Not suitable for extracting reishi's [[Terpenoids and Triterpenoids|ganoderic acids]].

### Best For
- Turkey tail (PSK/PSP)
- Shiitake (lentinan)
- Maitake (D-fraction)
- Cordyceps (cordycepin is water-soluble)
- Lion's mane fruiting body (hericenones)

## Alcohol/Ethanol Extraction

### What It Extracts
- [[Terpenoids and Triterpenoids]] (ganoderic acids, inotodiol, lanostane compounds)
- Ergosterol and derivatives
- Some phenolic compounds
- [[Cordycepin]]
- [[Hericenones and Erinacines|Hericenones]] (from lion's mane fruiting body)
- Non-water-soluble flavonoids

### Process
Macerate mushroom material in 50–95% ethanol for days to weeks, or use accelerated solvent extraction. Filter and concentrate.

### Limitation
Ethanol can cause long-chain beta-glucan polysaccharides to **precipitate out of solution**. If a manufacturer wants to guarantee a specific beta-glucan content, hot water extraction alone is preferable. Dual extraction, while capturing both compound classes, may reduce beta-glucan chain length.

## Dual Extraction: Rationale and Process

### Why It's Used
[[Ganoderma lucidum]] (reishi), [[Inonotus obliquus]] (chaga), and other mushrooms with significant triterpenoid content alongside polysaccharides require both extraction types to capture the full bioactive profile. Neither hot water nor alcohol alone extracts everything clinically relevant.

### Standard Dual Extraction Process
1. Alcohol maceration (typically 70–95% ethanol, 2–8 weeks, or accelerated method)
2. Filter and retain alcohol extract
3. Hot water extraction of the remaining marc (material) at 80–100°C
4. Combine both extractions; concentrate; optionally spray-dry

### Trade-off
As noted, the alcohol step may cause some beta-glucan precipitation. Some manufacturers address this by adjusting the order (hot water first, then alcohol), or by using separate single-extraction products and combining. **Transparency about this trade-off is a marker of quality** in supplement companies.

### Applicability by Species

| Species | Recommended Extraction | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| [[Ganoderma lucidum]] (Reishi) | Dual extraction — strongly recommended | Ganoderic acids require alcohol; beta-glucans require hot water |
| [[Inonotus obliquus]] (Chaga) | Dual extraction — beneficial | Triterpenoids and phenolics need alcohol; polysaccharides need hot water |
| [[Hericium erinaceus]] (Lion's Mane) | Hot water usually sufficient for fruiting body | Hericenones water-soluble; erinacines from mycelium are diterpenoids needing alcohol |
| [[Trametes versicolor]] (Turkey Tail) | Hot water usually sufficient | PSK/PSP are water-soluble |
| [[Cordyceps militaris]] | Hot water sufficient for most | Cordycepin is reasonably water-soluble |

## Whole Fruiting Body vs. Mycelium-on-Grain (MoG)

This is one of the most contentious debates in the functional mushroom supplement industry.

### Fruiting Body
- The true mushroom structure formed after vegetative growth
- Contains the highest concentrations of [[Beta-Glucans]], [[Ergothioneine]], hericenones, phenolic compounds
- No grain substrate in the final product

### Mycelium-on-Grain (MoG)
- Mycelium grown on a grain substrate (typically oats, brown rice, or sorghum) and then dried and ground whole — **including the grain**
- Cheaper and faster to produce than cultivating fruiting bodies
- The final product contains **substantial starch from the grain**
- When manufacturers report "total polysaccharides" for MoG products, much of that number represents **starch (alpha-glucans from grain), not beta-glucans** — starch is not immunologically active in the same way
- Real Mushrooms and other companies have published third-party testing showing some commercial MoG products contain **<1% beta-glucans** vs. 20–40% in fruiting body extracts
- However: mycelium does produce compounds the fruiting body does not — notably **erinacines** (in lion's mane), certain terpenoids, and some fermentation products

### What the Science Says

- Most clinical trials demonstrating health benefits used **fruiting body extracts** or isolated compounds from fruiting bodies
- For [[Hericenones and Erinacines|erinacines]] specifically (lion's mane neuroprotection), **mycelium is the correct source** — fruiting body does not contain them
- For beta-glucan-dependent immune activity, fruiting body extracts are superior
- A quality MoG product could be standardized for actual erinacine or beta-glucan content regardless of matrix — the problem is that most MoG products are not tested or standardized
- The debate is partially artificial: **what matters most is testing and standardization, not categorical source**

### Consumer Guidance
Look for products that:
1. Specify fruiting body or mycelium (not just "mushroom")
2. List actual beta-glucan or terpenoid content by validated assay (not just "polysaccharides")
3. Have certificates of analysis from third-party labs

## Bioavailability Considerations

| Factor | Effect on Bioavailability |
|---|---|
| Raw vs. cooked/extracted | Chitin limits bioavailability of protein, B vitamins, and polysaccharides in raw form; cooking or extraction is essential |
| Beta-glucan molecular weight | Higher MW (HMW) forms are less absorbed but may have more potent gut-level immune effects; LMW fragments may be better absorbed systemically |
| Hot water vs. dual extraction | Hot water extracts ~10× more bioavailable beta-glucans than unprocessed powder (estimated) |
| Stomach acid stability | Beta-glucans and polysaccharides are largely stable through gastric digestion; absorption occurs in small intestine and colon |
| Fat co-administration | Triterpenoids (ganoderic acids) benefit from co-administration with dietary fat for absorption |
