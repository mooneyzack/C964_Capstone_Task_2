## Overview

Triterpenoids are the second major bioactive compound class in medicinal mushrooms, synthesized via the **mevalonate (MVA) pathway** from squalene. They are **lipophilic (fat-soluble)** and thus not extractable by hot water — requiring **alcohol extraction** or dual extraction. This distinguishes them fundamentally from the water-soluble [[Beta-Glucans]].

## Ganoderic Acids (Reishi — *Ganoderma lucidum*)

Ganoderic acids are a family of lanostane-type triterpenoids unique to *[[Ganoderma lucidum]]* (reishi). Over **300 individual ganoderic acids** have been identified (designated by letters A, B, C, DM, T, X, etc. and various subtypes).

**Key biological activities:**

- **Direct cytotoxicity:** Ganoderic acids A, B, C, DM, and T have demonstrated inhibitory activity against multiple cancer cell lines (hepatocellular, lung, colon, breast) via induction of apoptosis through caspase activation and mitochondrial pathway engagement
- **Anti-angiogenic effects:** Suppression of VEGF production and metalloproteinase activity
- **Anti-metastatic:** Inhibition of matrix metalloproteinases (MMPs) that facilitate tumor invasion
- **Immune potentiation in hematological malignancies:** Macrophage-like differentiation induction, MAP-K pathway activation, IL-3-dependent cytotoxic action, and induction of cytoprotective autophagy
- **Anti-inflammatory:** Inhibition of NF-κB and MAPK signaling in macrophages
- **Antiviral:** Some ganoderic acids demonstrate inhibitory activity against influenza and herpes viruses
- **Adaptogenic/stress effects:** Modulate cortisol metabolism via inhibition of 11β-hydroxysteroid dehydrogenase
- **HMG-CoA reductase inhibition:** Inhibit the same enzyme targeted by statin drugs (anti-cholesterol mechanism)
- **Antihistamine:** Inhibit histamine release (antiallergic activity)
- **ACE inhibition:** Inhibit angiotensin-converting enzyme

**Chemical characteristics:** C30 highly oxidized lanostane-type structures; molecular weights 400–600 Da; responsible for reishi's characteristic bitter taste. In commercial analysis, triterpene content in reishi products ranges from **undetectable to 7.8%** — standardization is essential for medicinal use.

## Inonotusols (Chaga — *Inonotus obliquus*)

Lanostane triterpenes found in [[Inonotus obliquus]] (chaga):
- Inhibit nitric oxide production by blocking LPS-induced iNOS expression in BV2 microglial cells
- Demonstrate specific anti-inflammatory mechanism via NF-κB luciferase activation inhibition

## Betulinic Acid (Chaga / Birch Host)

An important caveat: betulinic acid found in chaga is actually **sourced from the birch tree (*Betula*) host** rather than the fungus itself. The chaga sclerotium accumulates this compound from the host, blurring the line between mushroom and host-tree medicine.

**Established bioactivities of betulinic acid:**
- Cytotoxic activity against melanoma and neuroectodermal tumors
- Inhibits HIV-1 replication
- Targets mitochondrial apoptotic pathway
- Established mechanism distinct from amatoxin-related cytotoxicity

## Inotodiol and Ergosterol Peroxide (Chaga)

- **Inotodiol:** p53-dependent apoptosis in HeLa cervical cancer cells
- **Ergosterol peroxide:** Induces apoptosis in HCT116 and HT-29 colorectal cancer cells via caspase activation; also active in lung cancer cell line A549

## Polysaccharides Beyond Beta-Glucans

Mushrooms contain additional polysaccharide classes that bridge the polysaccharide and protein compound categories:

- **Heteropolysaccharides / Proteoglycans:** Polysaccharide-protein complexes including PSK and PSP from turkey tail — protein-bound polysaccharides rather than pure glucans (see [[Beta-Glucans]])
- **Grifolans:** Maitake-specific polysaccharides (at least 47 bioactive polysaccharides identified in *[[Grifola frondosa]]*)
- **Glucuronoxylomannan:** Contributing to antiviral properties in some species
- **Galactomannan, fucogalactan:** Found in various species, contributing to prebiotic and anti-inflammatory effects

## Phenolic Compounds and Antioxidant Capacity

Mushrooms contain a diverse array of phenolic compounds including:
- Caffeic acid, ferulic acid, gallic acid, protocatechuic acid, chlorogenic acid, cinnamic acid
- Flavonoids: quercetin, kaempferol, naringenin (in smaller amounts than in plants)
- **Hispidin and related styrylpyrones** (predominantly in chaga/Inonotus) — primary contributor to chaga's exceptional antioxidant score
- Ergosterol peroxide and related sterol oxidation products

**Antioxidant rankings:**
- Chaga (*[[Inonotus obliquus]]*) has one of the highest ORAC (Oxygen Radical Absorbance Capacity) scores measured in any food — approximately 50× the antioxidant power of blueberries on a dry weight basis. This is primarily due to melanin-like pigments and hispidin derivatives in the sclerotium
- Wild porcini, chanterelles, and maitake also score notably high in phenolic content versus common commercial species
- DPPH, ABTS, and FRAP assays consistently show medicinal mushroom extracts outperforming common vegetables

**Mechanisms:** Phenolic hydroxyl groups donate hydrogen atoms to neutralize reactive oxygen species (ROS); some phenolics also chelate metal ions (preventing Fenton-type oxidation), and others induce endogenous antioxidant enzyme systems (superoxide dismutase, catalase, glutathione peroxidase) via Nrf2 pathway activation.

## Ergosterol and Derivatives

Beyond its role as the vitamin D2 precursor (see [[Nutritional Profiles]]):
- Is the primary sterol in fungal membranes (analogous to cholesterol in animal cells)
- Is the target of antifungal drugs (azoles, polyenes)
- Has been found in in vitro studies to inhibit HMG-CoA reductase activity
- **Ergosterol peroxide (EP):** its oxidized form, has demonstrated direct anti-tumor effects via p53-dependent and independent apoptotic pathways in colorectal (HCT116, HT-29), lung (A549), and other cancer cell lines

## Extraction Requirements

Triterpenoids are fat-soluble and **require alcohol extraction** (typically 50–95% ethanol). Hot water alone cannot extract ganoderic acids from reishi or inonotusols from chaga. This is why **dual extraction** (both hot water and alcohol) is required for species where both polysaccharides and terpenoids contribute to the medicinal profile.

See [[Extraction Methods]] for full dual extraction methodology and species-specific guidance.
