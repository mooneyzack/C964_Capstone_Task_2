## Domain 1: Mycotechnology

### Overview

Mycotechnology spans mycelium-based materials (packaging, leather, composites), construction (insulation, bricks, acoustic panels), biocomputing (electrical signaling, logic gates, memristors), and environmental applications (mycoremediation, mycofiltration, plastic degradation). The global mycelium market reached $3.6B in 2025, with construction materials alone at $910M and projected to reach $2.55B by 2033.

### Key Findings

**Materials science**: Mycelium composites achieve tensile strengths of 6.3 MPa (conventional) to 12.5 MPa (ultra-high-pressure processing), thermal conductivity of 0.031–0.05 W/m·K (comparable to mineral wool), and fire ratings of B-s1-d0. The binding mechanism is a combination of physical hyphal penetration and enzymatic substrate modification.

**Commercial landscape**: Both leading mycelium leather companies faced severe headwinds — MycoWorks went insolvent October 2025 (IP acquired by Stage Fund), Bolt Threads suspended production in 2023 and restructured. The companies furthest along commercially are Ecovative (packaging, margin-positive at scale), Mogu (acoustic/flooring panels, €11M Series A), and food-sector companies (Nature's Fynd $500M+, Meati $278M).

**Biocomputing**: Andrew Adamatzky (UWE Bristol) has demonstrated that mycelium generates electrical spikes matching Zipf's Law distribution, can implement Boolean logic gates, and produces a "lexicon" of up to 50 distinct signal patterns. Graz University of Technology showed Ganoderma lucidum mycelium as biodegradable circuit substrate. Ohio State demonstrated mushroom memristors.

**Construction**: The MycoHAB project (Namibia, May 2024) built the first house from mycelium bricks. Self-healing living building materials using Neurospora crassa were published in Cell Reports Physical Science (April 2025).

## Domain 2: Psychedelic Science

### Overview

Psilocybin clinical research has reached Phase 3 trials, with COMPASS Pathways reporting the first-ever Phase 3 efficacy data for a classic psychedelic (June 2025). The neuroscience mechanism is increasingly well-characterized: 5-HT2A agonism, DMN disruption, BDNF/mTOR-mediated neuroplasticity via three distinct pathways. The legal landscape is shifting rapidly — Oregon and Colorado have regulated access programs, Australia rescheduled psilocybin as a medicine, and FDA approval could come as early as late 2026.

### Key Findings

**Clinical trials**: COMPASS COMP005 (Phase 3, 258 participants): -3.6 MADRS points vs. placebo (p < 0.001). COMP006 (Phase 3, 581 participants): -3.8 MADRS points (p < 0.001). Usona Phase 2: Hedges' g = 1.07 (large effect). NDA submission targeted Q4 2026.

**Neuroscience**: Three distinct neuroplasticity pathways identified — indirect (glutamatergic via AMPA), direct (classical 5-HT2A), and hyperdirect (TrkB/BDNF binding with 1,000x higher affinity than conventional antidepressants). Structural changes persist at least one month in animal models.

**Microdosing**: The self-blinding citizen science trial (Szigeti et al., 2021, eLife) found no significant between-group differences on any accumulative outcome. Cognitive performance showed zero differences. The evidence consistently indicates benefits are largely explained by placebo/expectancy effects.

**Legal**: Oregon has served ~16,000 clients with only 6 adverse reactions in Q1 2025, but 12 of 35 centers have closed due to costs. Colorado accepting licenses from December 2024. Australia first country to reschedule (July 2023). Canada SAP approved 301 requests but approval rates declining.

**Entourage effect**: Largely unsupported by controlled evidence. Baeocystin and aeruginascin are "devoid of in vivo psychedelic-like effects" individually.

## Domain 3: Fermentation & Food Science

### Overview

Fungi are central to both traditional and cutting-edge food science: koji (Aspergillus oryzae) drives East Asian fermentation and is revolutionizing Western gastronomy; Rhizopus oligosporus transforms tempeh nutrition; filamentous fungi naturally produce meat-like textures for the $500M+ mycoprotein industry; precision fermentation uses engineered fungi to produce animal-identical proteins; and umami science explains why mushrooms are among the most flavor-potent foods.

### Key Findings

**Koji**: A. oryzae's genome encodes 65 endopeptidases, 69 exopeptidases. Cell wall-bound glutaminase converts L-glutamine to L-glutamate, the primary umami compound. Western applications include koji-aged meats (2-day dry-age equivalent), koji charcuterie, and koji butter.

**Mycoprotein**: Quorn's continuous fermentation produces 300 kg/hour with 55x lower carbon than beef. Meati's Neurospora crassa achieves PDCAAS of 1.0 (matching animal protein). Nature's Fynd discovered in Yellowstone hot springs, uses open-tray growth. The meat-like texture is an intrinsic property of filamentous fungi, not engineered.

**Umami science**: Dried porcini contains ~1,340 mg free glutamate per 100g. Combining glutamate with 5'-nucleotides (GMP) produces up to 30x amplification via allosteric binding at T1R1/T1R3 receptor. Drying releases GMP that is essentially absent in fresh mushrooms.

**Precision fermentation**: Perfect Day produces molecularly identical whey protein from engineered Trichoderma reesei (FDA GRAS GRN 863). New Culture produces mozzarella from precision-fermented bovine casein.

**Tempeh**: R. oligosporus alone does not produce B12, but co-fermentation with P. freudenreichii yields 8.26 µg/100g — significant for plant-based nutrition.

## Domain 4: Citizen Science & DNA Barcoding

### Overview

DNA barcoding has revolutionized fungal taxonomy, revealing that morphological identification alone captures only 3–8% of true diversity. The ITS region is the universal fungal barcode, but supplementary markers (TEF1-α, RPB1/2) are essential for species-level resolution in many genera. Environmental DNA metabarcoding detects 2–10x more species than fruiting body surveys. Citizen science platforms (iNaturalist, Mushroom Observer, FunDiS, MycoMap) aggregate observations at scale, and sequencing has become accessible to amateurs ($3.50/reaction Sanger, ~$1,000 MinION entry cost).

### Key Findings

**ITS limitations**: 10–20% of fungal ITS sequences in GenBank carry incorrect species names. ITS fails for Fusarium, Aspergillus, Penicillium, Cladosporium, and many species complexes. TEF1-α is the most promising supplementary barcode.

**eDNA**: The "Man against machine" study (Krah et al., 2019) showed metabarcoding detects significantly more species than fruit body surveys. In deadwood fungi, 70% of threatened species were found exclusively by metabarcoding.

**Citizen science accuracy**: iNaturalist's CV model for fungi has significant limitations — a 2023 study found ~70% of species-level fungal identifications unique to iNaturalist were wrong. Specialized platforms (Mushroom Observer, MycoMap) provide higher-quality data.

**Sequencing accessibility**: NAMA's DNAMA generated 803 ITS barcodes (83.7% success rate) for 513 taxa at a single foray using Oxford Nanopore. Sanger sequencing costs $3.50/reaction commercially.

**UNITE database**: Version 10.0 contains 3.85M ITS sequences and 239K Species Hypotheses with DOIs — the gold standard for curated fungal sequences.
